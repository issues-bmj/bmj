Orgasmic Chemistry


The science beneath the sheets

Meiji Tran

Spring 2005

   
    Sex is a constant source of fascination, conversation, and investigation, yet ambiguities still abound in the realm of human sexuality. Science has collected mounds of physiological facts on orgasms but has been unable to solve one of its most controversial mysteries: what is the purpose of a female orgasm?

    The orgasm can be considered only one of three components that make up a sexual climax. According to Gemma O’Brien, Ph.D., a University of New England physiology and human biology lecturer, the sexual climax involves the natural responses of the reproductive tract, including ejaculation, viscerosomatic changes such as an increased heart rate, and various brain responses. “It’s in the brain that orgasm occurs—all of that psycho-physiology, the ‘happy’ experience,” says Dr. O’Brien.

    During the Victorian Era, doctors believed orgasms to be a natural cure for hysteria and prescribed masturbation. Hysteria was considered an incurable and chronic illness that afflicted approximately 75 percent of women. Hysteria, Greek for uterus, was diagnosed as the “malfunctioning of the womb.” Its symptoms included lassitude, irritability, forgetfulness, depression, headaches, insomnia, heart palpitations, insatiable arousal, and vaginal lubrication. However, since sexuality was suppressed in the Victorian Era, doctors could have misinterpreted a woman’s libido as hysteria.

    Darwinian theorists believe that every evolutionary adaptation fulfills a specific function, and one early explanation of the female orgasm was the “pole axe” theory. This idea holds that orgasms act to sedate women after sex, facilitating insemination by keeping her recumbent and allowing her time to retain sperm. However, this “would not account for a woman’s multi-orgasmic capacity,” says Ian Kerner, Ph.D., author of the New York Times best-seller She Comes First: the Thinking Man’s Guide to Pleasuring a Woman. Furthermore, nature does not guarantee that all women climax during intercourse, so the “pole axe” theory is considered by many to be a dubious argument. Theories about the adaptive value of the female orgasm are prolific, but what if there is no value in orgasms at all?

    Late Harvard evolutionist Stephen Jay Gould argued that the clitoris is simply the by-product of the fact that men and women are developmentally similar. In embryos, the penis and the clitoris start off as identical organs that develop differently under the influence of hormones. Gould insisted that the clitoris is comparable to the male nipple and serves no adaptive purpose at all; the design of the female genitals does not even allow for easy stimulation for her to orgasm through intercourse. Moreover, female orgasms are usually achieved through experience, which contributes to the idea that the clitoris is purely vestigial. Gould often emphasized that chance events and structural constraints play major roles in the evolutionary process. For him, the question of the female orgasm provided a classic instance of why scientists should not always assume that every trait contains an adaptive value. Nevertheless, various scientists have rejected Gould’s notion that female orgasms are a relic of nature’s negligence.

    Some scientists suggest that the orgasm evolved in order to inspire feelings of intimacy and trust between lovers because a hormone known as oxytocin, which creates sensations of ecstasy, is released in the brain at the point of orgasm. Laura Hill-Sakurai, M.D., assistant professor of the Department of Family and Community Medicine at the University of California, San Francisco, challenges Gould’s ideas, declaring that the clitoris is “not like the male nipple, which is just hanging there without causing trouble. The female orgasm is there for a reason. It builds intimacy and encourages communication.”
