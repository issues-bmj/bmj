continued from Cheap and Easy


   Skeptics argue that DCA research is years away from clinical trials because of a lack of funding. The chemical structure of DCA is too simple and inexpensive to produce so it is unattractive to pharmaceutical companies that only fund research that will produce patents or profits. Drug patents last for 20 years and grant pharmaceutical companies the exclusive rights to produce, market, and profit from a particular drug in that time span. Unfortunately, DCA has already been on the market for decades now as a treatment for lactic acidosis. Critics point out that the process of getting funding for this unpatented and unprofitable drug will be extensive.

    According to a February 1993 report by the Congressional Office of Technology Assessment, it costs a company $359 million on average to get one new medicine from the laboratory to the pharmacist’s shelf. Pharmaceutical companies know other chemotherapy drugs are more profitable, thus researchers, including Michelakis’s group, will face a difficult road ahead in gaining additional financial support. In a recent online letter to the public, Michelakis says that his team will need to raise $1.5 million for the initial research in humans to begin. Currently, Michelakis’s research is funded by the Canadian Institutes for Health Research, Alberta Heritage Foundation for Medical Research, and Canadian Foundation for Innovation, as well as by private donations. “The dogma right now about developing drugs is that they can’t be developed from research without funding from industry. Their sole purpose is profit,” remarks Michelakis.

    In the end, cancer patients are caught in the battle between pharmaceutical companies and drug researchers. The patients suffer most, since they are the ones waiting hopefully for potential “miracle cures” like DCA to be approved by the Food and Drug Administration. Michelakis and his team will need to conduct more research and gain more funding before DCA can be used as a cancer treatment. “Patients have to approach this with guarded optimism. Lots of examples appear promising in animals but not in the case of humans,” says Michelakis. Ultimately, drug research should be intended to improve patient welfare because the advantages of an inexpensive treatment for cancer treatment drug are invaluable.
