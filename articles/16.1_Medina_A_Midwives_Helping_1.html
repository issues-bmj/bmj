Midwives Helping People Out


Certified Nurse-Midwives: An Alternative Choice

Amanda Medina

Fall 2008


    While having a baby can be simultaneously joyous and stressful, it is undoubtedly one of the greatest life-changing events. An obstetrician is the commonly preferred choice when choosing a practitioner to guide a neonate into this world. Yet, an increasing number of individuals are opting for an alternative choice: the certified nurse-midwife (CNM).

    Although most individuals understand the role of an obstetrician during the childbirth process, the role of the CNM is less well-known. There is a common misconception that the majority of CNM-attended births occur in the mother’s home or in birthing centers. However, in 2005, according to the National Center for Health Statistics, 96.6 percent of CNM-attended births actually occurred in hospitals. Furthermore, the National Center for Health Statistics reports that the use of CNMs is on the rise, as the number of CNM-attended births has increased nearly every year since 1975, when midwife-attended births were first recorded. In 1975, CNMs delivered approximately 0.6 percent of all births, but by 2005 this number had risen to 7.4 percent.

As certified birth attendants, both CNMs and obstetricians share a similar scope of practice that includes diagnosis, therapy management, risk reduction, family planning, and gynecological care. However, it is the differences in their approach and ideology that truly distinguish these two types of practitioners from each other and their ultimate effect on maternal and neonatal outcomes.

    In a study published in the September 1995 issue of Journal of Nurse-Midwifery, Deborah Oakley, PhD, University of Michigan at Ann Arbor, comments on these two divergent models of care. Oakley observes that the CNM approach emphasizes education, psychosocial care, and a restrained, individualized approach to technology, whereas the obstetrician approach emphasizes a more routine use of state-of-the-art technology.

    Leslie Cragin, CNM, PhD, University of California, San Francisco, further emphasizes the differences between the two practitioner groups: “CNMs believe birth is a normal health and life process. The best outcomes are achieved if interventions are performed only when medically necessary. CNMs often feel medical doctors are too eager to intervene.” Cragin believes that many medical physicians focus on “curing illness and do not allow for normal processes to occur.”

    Oakley’s research focuses on the maternal and neonatal outcomes of women planning a hospital birth attended by a CNM, in comparison to physician-supervised births, to determine if differences in care providers engender differences in medical processes and health outcomes. In these various studies, the two groups of women whom Oakley examined generally shared similar pregnancy and health risk factors, allowing for a more statistically reliable comparison.

    In the approach to physical prenatal care, some distinctions and similarities were identified between the two provider groups. Oakley observed that women in the physician group had significantly more ultrasounds than the women in the CNM group, 1.7 percent compared to 0.9 percent. Additionally, she found that 37 percent of the physician group, compared to 28 percent of the CNM group, had physiological tests performed such as X-rays and other laboratory assessments of conditions not specific to pregnancy. However, Oakley states that the number of women receiving pregnancy-related tests such as nonstress tests, contraction stress tests, and amniocentesis did not significantly differ between the two provider groups. Cragin believes that the increased number of tests within the physician cohort can be attributed to both practitioner ideology and a societal preference for use of medical technologies.
