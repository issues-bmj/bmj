continued from The War on Female Genital Mutiliation


    According to research by Sarah Rodriguez on the history of female circumcision in the Journal of the History of Medicine and Allied Sciences in December 2007, physicians in the United States practiced FGM for many years, performing clitoridectomys if a woman behaved outside of the female norms “by masturbating or by not responding to her husband’s affections.” Medical journals reveal that clitoridectomys were used to treat women’s “problems” including hysteria, epilepsy, masturbation, nymphomania, melancholia, and homosexuality as recently as the 1950s. In 1990, according to the Centers for Disease Control, an estimated 168,000 women and girls in the United States had undergone FGM.

    Today, the international community recognizes FGM as a violation of human rights. The WHO states that “the practice reflects deep-rooted inequality between the sexes, and constitutes an extreme form of discrimination against women,” also recognizing that FGM “has no health benefits and harms girls and women in many ways.”

    In the last decade, much more information regarding FGM has been uncovered. In June 2001, the Office of the Senior Coordinator for International Women’s Issues of the United States Department of State released some of the first thorough data and analysis of FGM practice, specifically in countries throughout Africa. In 2000, the US Agency for International Development (USAID) funded a series of Demographic and Health Surveys (DHS) conducted in Egypt. Of the 15,648 women, ages 15 to 49, who participated, nearly 97 percent had undergone some type of FGM. The DHS also found that about 75 percent of women thought the practice should continue.

    Currently, millions of women are living with FGM. A procedure is available to reconstruct a new clitoris, leading to restored sensation and sexual pleasure. This remarkable, but costly, surgery is currently being introduced at the Yalgado Ouedraogo University Hospital in Burkina Faso.

    FGM is a unique issue because it is not only one of health, but of children’s, women’s, and human rights as well. It is just as much a cultural, religious, educational, economic, legal, and moral dilemma as is it one of health. For this very reason, appropriate sectors should collaborate to address the underlying problems potentially causing FGM. Because FGM is such a multifaceted issue, great amounts of advocacy and financial resources can be pooled, not only by health organizations, but also by feminist groups, child advocacy agencies, and other institutions. Cooperative forces such as Lawyers Without Borders should provide their services just as the health organizations have done to make the fight against a FGM a truly holistic and effective one.
