The War on Female Genital Mutilation


Fighting For Women's Rights

Marissa Silverman

Spring 2009


    Last spring, the hit reality TV show America’s Next Top Model took its drama to a whole new level when one of the contestants, Fatima, a young Somalian, revealed that she had been “circumcised” at the age of seven. The girls – like most viewers in America – were shocked, since most were unaware of the possibility of such a procedure for women. Female genital mutilation (FGM) or “female circumcision,” is a subject rarely discussed, particularly in the United States. While FGM may be thought of as a problem specific to Africa, it is practiced in regions throughout Asia, the Middle East, the Americas, and Europe as well. In fact, there are an estimated 100 to 140 million young girls and women worldwide who have fallen victim to FGM, and an additional three million at risk annually. FGM is a serious health concern that must be approached with cultural sensitivity, but also from a critical and scientific perspective.

    FGM has a long historical practice, dating back to the time of the ancient Egyptians, who appeared to have had similar practices. The most prevalent incidences of circumcision, however, are linked to religious movements. Although religious groups and leaders sometimes use religious texts to justify FGM, many scholars argue that there is no mention or promotion of female circumcision in the Bible, Torah, or Koran. Even male circumcision, widely practiced amongst Jews and Muslims, is still a matter of great controversy. Male circumcision involves the removal of part or the entire penile foreskin, but it is widely accepted that both circumcised and uncircumcised males are still able to achieve maximum sexual pleasure. In contrast, the clitoris is unique in that it is designed exclusively for pleasure and therefore has a very concentrated number of nerve endings that allow a woman to experience sexual pleasure and orgasm. FGM targets the clitoris in order to reduce the pleasure or an orgasm. FGM is divided into four categories: clitoridectomy, excision, infibulation, and other. Clitoridectomy is partial or total removal of the clitoris, while excision is partial or total removal of the clitoris and labia. Infibulation involves removal of the clitoris and labia and the remaining tissue is sewn together with a small hole retained for menstruation and urination. This hole is torn open and then re-sewn during childbirth. Other forms of FGM as de fined by the World Health Organization (WHO) include “all other harmful procedures to the female genitalia for nonmedical purposes, e.g. pricking, piercing, incising, scraping and cauterizing the genital area.” These procedures are believed to reduce the likelihood that a woman will engage in premarital sex, adultery, or masturbation.

    Regardless, whether or not these procedures achieve such a goal is secondary to the fact that they are enormously detrimental to a women’s physical, mental, and emotional health. The United Nations Population Fund (UNFPA) provides a comprehensive list of the immediate effects, ranging from severe pain and shock to death, and long-term consequences, such as the formation of cysts and abscesses, infertility, and various mental illnesses. The risk of HIV/AIDS transmission and infection is also increased, due to the unsanitary conditions in which these procedures are usually performed and the damage done to the reproductive organs and tissues.


    Although the medical risks associated with FGM seem to be enough reason to halt FGM, there are several other factors continuing this practice. First, there is enormous pressure for young girls to receive an FGM procedure. In many communities men only want to marry girls who are “circumcised,” so parents are pressured to push FGM on their daughters. Other cultural beliefs encouraging the practice of FGM include a belief that the clitoris is poisonous to men and children, that FGM can cure foul genital odors, prevent vaginal cancer and homosexuality, and make a woman more beautiful. Some suggest that it prevents HIV/AIDS, contrary to the studies showing otherwise.
