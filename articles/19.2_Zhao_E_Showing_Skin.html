Showing Off Some Skin


Eric Zhao

Spring 2012


    First impressions are everything in making or breaking a relationship. In most cases, before the first elegant greeting to initiate conversation or the first firm handshake, the quality of your skin is already allowing the other person to make a critical judgment about you. In January of 2012, Ian Stephen and his colleagues at the University of Nottingham published an article in the journal Evolution and Human Behavior that claims women are more attracted to men with healthier skin than men with a masculine face.

    Previous research by psychologist Shelley Adamo at Dalhousie University in Canada published in the journal Animal Behaviour shows that females seek mates based on their disease resistance as well as their current physical condition and vigor. As a result, by favoring fitness in men for a certain immune function or other fitness advantage, the female may ensure that her offspring may also benefit from such selected evolutionary advantages. In order to find such robust men, women choose to look for male physical attractiveness. According to Dr. Michael Cunningham of the University of Louisville, Kentucky, women choose men who possess characteristics that reflect sexual maturity and dominance and find such features in the face. However, Stephen furthers research on what an indication of good health and sexual maturity by showing through his experiment that women find men with a golden complexion, which indicates a healthy diet, attractive. Using photographs of both African and Caucasian men and measuring the tone of face color by varying redness, yellowness, and lightness, Stephen and his associates allowed women to make critical judgments about facial attractiveness, through masculinity and skin color, while controlling for factors, such as the phase at which the women were on their menstrual cycle, relationship status, and their own attractiveness. Researchers hypothesized that the women’s preferences for a masculine facial structure would cancel out. Three different factors varying the individual contribution of carotenoid and melanin to the healthy yellowness of skin color were measured. In the first study, increased yellowness and lightness of skin tone was preferred in Caucasian and black South African faces. In the second study, individuals with diets high in carotenoids, fruits, and vegetables have increased yellowness of skin tone were preferred. In the third study, increased carotenoid coloration was preferred over increases in melanin coloration. Stephen’s results show that skin color was a more consistent indicator of attractiveness, and men with healthier looking “golden” skin scored highest on the attractiveness scale. Skin color was determined to be a more important factor in determining the scale of attractiveness than morphological masculinity. This means that current or recent health of the individual is more important in predicting attractiveness than past health during development. The study was successful in connecting carotenoid coloration to a healthy diet as an indicator of overall robustness, and like in other animal species, such a sign of vigor is important in determining mate choice.

    Stephen’s research is significant to the general population because appearance is regarded as an important aspect of modern social interactions. With widespread marketing by a myriad of industries, from the clothing industry to the practice of aesthetic surgery, aiming to lure consumers with promises of improving the appearances of an individual, such research is vital in assisting these consumers in attaining their goals. Moreover, the highly-sought golden skin color comes from a diet high in carotenoid pigments, such as fruit and vegetables which further promote health and fertility. Perhaps, the best way to maintain an attractive demeanor is simply to maintain a steady and hearty diet supplemented with a healthy and moderate lifestyle.


About the Author

Eric Zhao is a third year Integrative Biology major and intends to pursue a career in medicine. With a profound curiosity in neuroscience, he is currently a student researcher at the Ernest Gallo Clinic and Research Center, studying alcoholism and addiction.
