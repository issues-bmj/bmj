continued from Mind Over Matter


    ICMS is the bread and butter of neuroscience. For decades, almost centuries now, neuroscientists have employed the use of ICSM to infer much our basic knowledge about how the brain is organized. In effect, by stimulating different parts of the brain and observing the physiological responses of the body, scientists have developed a map of the brain, partitioning it into regions corresponding to the physiological responses. By iterating through various simulations at a range of voltage thresholds at different parts of the cortex, scientists constantly update the map to more accurately reflect the localization of function, or physiological response, in the brain.


    So far, Carmena and Overduin have successfully modelled  how the brain controls movement by looking at brain activity. In modeling how the brain controls movement they have essentially solved the first part of the CLDA, enabling them to applying their understanding toward developing a simple device. In the simple neuroprosthetic device, the decoders are biomimetic, ‘that is, [decoders] that mimic how the brain controls movement by reading and decoding brain activity,’ Overduin posits.


    The problem with developing neuroprosthetic devices that subscribe to this first approach is that simple decoding fails to tap into the brains ability to adapt to external devices, making improving and getting better at a task virtually impossible.


    Nevertheless, Overduin thinks that with the right algorithms the brain can eventually learn, like the soccer player, to adapt and improve itself through a feedback mechanism.


    “We experience neuroplasticity every time that we have to learn a new skill, to adapt to change in our bodies as we fatigue, get older, get injured, wear a cast. All of those adaptations rely on the brain’s own mechanisms for adapting to changes in the musculoskeletal system. One approach is thus to put in a BMI, and regardless of the quality of the decoding, to rely on the brain’s own ability to adapt to the BMI, or in other words to help with the decoding process itself. With this second approach, you could exploit the brain’s own ability to adapt to and the decoder by replanning its movements in a fashion the decoder will understand. While this approach has utility for neuroprosthetics, this plasticity takes a while, occurring over days and weeks.”


    However, even with a smart enough decoder and plastic enough brain, the learning curve for a given task seems to be too high. In order to facilitate learning and help the brain teach itself, the decoders need to be cleverly engineered to integrate algorithms that adapt to the ‘brain’s movement plans.’


    “This third approach takes advantage of the fact that plasticity in the decoder can be much faster than the brain’s own mechanisms of neural plasticity,” Overduin said.


    The next step toward developing neuroprosthetics for human subjects are improvements in the ‘realm of miniaturization, energy storage, and biocompatibility’ according to Overduin. In their current state, BMIs are not built to last a life-time, which poses huge practical problems.


    “Widespread application of this work to patients is still premature, in my opinion, as so much still has to be done on the engineering and testing side. Noninvasive technologies are still able to offer better outcomes for all but the most paralyzed patients, even those whose control is limited to their fingers, or their eyes. Invasive BMIs are hard to justify unless they can offer the patient more dimensions of control, and also survive within the patient for many years,” Overduin cautions.


    Beyond having an expiration date, BMIs are not yet sophisticated enough to completely restore mobility in people suffering from tetraplegia. In the case of the soccer player, simple changes to a player’s practice schedule and routine can ultimately yield better results and performance. However, in the case of the individual suffering from tetrapalegia, making lifestyle changes is not nearly as easy, since these individuals suffer physically from corticospinal damage and cannot start walking until they regain control over their motor cortex.  With the right assistance and methods, researchers are hopeful that neuro-corticospinal disorders will be curable in the near future.  Though significant progress has been made in terms of giving more control to subjects over neuroprosthetic devices, there is a lot more left to learn before we start seeing BMIs treatment as a standard medical procedure. Advances in neuroprosthetic devices and BMIs will hopefully treat neurological problems that afflict people everywhere.
