Attention All Scientists!


Edith Han

Spring 2006


    When you turn on the television, how many advertisements for the so-called “life-style drugs” ﬂash before your eyes? Need Viagra® or Levitra® for erectile dysfunction?  How about PropeciaTM for baldness, or XenicalTM for obesity?  Got acne? Maybe Proactive® can help. The list goes on. But where are the ads for life-saving drugs?  Does it really come as a surprise that, according to the Global Forum for Health Research, only 10 percent of the pharmaceutical industry’s research and development (R&D) eﬀorts are targeted at 90 percent of the global disease burden?

    According to the U.S. Orphan Drug Act passed by the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) in 1983, a neglected disease is any disease that: “(a) aﬀects less than 200,000 persons in the United States, or (b) aﬀects more than 200,000 in the United States and for which there is no reasonable expectation that the cost of developing and making available in the United States a drug for such disease or condition will be recovered from sales in the United States of such drug.”  In truth, neglected diseases are essentially those for which there are inadequate proﬁt incentives to perform R&D, resulting in a lack of therapeutics despite the widespread existence and often crippling nature of these maladies. Unfortunately, but not unexpectedly, these are also the diseases that aﬀect the world’s poorest people.

    Although basic research is performed on the neglected dis- eases, industrial entities lack incentives for expanding upon most of this research. This disconnect between basic research and preclinical trials—aptly termed the preclinical gap—is one of three gaps between research at the bench and drug delivery to the masses.  In the article “Drug development for neglected diseases: A deﬁcient market and a public health policy failure,” published in the June 2002 issue of the Lancet, Patrice Trouiller, PharmD, and colleagues of the Centre Hospitalier Universitaire, report that between 1975 and 1999, out of the 1,393 new drugs made available to the public, only 16 were meant for neglected diseases.

    The second gap is a proﬁt-based gap. According to William Fisher III, PhD, Hale and Dorr Professor of Intellectual Property Law at Harvard University, in 2002, 94.9 percent of the global sales of U.S.-based brand-name pharmaceuticals came from Northern America, Europe, Japan, Australia, and New Zealand. Because clinical development is very expensive, developing a drug for a neglected disease that is not marketable in high income countries is ﬁnancially risky.

    Furthermore, even after development, there is often an access gap resulting from problems regarding the registration for the distribution of the drugs in other countries, lack of production, high prices, and drugs being poorly adapted for non-industrialized conditions such as hot climates.

    As a result, even if a treatment for a disease exists, price mark-ups may be so high that these drugs are virtually inaccessible in countries where people live on less than one U.S. dollar per day.  Due to these gaps between research and access, millions of people die every year from preventable and curable diseases.   But while there are many obstacles between R&D and the promotion of global health, universities may act as powerful players in bridging the gap between research and healthcare.  Because many universities have the potential to become major players in the pharmaceutical market, some people suggest focusing advocacy eﬀorts there.

    Why should time, funding, and energy be spent on universities?  Even though the pharmaceutical industry, like any other business, has a goal of maximizing proﬁts, there is potential for universities to signiﬁcantly bridge the gap between research and access to medicines if they change their licensing practices to address the R&D and access gaps.  First, the principles that guide a university are the creation and provision of knowledge for the public beneﬁt—a creed that runs parallel to the goal of improving global health.  Additionally, universities are playing an increasingly larger role in biomedical research and medical training. According to Francis Narin, PhD, and colleagues in a 1997 article published in Research Policy, patenting activity in the universities is of great importance because approximately 50 percent of the drug industry’s new products rely heavily upon academic research.  Since academic research sets the foundation for further development by the pharmaceutical industry, universities are in a position to successfully improve access to essential medicines in lower and middle income (LMI) countries.
