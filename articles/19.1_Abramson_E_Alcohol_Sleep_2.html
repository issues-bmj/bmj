continued from Alcohol and Sleep


   Perhaps the most shocking aspect is how alcohol alters the body’s homeostasis. Initially, administration of alcohol led to a reduction in the body’s core temperature upon falling asleep. But similar to the aforementioned rebound effect, this reduction was followed by an increase in temperature. Although further research is needed to determine how this impacts the human body, it suffices to say that alcohol consumption has the ability to change the body’s core temperature during sleep – a powerful impact.


    As mentioned earlier, growth hormone is released during sleep. Alcohol also affects growth hormone secretion during sleep – something obviously crucial to growing young adults. Alcohol suppresses growth hormone secretion at a dose-related rate, suggesting that binge drinking may significantly alter this hormone’s release during sleep.


    Other neurochemicals involved with sleep that alcohol affects are: gammaaminobutyric acid (GABA), glutamate, and adenosine. GABA is an important neurotransmitter system in the central nervous system (CNS). As the major inhibitor in the CNS, GABA dampens the neuron’s ability to generate a new nerve signal. Alcohol enhances GABA’s actions and therefore helps dampen some neurons signaling power. Thus, alcohol has sedative effects in part because this GABA enhancement leads to drowsiness and SWS promotion.


    In contrast to GABA, glutamate is the main excitatory neurotransmitter in the CNS. As expected, alcohol is an antagonist of glutamate. Similar to GABA and glutamate, adenosine also plays a part in alcohol’s sleep effects. It is a neurotransmitter modulator and slows the function of glutamate in the CNS. Alcohol seems to increase the inhibitory effects of adenosine and consequently may promote SWS and the ability to fall asleep quickly.


    So, using alcohol as a sedative may have long term ramifications that should be considered with the possible short term benefits. Further research is still necessary to determine the full range of alcohol’s effects on sleep – and this research will go far past the immediate future, especially considering there is still a lot to be learned about sleep itself. In the meantime, knowledge of what researchers have found should provide adults a means of making educated decisions on the use (and possible abuse) of alcohol and how it affects our sleep.
