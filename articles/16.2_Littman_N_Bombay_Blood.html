Bombay Blood


Oh! A Rare Phenotype

Nick Littman

Spring 2009


    Picture this: you are about to have a lifesaving liver transplant and the doctors tell you that they do not have enough of your blood type to successfully complete the surgery. In an even worse scenario, they might not have any blood of your type. According to the American Red Cross, blood type AB is the rarest of the ABO blood groups in the United States, since it is only found in about one percent of the Caucasian population in the US. However, there are some blood types far more uncommon and not classified under the typical ABO system. One such example is type Oh (pronounced “OH”), also known as Bombay blood. Persons with type Oh blood can only receive blood from another Oh individual. According to the National Center for Biotechnology Information, type Oh is found in only one in 1,000,000 Europeans and one in 10,000 Southeast Asians, making it one of the rarest blood types in the world.

    The ABO blood type system was created to indicate what blood an individual is compatible with for a blood transfusion. Each letter represents different antigens located on the surface of red blood cells. If the wrong blood type is used in a transfusion, it will lead to an acute auto-immunological reaction, in which a person’s immune system attacks the foreign blood cells, resulting in possible fatality. Based on the ABO system, a person with blood type O can only receive blood from others with blood type O but can donate to any blood type and are thus known as the universal donor. People with blood type AB can receive any blood type but can only donate to others with blood type AB and are known as the universal acceptor. It is generally considered best to receive your exact blood type to minimize the opportunity of an auto-immunological reaction. Unfortunately, the traditional system created for blood transfusions does not work for everybody.

    People with type Oh blood do not fall under the traditional ABO blood system. Type Oh blood is different from type O blood because it is genetically deficient for the H antigen, a precursor to the A and B antigens. Therefore, if a person cannot make H antigens they cannot make either A or B antigens. If someone with Oh blood was not tested for the presence of the H antigen, they would appear to have blood type O, but would still have a negative reaction to the H antigens if they were accidentally transfused with type O blood. Thus, the only type of blood someone with type Oh can receive is type Oh blood.

    Since it is a genetically recessive phenotype, type Oh blood is extremely rare. According to statistics from the National Center for Biotechnology Information, the H deficient gene occurs in only 0.2% of the non-Southeast Asian population, so the chance that someone has two parents with H deficient genes is quite slim.

    There are no known adverse health effects of type Oh blood – the only challenge someone with Oh blood faces would occur in the case of a blood transfusion. Since the occurrence of Oh blood is very low in western countries, there is unlikely to be any Oh blood in the blood bank. The most likely source of Oh blood for an Oh blood carrier would come from one’s own family, so carriers should request that family members undergo blood type testing as a precautionary measure.
