The Future of Geriatric Medicine


A pending healthcare crisis?

Sumrina Yousufzai

Fall 2007


    The current generation of baby boomers not only face inadequate healthcare and reduced Social Security benefits within the next thirty years but also a shortage of geriatricians as they become senior citizens. As more than just end-of-life care, geriatrics has advanced research in several different medical fields such as family medicine. But now, as fewer medical students decide to enter the field, medical researchers and professionals are concerned with how this field will recover.

    Despite its rise since the 1970s, the field of geriatrics is expected to decline rapidly over the next 30 years. The American Geriatrics Society estimates a reduced availability of skilled geriatricians for future generations of the elderly. According to Leslie Libow, MD, Professor of Geriatrics and Adult Development at the Mount Sinai School of Medicine in New York, “The graying of America, a triumph of medical and cultural advances, has caught us unprepared. Our health care system is challenged by the sheer number of elderly people and the demands of providing cost-effective care to those who are frail.”

    As the nation’s aging population grows rapidly with each passing year and more Americans outlive projected age limits even with serious illnesses, the need for professionally trained and experienced geriatricians is becoming increasingly important.

    According to the American Geriatrics Society, all baby boomers will have become age 65 or older by 2035, making that age cohort 70 million strong. They estimate that there are 7,000 geriatricians in the US as of 2005, which translates to approximately one geriatrician for every 5,000 adults aged 65 and older. By 2030, that number is expected to decrease to one geriatrician for every 7,665 elderly adults.

    Seth Landefeld, MD, of the division of geriatrics at the University of California, San Francisco, identifies “inadequate reimbursements for time-intensive care of the aged” as one of the main challenges that the future of geriatric medicine faces. “Medicare pays nearly as much to freeze a benign skin lesion— which takes about a minute—as it pays to spend 30 minutes with a patient and family sorting out why the patient is less able to do things around the house than what used to be the case,” Landefeld says.

    In Internal Medicine News, Steven A. Levenson, MD, and President of the American Medical Directors Association, explains that Medicare’s physician fee schedule for nursing home care urgently needs to be adjusted to reflect the real costs of diagnosis and treatment, since geriatricians often spend more time with their patients for not only diagnosis and examination purposes but for follow-up care and rehabilitation services. “Virtually all of our patients move slowly and many think slowly, so they nearly all take more time,” adds Landefeld.

    Increasing the Medicare benefits for doctors will not only increase medical student interest in geriatrics but also motivate current geriatricians to continue their work and not abandon the field for one that is more lucrative. This will pave the way for more legislative changes that will protect the current elderly population from substandard care and eventually create a workforce of geriatricians to treat the future aging population of America.

    The stigma associated with the field of geriatrics is that it is often considered end-of-life care rather than a research-related medical field with the potential for future advancements. However, as Libow notes, federally and publicly-supported research in the field of geriatrics has established a wealth of knowledge about heart failure, systolic hypertension, nutritional problems, Alzheimer’s disease, and gait problems. Libow notes that geriatrics “spans general medicine and surgery, neurology, psychiatry, rehabilitation medicine, pharmacology, palliative care, and nursing home medicine.”

    Moreover, although geriatrics primarily concerns itself with the diseases and immediate medical concerns of aging, it is a combination of palliative care, physical therapy, acute care, and a variety of other medical fields. Most elderly patients have more than three medical conditions, and geriatricians must, therefore, be capable of managing the complicated health situation, rather than simply providing end-of-life care.

    In order to increase medical students’ interest in the field, Landefeld suggests that medical schools expose students to the challenges, joys, and personal and intellectual rewards of caring for older persons and their families and provide opportunities for students to participate in active learning and discovery about the problems of aging. When asked about his career decision, geriatrician Christopher Callahan, MD, Indiana University Center for Aging Research, cheerfully remarks, “I like the stories old people have to tell of a life lived. I like complexity and I like the people who also chose a career in geriatrics. They are some of the finest and most talented people in the country. I am proud to be associated with the field.”


About the Author

Sumrina Yousufzai is a fourth year Molecular and Cell Biology student interested in the field of geriatrics and gerontology.
