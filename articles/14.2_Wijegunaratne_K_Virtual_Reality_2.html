continued from Virtual Reality Therapy


   Another interesting application of VRT is in the area of pain reduction. In 1996, Hoffman and David Patterson, PhD, at the department of rehabilitation medicine at the University of Washington, co-founded the new technique of using VRT for pain control. According to Patterson, VRT has been found to be particularly successful in conditioning painful medical procedures such as burn wound-dressing changes and aggressive physical therapy. As they reported in the July 2004 issue of Scientific American, Horrman and Patterson worked with the staff at Harborview Burn Center in Seattle, Washington, to determine whether immersive VRT techniques could be used to distract patients from their pain. In the study, two teenagers who were undergoing removal of staples from skin grafts reported severe to excruciating pain while playing Nintendo games but experienced large reductions in pain while immersed in SpiderWorld, a virtual environment. The study concluded that the illusion of being immersed in the 3D virtual environment was far stronger in reducing pain perception than the illusion of going inside a 2D video game. As Hoffman notes, pain has a strong psychological component. When the patient is immersed in a virtual environment, the “spotlight” of the patient’s attention can be lured away from his pain. In this way, the patient no longer focuses his complete attention on the pain and thus reduces the overall amount of pain he perceives.

    One of the latest virtual environments being used specifically for burn patients is called SnowWorld. Developed by Hoffman and his colleagues at the University of Washington Human Interface Technology Lab in collaboration with Harborview Burn Center, SnowWorld provides the illusion of flying through an icy canyon as snowflakes fall down. The cold, snowy imagery is designed to help “put out the fire” from the patient’s pain during burn wound care. As patients slide through the virtual canyon, they can shoot snowballs at snowmen, igloos, robots, and penguins in order to distract themselves. Although this virtual environment has proven to be extremely successful, Patterson points out that “having a whole series of worlds that will have a broad range of appeal will be of great help,” since most of the virtual environments currently available target patients between the ages of 10 and 30.

    VRT is becoming increasingly popular in the health and medical settings. It has been used as a form of pain distraction for pediatric cancer patients undergoing chemotherapy. There are also many virtual reality medical centers that provide the general public with a variety of virtual environments specifically designed for combating a particular phobia. The treatment costs between $100 to $300 an hour and is typically completed in eight one-hour sessions. Though room for improvement still exists, this revolutionizing advancement in psychological treatment finally offers an effective solution for patients who are combating phobias, PTSD, or pain.
