continued from Envisioning the 21st Century


   Another lens, the Viscian Implantable Collamer Lens™ (Viscian ICL) was cleared by the United States Food and Drug Administration for widespread clinical use on December, 22, 2005. Manufactured by Staar Surgical, the lens is capable of correcting for visual deﬁcits that necessitate prescriptions ranging from -3.0 diopters to -20.0 diopters.

    What accounts for the Viscian ICL’s improved versatility over that of Verisye™? It consists of a co-polymer called collamer that contains a small amount of puriﬁed collagen, making the lens highly biocompatible and therefore stable within the eye. Kawesch explains that “the soft, ﬂexible nature of the Visian ICL allows for smaller surgical incisions and potentially reduced recovery times.” During a
20 minute procedure, the implant is placed behind the iris and in front of the eye’s natural lens. “Typically, the small incision is self-sealing and no stitches are required,” he says. A trial funded by Staar Surgical, the results of which were published in the September 2004 issue of the Scientiﬁc Journal of Ophthalmology, examined 294 people ages 22 to 45 with an average pre-operative nearsightedness of -10.06 diopters. After three years with an implantable collamer lens, 59 percent had 20/20 vision or better, while nearly 95 percent had at least 20/40 vision.

    How do these phakic IOLs fare when compared directly with LASIK in terms of vision improvement? Another study by Staar Surgical compared the ICL to LASIK for correction between -8.0 diopters and -12.0 diopters. A one-year post-operative study showed that 52 percent of ICL patients achieved 20/20 or better vision, compared to 36 percent for LASIK. According to Edward E. Manche, MD, Director of Cornea and Refractive Surgery for Stanford Hospitals and Clinics, “the quality of vision is likely to be better than LASIK because you’re not changing the natural asphericity of the cornea, which some people believe leads to some of the visual side-eﬀects of corneal refractive surgery.”

    Still, with all surgical procedures come inherent risks. According to the FDA, the number of possible complications associated with phakic IOL includes an increased chance of retinal detachment, corneal swelling, cataracts, or loss of endothelial cells covering the cornea. Consequently, the FDA has required that lens implants be used only for patients with suﬃcient endothelial cell density to withstand such a loss.

    The reversibility of lens implants appears to be one of the main advantages of phakic IOLs over permanent laser vision correction techniques. “When you have a procedure as permanent as LASIK, you don’t have a safety net. This procedure doesn’t have that ﬁnality,” says Manche. The lens implant can simply be removed if complications arise or the correction loses eﬀectiveness.

    Unfortunately, phakic IOLs are not ﬂawless—corrective lens implants cost as much as laser vision correction, if not more. The cost of the procedure is approximately $3,500 per eye and is done one eye at a time, typically three to six weeks apart. Additionally, like laser vision correction, implanted lenses are generally not covered by insurance. Consequently, many patients may opt for laser vision correction as a more economical alternative.

    Clearly, phakic IOLs do not constitute the ﬁnal solution to deﬁcits in vision. However, at the current breakneck pace of technological advancement, it appears that the real challenge lies in matching a patient’s expectations about various procedures with the treatment that is most suited for his or her visual needs. It is always important to remember that no procedure is “absolutely perfect for every patient that walks through the door,” says Manche. But, at least now, the future for the visually impaired has never been clearer.
