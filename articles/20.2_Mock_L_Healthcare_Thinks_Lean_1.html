Healthcare Thinks Lean


Lauren Mock

Spring 2013

   
    In recent years, hospitals have been applying lean thinking, a process improvement approach originating from manufacturing industries, to redesign healthcare processes to improve the quality of care while controlling and reducing costs. Implementing lean methodologies involves not only a focus on operational aspects of healthcare processes, but also consideration for human and cultural factors as well.

    Under pressure from a stagnant economy, government agencies, payers and insurers have passed the impact of financial tightening to hospitals by reducing reimbursements. In response to this financial crisis, hospitals have aggressively pursued cost reduction initiatives to maintain their shrinking margins. Despite these reductions, medical professionals and healthcare consumers continue their demands to provide or receive quality care. To resolve these seemingly opposing requirements, healthcare has looked to other industries for solutions.

    In translational medicine, medical innovation and pharmaceutical discovery borrow process knowledge from research and development in high tech industries to accelerate the delivery of new drugs and medical procedures to market. Likewise, lean healthcare borrows process know-how in manufacturing and production from, of all places, the automotive industry, to improve processes to achieve rapid, high quality delivery of healthcare at low cost.

    Key components of lean thinking were first pioneered at Toyota Corporation when Taiichi Ohno and Dr. Shigeo Shingo designed the Toyota Production System, incorporating innovative principles such as Just-in-Time delivery to customers of products in the right quality and quantity, and at the right time. These process contributions have proven effective when deployed in a manufacturing environment. Fortunately, lean thinking is generic. It supports process improvement along any value stream and can be applied to any workflow. From automotive manufacturing, lean thinking diffused to other industries, including healthcare.

    James P. Womack and Daniel T. Jones coined the word “lean” to describe this type of highly efficient product delivery.  In their influential book, Lean Thinking: Banish Waste and Create Wealth in Your Corporation (1996), the authors describe value as a “capability provided to customer at the right time at an appropriate price, as defined in each case by the customer.” In particular, lean thinking, as Womack and Jones wrote, can be used to “specify value, line up value creating actions in the best sequence, conduct these activities without interruption whenever someone requests them, and perform them more and more effectively.”

    Operational principles of lean thinking focus upon customer value and the needs of a customer. This concern helps distinguish the various steps of a value creation activity, the value stream. For example, one value stream in a hospital is the patient treatment value stream which may involve steps like admissions, evaluation, testing, treatment, and finally, discharge.

    Within a value stream, value adding steps contribute directly to creating the product or service a customer demands. Non-value adding steps, such as waiting, excess inventory, remediating mistakes, or unnecessary movement do not. Other steps cannot be eliminated given the limits of current technologies, but may be required such as record-keeping or reporting.

    Steps of the value stream that do not add value for a customer are wasteful. They consume the hospital’s money, capacity, time and other resources. For example, in a patient value stream, much of a patient’s time is spent wastefully waiting. With the objective of eliminating or reducing waste, lean methodologies employ methods such as synchronization of tasks, standardization of work to make tasks simple and repetitive to reduce errors, and reducing or eliminating inventory. Applying lean thinking to healthcare processes reduces waiting times, minimizes re-admission rates, saves money through cost avoidance, and decreases lab turnaround times. These techniques drive efficiency, productivity, and timely, error-free delivery that lower the costs of care.
