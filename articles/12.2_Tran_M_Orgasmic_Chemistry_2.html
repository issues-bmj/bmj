continued from Orgasmic Chemistry


   The orgasm might have also developed as an evolutionary pleasure mechanism to promote procreation: “It would seem that the pleasure is a means to an end—rather like the selfish gene, except this is a generous gene,” says Dr. O’Brien. “In ‘carrot and stick’ terms, the carrot of pleasure is held out to encourage us to mutually stimulate the two sets of external genitalia, with the ultimate goal for the organism being procreation.” Not only do orgasms affect bonding between couples and promote procreation, but some argue that they also encourage women to prefer one mate over others.

    Over the last decade, scientists have proposed that the female orgasm is an advanced adaptation that allows women to subconsciously decide when and by whom she will conceive. In 1993, British biologists Robin Baker, Ph.D., of the University of Manchester, and Mark Bellis, Ph.D., Director of the Center of Public Health, tested the “up-suck” hypothesis, which suggests that women do not always orgasm during intercourse because they favor certain sperm over others. Baker and Bellis discovered that orgasms that occur between one minute before and 45 minutes after ejaculation allow women to retain considerably more sperm than they would during non-orgasmic sex. The muscular contractions allow the sperm to enter the cervix more easily and increase the sperm’s chances of encountering an egg. Multiple orgasms boost the probabilities even higher. If women do not orgasm during sex, little to no sperm is retained. “Maybe that is why so many women are having problems with fertility these days!” says Kerner.

    Procreation also seems to have a biologically instinctive aspect. For example, female animals, from insects to mammals, prefer males with a high degree of body symmetry because they have strong immune systems and are able to successfully forage for food. In order to secure strong genes for their offspring, females will look to the symmetrical male. Evolutionary biologists Randy Thornhill, Ph.D., and psychologist Steve Gangestad, Ph.D., at the University of New Mexico in Albuquerque have confirmed that this is also true in humans. In their study from 1993, more women reported orgasms during intercourse with symmetrical mates. This supports the theory that the female orgasm is, indeed, an adaptation for sperm selectivity.

    Thus far, not all scientists accept the female orgasm as an adaptive trait. If the proadaptation theory is correct, then a healthy couple experiencing difficulty conceiving might find a solution in uterine contractions. However, even if Gould has been correct all along, and the female orgasm is not a clever result of adaptation, its value still does not diminish. Dr. Hill-Sakurai asserts: “the female orgasm is a biological process, but you can’t separate it from psychology. Regardless of what it evolved for, there is value in a couple’s attempt to understand it.”
