Smart Pills


Hyangsoo Lee

Fall 2012


    The label on the bottle says, “Take 3 pills per day, 1 pill 30 minutes after every meal, for 7 days.” While the directions sound simple enough, actually taking prescribed pills according to the instructions provided by the doctor may not be so easy. A lot can happen within the 30 minutes after a meal; going to lecture, getting dessert, drinking coffee, etc., which can all divert one’s attention from remembering to take that pill. When confronted by the doctor at a follow-up appointment on whether the medication was taken regularly, it may be difficult to ‘fess up’ and admit to the still-full bottle of pills on your desk. Aside from the obvious statement that it is important that prescribed pills be taken in concordance to their assigned time and amount, doctors also need to be aware of when patients are not taking their pills so that prescriptions can be adjusted. To provide a more honest interaction that does not leave room for excuses between a doctor and his patient, digestible microchips embedded in drugs are being developed which tells doctors whether a patient is taking their medications as prescribed.

    It is surprising how many people forget to take their pills; rest assured, you are not the only one who has skipped out on their medication. According to Eric Topol, director of the Scripps Translational Science Institute at La Jolla, California, that number is quite large: “about half of all people don’t take medications like they’re supposed to.” At the level of Tylenol or Dayquil, not abiding by the ‘take two tablets every 6 hours’ might not seem like a big deal, but certain medicines may have dangerous consequences if they are skipped. One example is antibiotics. According to the Food and Drug Administration, if the full course of antibiotics against an infection is not taken as prescribed, a small number of bacteria may remain and these can multiply and spread to create a new strain that has resistance to antibiotics. Known as Methicillin-Resistant Staphylococcus Aureus (MRSA) infections, these types of bacteria can be especially hard to kill with antibiotics currently on the market and may even end up being fatal to the patient.

    A digitized pill can overcome the problem of untimely medicine consumption. Any regular oral pill can become ‘digital’ by embedding a microchip into it, which functions as a sensor. These sand grain-sized sensors are composed of a minute silicon chip containing trace amounts of magnesium and copper. Once the pill with the implanted microchip reaches the stomach after being swallowed, the sensor generates a slight voltage in response to the digestive juices. This reaction sends a signal to the surface of the person’s skin, where a patch then relays the information to a mobile phone belonging to a healthcare provider. Developed by a company in California called Proteus Digital Health, these sensors were recently approved by the FDA and the analogous regulatory agency in Europe after they passed safety and efficacy tests.

    However, despite the solutions these digital pills offer in terms of monitoring patients’ medicine uptake, there have been voices of concern regarding this technology. Firstly, what gives doctors the right to monitor patients as though the patients are children? To this, George Savage, co-founder and chief medical officer at Proteus, replies that the purpose of the sensors is not for doctors to castigate people, but to understand how patients are responding to treatments. If someone is found to have difficulty taking his pills on a regular basis, the healthcare provider may use this information provided by the microchips to prescribe larger dosages that may be taken less frequently or suggest a different treatment altogether. Secondly, how will these microchips affect the price of medicine? Currently, the sensor has only been tested on a select number of placebo pills. In the future, Proteus hopes to produce the sensors in bulk, which will make them cheaper - the company’s target is a penny per pill. Although this technology is still undergoing development, the age of digital medicinal care may be fast approaching in the foreseeable future. In a world where everything is becoming digitized, even pills are turning ‘smart.’
