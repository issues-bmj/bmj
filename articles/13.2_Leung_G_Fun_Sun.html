Fun, Sun, Get a Surgery Done!


The growing trend of transplant tourism

Geraldine Leung

Spring 2006


    The notion of tourism conjures up images of exotic locations, fun in the sun, and escape from the banalities of everyday life. However, a recent trend indicates tourists may have found a new reason to travel: readily available surgical procedures. On the positive side, many people are combining their vacations with health care, pursuing common medical procedures such as cosmetic surgeries, cardiovascular bypasses, and hip replacements. Regardless, a much more sinister drive for medical tourism exists – transplant tourism.

    As ﬁnding an organ donor becomes increasingly diﬃcult in the United States (U.S.), desperate patients are turning to the organ black market in other countries. Wealthy patients have the option of traveling to foreign countries to receive organs harvested from paid donors, allowing the organ recipients to cut corners and shorten their wait period. Consequently, transplant tourism provides an advantage to those who are ﬁnancially well-oﬀ.

    In the U.S., patients in need of an organ transplant face the grim reality of organ shortages. According to the United Network for Organ Sharing (UNOS), in 2002, more than six thousand patients died while waiting for a matching donor. Moreover, a new patient is added to the UNOS list every fourteen minutes. Harish Mahanty, MD, a transplant surgeon at the California Paciﬁc Medical Center, explains that there are “17,000 patients on the waitlist for a liver transplant, but only 4,000 surgeries are performed a year.” The waiting period is dependent upon an individual’s Meld score, a numerical assignment representing the patient’s need for surgery. For a patient with blood type O and a high Meld score, the wait usually lasts up to one year. Patients with a lower Meld score may never receive surgery. For kidney transplants, Mahanty states that “it is pure waiting time and may last up to ﬁve years.” As patients grow tired of waiting, some venture into the organ black market for a quick solution to their medical dilemma.

    The organ black market is a growing industry that partakes in the traﬃcking of harvested organs, a practice that is illegal in the U.S. but diﬃcult to regulate globally. The invention of cyclosporine, an immunosuppressant, has spurred the popularity of the organ black market because patients no longer require exact matches to their donors’ genetic haplotypes.

    Transplant tourism involves the transplantation of an organ from a donor residing in a developing nation to a relatively wealthy recipient who has traveled to the donor’s country for surgery. Donors are willing to sell an organ for a multitude of reasons: to pay oﬀ debts, deal with a sudden familial health crisis, or provide for their households. Lawrence Cohen, PhD, professor of anthropology at the University of California, Berkeley (UC Berkeley), quips, “People will go to China to get a kidney from an executed prisoner, and the hospital will be next to the execution site.”

    In addition to the substantial ﬁnancial considerations at play in the realm of transplant tourism, the safety of both patients during and after the surgery is debatable. Mahanty believes that it is risky to turn to transplant centers that are not reputable, as black market surgeries do not guarantee that licensed doctors and nurses perform the operation. Nancy Scheper-Hughes, PhD, professor of medical anthropology at UC Berkeley, comments that donors may be “slam-dunked” onto the operating bed. Health complications can arise if the donor and the recipient are not provided with adequate post-operative care. In truth, donors often do not receive any further treatment after the procedure is complete and often are stigmatized as “organ prostitutes,” as Scheper-Hughes remarks. Recipients may also not receive an adequate dosage of immunosuppressant medication. Such sloppy medical practices would not be tolerated in the U.S. As Mahanty notes, “It is the post-op care where the care is really at. In the U.S., ethically, we are obligated to care for [patients].”

    Aside from the purely clinical problems that arise from transplant tourism, the interplay between wealthy organ recipients and poor donors sets the stage for profound socio-economic consequences. As Scheper-Hughes notes in her article “Keeping an Eye on the Global Traﬃc in Human Organs,” which appeared in the May 2003 issue of the Lancet, the organ black market is a locus at which the “circulation of mortally sick bodies travel in one direction and healthy organ in another.” In this sense, the organ black market allows the rich to exploit the poor. Cohen explains that donors living in poverty sell their organs due to ﬁnancial diﬃculties, but selling organs can actually whirl them into a more vicious cycle of debt. Since most donors are already deeply in debt, the compensation for their organs will go directly towards repaying their debts. Cohen emphasizes that many donors discover themselves unable to perform the hard labor that they were capable of doing before the operation. As a result, the donors tend to fall back into debt, without the ability to ever repay it.

    Unfortunately, Mahanty admits that he, too, has dealt with patients who have considered traveling abroad for organ transplants. As new studies are being conducted, researchers will hopefully glean more information about the medical and socio- economic repercussions of this exchange. While the best way to curtail transplant tourism would entail increasing the availability of organs within developed nations, it does not appear as if any current measures will be able to accomplish this in the near future. As Mahanty warily concedes, “The waiting time is getting longer because we don’t have enough organs [...] We are trying to utilize every organ that is procured in the right patient.”
