continued from Orthokeratology


    One of the few risks of orthokeratology that causes lasting damage to the patient is infection, a risk that any contact lens wearer faces. There have been reports of infectious keratitis – inflammation of the cornea that causes watery, pain in the eyes, and blurred vision – related to overnight orthokeratology lens wear in China. Although rare, eye infections are painful and, in worst cases, can lead to permanent vision loss. But Walline explains that the risk of severe problems can be greatly decreased by educating patients and emphasizing proper hygiene. With careful contact lens use, infections are highly unlikely to occur. “[In Asia,] [the lenses] were dispensed by literally anyone without follow- up,” explains Walline. “After the government stepped [in] to regulate who could dispense the contact lenses, the number of eye infections dramatically decreased.”


    Orthokeratology has been particularly popular in the Asian market, largely because nearsightedness is more prevalent in Asia. Recent studies have also found that orthokeratology slows the progression of nearsightedness in children, thus causing concerned parents to seek out orthokeratology and optometrists to recommend the procedure. “The clinicians have noticed that the patients treated with orthokeratology tend to have very stable prescriptions, compared to other patients who wear glasses, who tend to have faster progression of myopia,” says Maria Liu, MD, OD, PhD candidate at the University of California, Berkeley, School of Optometry, who is currently researching how and why orthokeratology prevents myopia. Although child subjects would be ideal for her research, Liu is bound by child safety regulations and has been working with college students because rules for their participation are much less stringent. Since myopia correlates with nearsighted-work, students doing regular intensive reading also experience progressive myopia past their developing years, making them a suitable target population for this research. Although Liu’s research has yet to see progress in reversing nearsightedness, it does imply that adults can also benefit from orthokeratolgy’s effect of slowing down myopia.


    Unfortunately, orthokeratology does not work for every patient. “About 20 percent of people have difficulty adjusting to gas-permeable contact lens wear,” says Walline. “So they find out within the first week or two that [orthokeratology] is not for them.” Walline adds that the treatment does not work for extremely nearsighted people either. At this time, there are no Ortho-K lenses with corrective methods designed to treat patients with more than six diopters of myopia. Also, the development of this field is affected by the popularity of other options for vision correction. “It will depend on whether or not the contact lenses get better, in terms of comfort and wearability,” comments Polse. “Orthokeratology became popular because people didn’t want to wear glasses and they didn’t like contacts, and they didn’t want refractive surgeries, either. If a person could put a contact lens in their eye and wear if for a month without ever taking it out, and it’s safe and comfortable, there would be very little interest in orthokeratology.” Since it has its own unique benefits, orthokeratology can still complement other treatments, even if it may not replace them.
