From the iPod(R) to the Test Scores

A Study of How Music Alters Your Brain


Garren Langford

Spring 2013


    Whether it is crooning from the radio, blasting in your friend’s dorm, or beating from your expensive headphones, music is everywhere in your life. Since music is such an important piece of your life, have you ever wondered how your brain responds to the stereo symphony of sound around you? Interestingly to know, your favorite genre of music could be responsible for the development of your intelligence since an early age! Even now, it’s possible that it can be affecting your behavior and ability to perform in your classes.

    Many people have taken musical lessons as a child. It might be a good idea to thank your mom for forcing you to go to violin lessons every week because studies show that performing such music creates changes in your brain that lasts for many years to come as you develop into who you are today. Data confirms that communication skills and reading ability are correlated to musical training experience.  A good reader and speaker is able to pick out specific speech sounds, put meaning to these sounds, and retain the information in their memory for next time. Taking musical lessons and listening to music every day are proven to improve these parts of your brain. Think of it as a muscle--the more you work out, the stronger it gets. This analogy is of great import when extended to consider musicians or music aficionados. By listening to music and speech all around you, your brain stores these experiences and is able to react better the next time. According to a study done by the Auditory Neuroscience Laboratory at Northwestern University, the brain’s response to music heavily depends on one’s experience with musical training--specifically, a musically trained brain records much clearer activity than a non-musically trained brain.

    Musical training does not require hours of studying classical theory. Simply listening to your favorite iPod playlists everyday also helps train your brain to distinguish the many sounds blended in the musical production process. This easy training helps you to pick out minor details in songs, enhancing your brain’s ability to discern other subtleties it can detect in everyday sounds and speech. It is this skill of being able to pick out subtleties that trains people who are hard of hearing or experiencing hearing loss to understand speech amongst a noisy background.

    Music can not only being enjoyment into one’s life, but can also enhance the quality of it on a scientific level. Consider the profound impact music could has had on your life without your knowledge! In an urban college campus, sounds are creating music all around you--and it might be beneficial if you sit down and listen a little closer.
