   Indeed, there is little financial or regulatory incentive for research sponsors to spend limited resources on analyzing and reporting results from failed clinical trials. In a recent commentary in the September 2011 issue of Science Translational Medicine journal, Michael Rogawski, Chair of the Department of Neurology in the UC Davis School of Medicine protested, “Translational medicine cannot approach its full potential if negative drug developments are unpublished.” Co-commentator, Howard J. Federoff, a neuroscientist and Dean of the School of Medicine of Georgetown University added, “Such reporting would lead to greater patient safety, improve treatment research strategies, and allow a more efficient use of limited resources.” Moreover, the opportunities to learn and improve research yield are vast: the 21 new drugs approved by the Food and Drug Administration in 2010, appears relatively paltry in comparison to the number of medical research papers that were published annually, over 800,000 in 2008, and the federal spending in medical research that has almost doubled in the past decade to over $30 billion in 2010.


    Despite the great potential for translational medicine to accelerate processes and improve research yields, some remain skeptical of applying engineering techniques to solve medical problems.  Derek Lowe, a drug research scientist wrote in his blog, “medical research is different than semiconductor research. It's harder. Ever seen one of those huge blow-ups of a chip's architecture? It's awe-inspiring, the amount of detail that's crammed into such a small space. And guess what - it's nothing, it's the instructions on the back of a shampoo bottle compared to the complexity of a living system.”


    Nevertheless, seeing such potential benefits, academic medical centers across the United States like ones at Stanford University, The University of Pennsylvania, and Duke University have established program offices to coordinate implementation of translational medicine. The University of California in San Francisco (UCSF), for example, proactively promotes translational medicine through its Clinical and Translational Science Institute (CTSI). The CTSI encouraged the partnership and collocation of biotechnology and pharmaceutical companies like Bayer, Celgene and Merck near the new UCSF campus to work with university labs to accelerate new discoveries to practical applications. In addition, within university research programs, scientists receive, with patient permission, rapid feedback on the progress of treatments to help researchers learn from the results of in-clinic patient care.

    Many universities have also established translational medicine studies as a post-graduate discipline. The Master’s in Translational Medicine, a two year program of the University of California at Berkeley and UCSF, was launched in fall 2010 through a $1.5 million gift from Andy Grove.  Today, leading research in translational medicine is announced through conferences and new publications such as The Journal of Translational Medicine, launched in 2003, and The American Journal of Translational Research and Science Translational Medicine, both which began publication in 2009.


    The importance of translational medicine cannot be underestimated. In a Fall 2002 letter to readers of Stanford Medicine Magazine, Philip Pizzo, M.D., Dean of the Stanford School of Medicine, said, “In the absence of translational and patient-oriented clinical research, the delivery of medical care would remain stagnant and uninformed by the tremendous progress now taking place in biomedical science.” Today, for every 5,000 to 10,000 compounds that enter drug discovery phase, only one reaches final FDA approval. Yet, as research continues in the emerging field of translational medicine, scientists, clinicians, patients, industry, and policymakers will continue to accelerate and improve processes, assuring that nothing gets lost in translation.
