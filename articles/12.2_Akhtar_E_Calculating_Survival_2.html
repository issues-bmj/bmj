continued from Calculating Survival


   Doctors seek to allay concerns by reminding the public that APACHE is only one of many tools used by doctors in making their assessment of situations. John Luce, M.D., of the University of California, San Francisco, whose research specializes in medical ethics, says, “What you have to keep in mind is one of our jobs is to prognosticate.” That question boils down to how doctors best come up with prognostic information, whether it includes studying textbooks, referring to personal experience, reading accounts in journals, or even using the APACHE system. Dr. Luce argues that all these methods “offer a legitimate amount of information if the doctor realizes that [each has its] limitations. In general, together, they can give you pretty good group data.” Dr. Knaus reaffirms this sentiment: “It doesn’t make the decision… APACHE just gives you information. It doesn’t touch the patient. It doesn’t treat them.” The belief among these doctors is that no physician would use the advice of the APACHE system alone; all understand that it only ranks as one other factor used in the doctor’s prognosis. The actual treatment of the patient is still extremely personal and run entirely by the doctor.

    Additionally, according to the United States legal code, the patient or the patient’s family is responsible for the choice of treatment. This means that the doctor presents and explains the varying options, but the choice of treatment legally belongs to the patient. APACHE critics, however, fear that the machine may introduce complacency or even encourage doctors to withhold certain treatment options based on the score of the machine. For example, although a particular treatment is available, the doctor may not mention it to the patient’s family since the patient’s survival rate is low.

    As far as the effect the machine may have on determining ICU resource allocation, Dr. Luce asserts that in his personal experience of 25 years of practice, he has never witnessed an event in which a patient could not receive the treatment he needed. “Discussions for the paradigm [of ICU prioritizing] turn out to be intellectual exercises that don’t really happen. In our country with our great resources, we tend to find things for people, especially when life is in the balance.”

    So what can we make of the debate regarding the APACHE system? Clearly, the machine has its strong points and its limitations. While all doctors agree that an additional prognostic tool is always beneficial, they acknowledge that the machine could induce devastating effects if used irresponsibly. Thus, the debate narrows down to the doctors’ choice to do what the general public already trusts them to do: recognize the gravity of their profession and treat every patient with the care he deserves.
