   So to counter this issue, scientists Wadih Arap and Renata Pasqualin decided to collaborate on making a synthetic version of the AMP molecule called KLAKLAK-2, to give humans an edge against our drug resistant adversaries.  KLAKLAK-2 is similar to all naturally-occurring AMP’s in that it has a left-handed twist to its corkscrew shape.   In order to make the molecule more competitive against cancer and bacteria, the scientists altered the molecule to give it a right-handed, counterclockwise twist instead.  Cancer and bacteria have evolved always fighting against the left-handed versions of AMPs, since previously, they were the only kind that existed. Because of this, cancer and bacteria have a diminished ability to resist man-made ‘mirror’ compounds because they cannot use the machinery they have evolved to disarm the right-handed weapons, says Ben-Jacob.  To enhance the effectiveness of this treatment, the Rice team has suggested combining the modified KLAKKLAK-2 with the original toxin, which would “confuse” drug-resistant bacteria, essentially delivering the drug version of a One-Two Punch.  


    In the arms race against drug-resistant diseases, “Nature is smarter than we are,” Ben-Jacob says. “Time and again, we have seen that seemingly simple cellular foes like bacteria and cancer can learn to mount effective defenses against any new drug we create. It is time to accept them as sophisticated enemies. We should attack them in much the same way that a well-trained boxer or military commander would go after a wily opponent — with multiple, coordinated blows of very different kinds.” Our survival depends upon it.
