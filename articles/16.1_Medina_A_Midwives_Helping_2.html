continued from Midwives Helping People Out


    When differences in prenatal education and psychosocial care were examined, Oakley notes that 98 percent of women in the CNM group, compared to 81 percent of women in the physician group, received nutritional education. Moreover, 92 percent of the CNM patients, compared to 71 percent of the physician patients, received specific suggestions regarding their prenatal diet. Oakley also cites that 75 percent of the women in the CNM cohort, as opposed to 57 percent of the women in the physician cohort, reported that sexual intercourse during pregnancy had been discussed. This illustrates the level of intimacy CNMs have with their patients, as 97 percent in the CNM group stated that this discussion had been with their primary care provider, as opposed to 69 percent in the physician group.

    A 1998 news release by the National Center for Health Statistics states that “CNMs generally spend more time with their patients during prenatal visits and put more emphasis on patient counseling and education and providing emotional support. Most CNMs are with their patients on a one-on-one basis during the entire labor and delivery process providing patient care and emotional support, in contrast with physician’s care which is often more episodic.” Cragin echoes this sentiment by explaining that “midwifery is about creating relationships with patients as well as collaborating with them to create a shared view of birth.”

    When neonatal outcomes were examined, further differences between the care-provider groups were revealed. In a 1998 study published in the Journal of Epidemiology & Community Health, Marian MacDorman, PhD, Centers for Disease Control and Prevention Division of Vital Statistics, observed that the infant mortality rate in singleton vaginal deliveries at 35 to 43 weeks of gestation was 13.9 percent lower for births delivered by the CNM group. Additionally, MacDorman cites that the percentage of low birth weight and pre-term births was higher in the physician group, although this statistic may be influenced by CNMs’ obligation to hand high-risk cases over to physicians. In contrast, Oakley’s study highlights excellent neonatal outcomes in both groups. While respiratory difficulties did not exist in either of the two provider groups, she points to meaningful differences in infant mortality and significant differences in the following indicators of health: gestational age, infant length, and head circumference and contusions.

    Oakley also notes that a significantly higher number of the neonates in the CNM group, 91.7 percent compared to 82.2 percent, were breastfed immediately after delivery. Furthermore, neonates in the physician group were less likely to remain with their mothers throughout their hospital stay, which illustrates the CNM emphasis on the importance of mother-infant bonding.

    In the November 2006 issue of the Journal of Obstetric, Gynecological, and Neonatal Nursing, Cragin concludes that “even among moderate-risk patients, the midwifery model of care with its limited use of interventions can produce outcomes equivalent to or better than those of the biomedical model.”” Therefore, since the two provider groups have similar safety and efficacy results, patients need to consider their preferred philosophical approach to the birthing process before selecting a provider.
