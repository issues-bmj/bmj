The Future of Contraception


The Possibility of Male Birth Control

Emily Fireman

Spring 2009


    “I’m on birth control,” may soon be something that not only women can say, thanks to promising new research on male hormonal contraception (MHC). While this hormone therapy is not reliable enough to market just yet, it does raise the possibility of a future line of hormone contraceptives designed especially for men.

    Like many other biological mechanisms in the body, spermatogenesis is regulated by a negative feedback loop, meaning that the more sperm there is available in the testes, the slower sperm production occurs. The process begins when hormones made in the brain trigger the production of the hormones in testicular cells, and these testicular hormones then help to produce sperm. One of these hormones, testosterone, not only participates in spermatogenesis but also signals the brain to stop producing the messenger hormones. Therefore, when testosterone levels rise, spermatogenesis ceases to occur. Male hormonal contraception involves large dose injections of testosterone, which prevent messenger hormones from signaling testicular cells to produce the other hormones necessary for spermatogenesis.

    Researchers from Schering, a pharmaceutical company in the Netherlands, published a study in the April 2007 issue of the Endocrine Society Journal about the effectiveness of MHC in preventing sperm production. While the research team is not the first to look into male hormonal contraception, their study is the largest to date and is also the first to be a double-blinded, controlled study, meaning that neither the researcher nor the participants know which participants have received the treatment. These methodological improvements make it “a stepping stone to product development and registration,” according to Peter Liu, PhD, an andrologist at the University of Sydney, in an editorial in the May 2008 issue of the Journal of Clinical Endocrinology & Metabolism responding to the Schering team’s findings.

    For 44 weeks, 354 men received either an injection of testosterone or a placebo injection every ten weeks. Ninety percent of men who received a testosterone injection stopped producing sperm for the entire study, while three percent saw no decrease in spermatogenesis. The other seven percent of men temporarily stopped producing sperm but began again before the study was over.
The results intrigued researchers. Why do some men continue to produce sperm even without signals from the brain, while others, more surprisingly, experience a rebound effect? Liu speculates that some men are capable of producing the hormones necessary for spermatogenesis without receiving a signal from the brain.

    Because ten percent is still considered a high failure rate for a birth control device, MHC is not yet ready to be available in the market. Nevertheless, the Schering study is important in establishing the potential for such a contraceptive device. Liu published a study in the May 2008 issue of Endocrine Care showing that testosterone injections are more reliable in conjunction with injections of progestin, a hormone closely related to testosterone.

    Even if this combination injection proves reliable, male hormonal contraceptives will still face marketing obstacles. Pharmaceutical companies question the profitability of MHC on two fronts. First, they wonder whether men will want to buy a product that promises infertility. Second, pharmaceutical companies worry that women will refuse to rely on a pregnancy prevention method that requires complete trust in the man, and that this distrust will discourage men from purchasing the product. Although valid, the pharmaceutical companies’ concerns run contrary to results from marketing research. Schering’s 2005 global survey found that 55 percent of men would be interested in a “new male fertility control.” Despite this positive response, Schering stopped funding MHC research in 2006.

    Researchers lament the reluctance of drug companies to give this birth control device a chance. Nonetheless, they hope that as they fine-tune the dosages and combinations of hormone injections, drug companies and the general public will be more receptive to the idea of male hormonal contraceptives as a reliable form of birth control, if not just something that can be used to complement the existing female contraceptive.
