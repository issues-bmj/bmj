Infectious Changes: Behavior of Mice Permanently Change Following Toxoplasma Infection


Michelle Zhang

Fall 2013


    There is a parasite lurking in the litter-boxes of many homes that is both harmful and intriguing. This parasite has caused the decline of the California Sea Otter Population, and is dangerous to pregnant women and immunocompromised individuals. But it’s more than just a pest. A recent study has given new insight into the way this formidable parasite is able to manipulate its host upon infection. Research done by Wendy Ingram, a graduate student at the University of California, Berkeley, working in the labs of Michael Eisen and Ellen Robey, used this single-celled parasite to permanently remove the fear of cats in mice. This surprising result is not only interesting because it helps elucidate the mechanism by which the parasite affects host behavior but also because it has implications for the medical community.


    The parasite central to the study is Toxoplasma gondii, or Toxo for short, and was discovered by the scientific community in 1908. How Toxo changes host behavior, however, is still unknown. Ingram’s goal is to understand how the mechanism behind these behavioral changes. In an experiment, Ingram studied whether mice infected with Toxo display no aversion to cats through a rigorous assay. Both infected and uninfected mice were exposed to predator (bobcat) and non-predator (rabbit) urine. Whereas uninfected mice displayed clear aversion to predator urine compared to non-predator urine, mice infected with Toxo displayed no aversion to predator urine.


    What is especially surprising about the result is that there was a sustained loss of the innate aversion to predator urine after the infection had cleared. This result was confirmed by testing for Toxo DNA in the brain of mice at 4 months, which showed undetectable levels of parasite DNA. After four months, when there was no longer an active infection of Toxo, mice still showed aversion to the bobcat urine. However, some researchers, such as Dr. Glenn McConkey from the University of Leeds, suggest that Ingram’s study may have overlooked Toxo hidden in tissues outside the brain. This would undermine the assumption that Toxo causes permanent changes in the brain. Ingram states that while it is possible, her sensitive assays for Toxo DNA show that there is no Toxo present in the brain.
