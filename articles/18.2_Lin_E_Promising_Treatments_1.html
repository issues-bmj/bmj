A Healing Vision

Promising Treatments for Diabetic Cataractogenesis


Eric Lin

Spring 2011

   
    According to the World Health Organization, cataracts are the leading cause of blindness worldwide, accounting for almost half of the cases of blindness. Cataracts are characterized by a clouding of the lens and are very common in the elderly. According to National Eye Institute, more than half of all Americans either have a cataract or have had cataract surgery by age 80.


    The degree of cataract severity ranges from slight opacity to complete obstruction of the passage of light through the lens. The lens helps to focus light on the retina, allowing our brain to receive electrical signals thereby producing the image that we see. Opacities in the lens severely inhibit the visual field and quality of life of the patient. Often times the opacity can become so severe that the patient will choose to undergo a surgical procedure to have the lens removed. However, these surgeries can become problematic, especially in third world countries. In a study published in the journal Current Opinion in Ophthalmology, Dr. Gullapalli Rao of the LV Prasad Eye Institute found that cataract removal surgeries are often performed by untrained professionals in unsanitary conditions, resulting in eye infections and irreversible eye damage.


    While cataracts have not made a significant contribution to blindness in United States, the number of patients with cataracts may begin to spike in the near future. A study led by Dr. Irina Obrosova at Louisiana State University found that diabetes is associated with a five-fold increase in an individual’s chance of developing cataracts. Diabetes puts a tremendous mental and financial strain upon both the patient and healthcare system. According to the American Diabetes Association, diabetes-related medical expenses cost taxpayers and patients over $217 million a year. Unfortunately, the situation could become worse because diabetics have a much higher risk for developing cataracts. The link between cataracts and diabetes could quickly accelerate the magnitude of damage that cataracts have in the United States.


    In order to understand the mechanism for the formation of diabetic cataracts, it should first be noted that there is no such thing as a standard cataract. Each type of cataract is unique and can have different ages of onset, location, and cause. According to Dr. Catherine Cheng of the University of California, Berkeley’s School of Optometry, cataracts can be caused by a “multitude of factors including genetic predisposition, trauma to the eye, inflammation, metabolic disorders, radiation damage, and drug use.”


    The uniqueness of diabetic cataracts is due to the metabolic mechanism of the lens. The lens is composed of avascular tissue, meaning it does not have blood vessels. Unlike other tissues that have blood vessels, the lens relies far more on glycolysis to produce the necessary ATP for function. Glycolysis is the metabolic pathway that prepares glucose to be eventually converted into ATP, the compound which supplies most of our energy.  In most cells, glycolysis supplies around 17% of the necessary ATP, whereas in avascular lens cells glycolysis supplies 65% of the necessary ATP.
