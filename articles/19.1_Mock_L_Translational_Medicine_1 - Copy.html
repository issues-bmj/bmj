An Engineer’s Re-Interpretation of Medicine: Translational Medicine


Lauren Mock

December 2011


    In 2005, Steve Jobs authorized his engineers to commence work on a revolutionary product that integrated multiple complex technologies. Less than two years later, Jobs announced and delivered his innovative product, the now ubiquitous iPhone to his customers. At a similarly rapid pace, Andy Grove, as President or CEO of Intel Corporation, launched complex computer microprocessors with drastically improved chip architectures in quick succession every few years. In contrast to the mercurial speed of high tech innovation , John Castellani, President of  the PhRMA pharmaceutical trade association reported in his organization’s Catalyst blog, “Biopharmaceutical research is a painstaking field, one in which an average new medicine can take 10 to 15 years to be developed and brought to patients.”


    To reduce time-to-market and improve the yields from medical research, an emerging field of medicine known as translational medicine seeks to apply engineering principles and high-tech product development processes to medical innovation. Reducing time and improving yields are critical. Once afflicted by prostate cancer and now Parkinson’s disease, Andy Grove, like many patients suffering from long-term illnesses, was frustrated waiting decades for new treatments. Grove sums, “What we have learned from decades of rapid development of information technology is that the key is relentless focus on ‘better, faster, cheaper’ — in everything … The best results are achieved through the cooperative efforts of different disciplines, all aimed at the same objective.”


    Implementing translational medicine aims to improve and accelerate the entire process of converting basic scientific discovery and leading-edge medical research into advances in practical patient care.  It is an interdisciplinary approach to medical innovation that requires procedural awareness and optimizing processes from “bench to bedside.” Participants from scientists, clinical researchers, practicing doctors, to pharmaceutical professionals or medical equipment suppliers, and most of all, patients have a critical stake in translational medicine. The benefits of this new paradigm can improve quality of life or ultimately save the lives of patients.


    To engineers, implementing many aspects of translational medicine appears to be nothing more than standard practice. For instance, creating an environment that promotes communication between interdisciplinary stakeholders in medical innovation is critical to translational medicine. In an interview for the Next Generation Pharmaceutical trade periodical, Francesco Marincola, Chief of the Infectious Disease and Immunogenetics at the National Institute of Health (NIH) posits, “The most difficult problem when trying to test ideas efficiently is the communication between the basic scientists and the clinicians.” Yet, fostering communications, for example, by creating an environment of collocated teams, either physically or virtually, to collaborate and streamline information interchange is a frequently used strategy in high-tech product development.


    Also common in technology delivery processes is the use of rapid project feedback and mandatory post-project reviews to analyze root causes of failure and inefficiencies. These project assessments provide a wealth of learning that improves product delivery and prevents repetition of failures. However, medical practice often lacks similar discipline. For example, glial cell derived neurotrophic factor (GDNF) provided great promise for treating Parkinson’s disease, yet Amgen Corporation, in a contentious decision, halted its Phase II GDNF trial in 2004 over its concerns about safety and lack of efficacy. Numerous stakeholders including researchers and patients like Andy Grove were left deeply frustrated by the reluctance of research sponsors to review results that fell short of clinical implementation but could provide an opportunity to capitalize upon failure with new learning.
