continued from Blue Collar MD


   For doctors who can transition from practicing as independent business-owners to practicing as salaried workers, institutional employment does offer an attractive option with many benefits. As part of a healthcare system, doctors receive a competitive, stable salary and may often receive performance bonuses based, for example, on how many patients the doctor treats.  The hospital administration handles the drudgery of administrative services for the practice — billing, claims processing, payment negotiation with insurers, staff and retirement benefits management, and operation of the electronic health records system. Consequently, doctors can focus specifically on patients and the practice of medicine.

    Yet, despite the promise of better care at lower prices, the benefits are debatable for the patient. Many patients, especially those who seldom need a doctor, may not notice much of a difference. Logistically, many patients still see the same doctor, and now receive the added benefits of coordinated care as patient records seamlessly transfer between specialists and patients conveniently complete lab tests in the same or nearby site.  However, many still fear that corporatized medicine with regional monopolies and business inflexibilities may subject patients to longer waiting lines for care from over-scheduled doctors unreachable after 9 to 5. The attentive, intimate patient-doctor relationship of the past may be replaced by assembly-line medicine from unfamiliar, blue-collar doctors.

    On a larger scale, healthcare policy experts like Paul Ginsburg, President of the non-partisan Center for Studying Health System Change, warns that insurers, patients and employers may face higher prices. In testimony on September 2011 before the U.S. House of Representatives Ways and Means Committee, Subcommittee on Health, Ginsburg stated, “. . . insurers consistently cited higher payment rates to obtain hospital and large physician group participation in health plan networks as a major factor driving higher insurance premiums.” Hospital systems with large groups of doctors can exert greater monopolistic market power on private insurers than doctors working in small private practices. This power can ultimately drive up private insurance prices paid by employers and consumers. Profit motives at large hospitals may give salaried doctors less flexibility in ordering procedures under fixed insurance reimbursements.

    Worse, in the reality of hard economics, healthcare may become less available as many physicians simply opt-out. In 2010, the Physicians Foundation Health Reform and the Decline of Physician Private Practice reported that about three quarters of physicians it surveyed plan to change their practices in the next one to three years, and that most of the changes will have the effect of reducing or eliminating patient access to care, including reducing work hours, closing the practice to new patients, reducing the number of patients seen, seeking alternative employment outside healthcare, or simply retiring. On the other hand, by accepting established doctors in the midst of career transition and by hiring many new doctors, health care systems may actually promote the availability of doctors by giving physicians the opportunity to start or remain in practice when their independent businesses become economically infeasible.

    The private practice provides physicians great autonomy in controlling their careers and the direction of their practices.  The nursing and administrative staff of private practices works for doctors, and the physicians retain influence over their employees who work with them in scheduling their day, delivering care, and managing the total patient experience. However, market consolidation driven by national health policies promotes economics that work against private practice. The inability to compete against healthcare giants for lower costs or talent of newly minted doctors in the labor market will demand a huge risk premium that the physician must pay for autonomy and control.

    Kevin Pho, M.D., a specialist in internal medicine summarizes in his healthcare industry blog, "Large conglomerates of employed physicians will soon become the dominant model of care in the United States. This change is not only coming from the top down, but from the bottom up as well." Still, opportunities remain for entrepreneurial physicians who seek the freedom of their own business. Larger practices remain that can afford professional management and investments to keep pace with information and medical technologies. Single or multispecialty practices may offer a diversified suite of services to create multiple revenue streams, some not requiring the constant attention of the physician.  Newer physician practice models may continue to offer the independent physician financially viable alternatives, such as part-time, locum tenens (temporary), and concierge type boutique practices where the doctor contracts directly with the patient on a monthly fee basis.

    With economic, political and social changes, the future of medicine for doctors and their patients remain uncertain. However, one thing that does remain certain is change and the resilience of doctors and patients to adapt to new models of care.
