Drinking Drugs


Pharmaceuticals in Our Tap Water


Gretchen Holm

Spring 2013


    Have you been feeling irritated, anti-social, and hungry? If so, drinking your tap water may be the problem.


    Every time you take a sip of water from your sink, you may be consuming chemicals that can cause fish to become hyper and aggressive. A team of Swedish researchers led by T. Brodin recently discovered that minute amounts of oxazepam, a common anti-anxiety medication, can cause profound behavioral changes in European perch – and it doesn’t make them more relaxed. In addition to becoming more hyper and aggressive, these fish became less social and even started eating more than their un-drugged counterparts.


    The disturbing connection to your tap water is this: water treatment plants aren’t designed to deal with medication. Once medications enter the water system, they are there to stay. When someone flushes his or her leftover anti-anxiety pills down the toilet, there is nothing to stop trace amounts of the drugs from reaching your sink. These medications can also enter our water system in less conspicuous ways. Sometimes your body does not metabolize all of the medications you take. This means that when you go to the bathroom, there are trace amounts of the medication in your urine – and, again, the drugs are not filtered out by water treatment plants.


    This may all sound too strange to be true, or like the kind of problem that only affects a few small groups of people. After all, it seems like major metropolitan areas must have state-of-the-art water treatment plants that would remove drugs from the water.


    This is not the case. A 2008 investigation by the Associated Press, known by the name PharmaWater, found that at least 1 in 6 Americans has drug-tainted tap water. And these aren’t just the rural Americans with questionable treatment plants. The investigators found that water even in San Francisco was contaminated – and with sex hormones, at that.


    To be fair, the concentrations of drugs in the water are quite low. The minute amounts of the drugs in tap water may offer some reassurance. However, that is where the study about fish comes into play. Remember, the perch showed marked behavioral differences with just two parts per billion of these common medications in their water. That is the equivalent of putting about 1/32nd of a teaspoon – or about two drops – into a large 16’ by 34’ backyard swimming pool. In other words, it does not take much to have a profound behavioral impact on the fish.
