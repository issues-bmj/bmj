The Genetic Causes Behind Distraction


Preethi Kannan

Fall 2011


    Have you ever had trouble concentrating? Constantly feel frustrated or distracted? These are symptoms faced by 3-5% of American children and adults that fight Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder, commonly known as ADHD. Adults and children dealing with this neurological disorder experience problems at work, in school and in their personal lives. In particular, children with ADHD are extremely restless in the classroom.  ADHD has been linked with reading disabilities, schizophrenia, and even bipolar disorder.  The most prominent symptom of ADHD in adults is difficulty planning and organizing. The condition is treated with a combination of behavioral and medicinal therapy, but in some cases the approach fails to yield positive results. For these patients, unsuccessful treatment may be attributed to a certain genetic predisposition to ADHD that cannot be medicated.


    The gene associated with ADHD is referred to as DAT1. The DAT1 gene is responsible for regulating dopamine transmission between brain cells. The gene comes in two forms, DAT1 10 and DAT1 9. People who have two copies of DAT1 10-- one each from his or her mother and father-- are known to be at greater risk for developing ADHD.  Having two alleles of DAT1 10 is correlated with lower levels of dopamine receptors in the brain. This is important because dopamine is most important as a gateway for information, allowing or repelling the influx of information. Those patients that were 10/10 carriers did not perform well in preventing their mind wandering regions from affecting and interplaying with memory performance regions.


    New research conducted by Dr. Nigel Williams of Cardiff University also shows that in addition to possessing DAT1 10 alleles, children with ADHD are more likely to have small segments of their DNA duplicated or missing. Strikingly, there is an overlap between copy number variations found in certain segments of the genome in children with ADHD and children with schizophrenia or autism. The most significant similarities occur on chromosome 16, a chromosome involved in many other serious psychological conditions. Thus, the statement that ADHD is genetic is not beyond the scope of reason.


    How, then, do current drugs treat ADHD? A study released by the National Institute of Health in August 2011 explains. To start, people have one of three dopamine receptors in the brain. One of the possible receptors, however, does not sufficiently reduce the release of the neurotransmitter glutamate. Excess glutamate is a cause of impulsive behavior and other symptoms of ADHD. Psychostimulants, the class of drugs commonly used to treat ADHD, work with dopamine receptors to reduce glutamate release-- but only in those with the proper dopamine receptors. This was the first study to show how this particular genetic difference might translate into varied success with drug treatment.


    What does this mean for the future of patients suffering from Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder? Among children with ADHD, 66.3% are treated with medication. The eventual goal behind the Society of Neuroscience is to narrow down the function behind all the candidate genes. The research conducted in addition by the National Institute of Drug Abuse has concluded that many of the adults and children who have the condition are not genetically capable of being cured by the psychostimulants they are being prescribed. While Dr. Nigel Williams’s lab hopes to find the links between ADHD and other genetic neurobehavioral disorders, it is important for researchers in the future to keep in mind that external factors influence development of ADHD. Hopefully one day, scientists will be able to use this new information to change the lives of many children around the world.
