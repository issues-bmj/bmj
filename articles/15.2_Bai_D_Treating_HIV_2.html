continued from TREating HIV


   Sarkar’s study focused on the genetic engineering of Tre. To make this enzyme similar to Cre, the researchers used directed evolution, a process similar to breeding. They sought a certain mutation of Cre that showed activity for the HIV DNA sequence and continued its replication to produce subsequent mutations until it successfully excised the entire viral sequence from the host DNA. Tre was found to show the most efficient recombination, or ability to cut out the HIV DNA as well as ligate and anneal the host DNA. “It was definitely a challenging task, as directed evolution of the recombinase took over a year and a half, followed by meticulous screening and selection,” says Joachim Hauber, PhD, Heinrich Petter Institute of Virology.

    Tre specifically targets the integrated provirus, a DNA copy of HIV integrated into the host DNA. Specifically, it recognizes the asymmetric, or non-similar, viral sequence within the host cell genome and efficiently excises it, mirroring Cre’s function with the lox P sites. The cut DNA is then released from the cell body and disposed of. With the viral DNA removed, the host cells no longer produce the virus and thus do not rupture or propagate infection.

    This is not the first time the idea of directly targeting the viral DNA has been suggested. Studies published in other journals, such as the 2000 issue of Biochemical Cell Biology, have reported that HIV-1 DNA flanked by lox P sites can be recognized and cut by the Cre enzyme. The idea of using recombinase to excise HIV viral DNA surfaced as early as 1997. Only now, however, has the idea been successfully carried out in a successfully engineered enzyme. The article itself concedes that an efficient and safe method of enzyme delivery will be necessary, but difficult to develop. Still, Tre’s success provides proof that the principle of attacking a retrovirus by cutting it out of its infected genome is a realistic hypothesis.

    Despite the success of the enzyme, researchers are still a long way off from providing a definite cure. In the June 2007 issue of Science, Alan Engelman, PhD, Harvard University, sees the study as promising: “The results raise the possibility that customized enzymes might someday help to eradicate HIV from the body,” but he adds that much more work needs to be done in turning the enzyme into an effective therapy. Even so, Sarkar’s reexamination of recombinase techniques paves the way for new HIV DNA excision technologies.
