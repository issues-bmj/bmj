continued from Medical Putty


   Administration of the putty involves the injection of the gelatin-like material into the fractured area. The putty essentially acts as a support similar to the screws and metal plates currently used. However, it creates an osteoconductive bone structure, which promotes bone cells to fill a gap between the bone ends. This will eventually degrade into harmless byproducts that can be flushed out of the body as the normal bone regenerates. Although the UGA has not released any specific details on the chemical structure of their specific fracture putty, they have explained the general process behind the quick healing of the fracture putty. The main component of the putty is the use of mesenchymal stem cells in the gel like substance. Mesenchymal stem cells are very useful in that they can differentiate into several cell types that include cartilage cells (chondrocytes) and bone cells (osteoblasts), two of the most important cells used in bone healing. The putty uses the stem cells to connect the ligaments together with the new tissue it forms. The stem cells are housed in a polymer that protects them from harm while the mesenchymal cells develop, which allows the ligaments to heal themselves. This process is much quicker than bone grafting because the cells used for recovery are already located in the putty, as mesenchymal stem cells can rapidly transform into a specified bone or cartilage cell.


    The fracture putty developed by the UGA has shown very promising results so far in clinical trials on small animals such as mice, allowing injured mice to be up and running with no observable defects in a period of one to two weeks. Results have shown that the putty can heal an entire goat's leg, revitalizing the bone entirely in a period of only four weeks. The group hopes to move onto testing larger animals to determine if the product they have created is viable.


    Another group, consisting of researchers from Northwestern University and Harvard University, led by Northwestern professor John Peroni, is using Bionanoscaffolds (BNS), which contain fundamental building blocks that can be combined with a structure of polypropylene fumarate (PPF) that can be broken down in the body with exposure to heat or light. Embedded into the PPF structure are biodegradable nonporous silicon structures that offer stability and mechanical reinforcement at a level that is comparable to an undamaged bone. The slow degradation of the PPF and the eventual strengthening of the bone allow the transfer of the mechanical load to the recovering bone, helping its recovery.  According to Peroni, the BNS is also designed to stimulate and promote the self-healing of the bone since it contains Peptide amphiphiles and molecules that attract mesenchymal stem cells to the damaged area in question and force them to differentiate into bone cells, rapidly speeding up the process. The BNS is also envisioned to release antibiotic agents that protect the area in question from infection and to promote regrowth of red blood cells and lymphatic reconstruction. BNS has an extremely soft-clay texture, so that it can hopefully be applied to any area in the body unlike its counterpart, the fracture putty. Using, mathematical models, this group derived several structures that the BNS could be shaped to be. The research phase of this project is also being led by a coalition of research groups and is currently applying this model to rat and rabbit models with different levels of fractures.


    The effect of having a treatment method for complex fractures will have a significant impact on orthopedics as a whole. Imagine, instead of waiting a month with a cast on, the bone itself would be fully functional within a period of one or two days. Broken bones could be treated as a minor wound and be healed quickly and effectively with a high chance of success. In addition to improving the general quality of life, fracture putty could significantly reduce the number of amputees on the battlefield as combat hospitals would have a much quicker and effective method for dealing with complex fractures. The Department of Defense also has high ambitions for fracture putty, as it hopes it will be used as a facial cranial replacement, a common wound found on the battlefield. If the project succeeds, it may increase support for biomedical engineering from the government and validate the use of stem cells in common everyday treatments.
