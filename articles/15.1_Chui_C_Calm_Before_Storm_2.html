continued from The Calm Before the Storm


   Currently, the biggest TB threat comes from the XDR-TB strains, defined in 2006 as MDR-TB that is also resistant to any fluoroquinolone and at least one of the three following injectable drugs: capreomycin, kanamycin, and amikacin. Like MDR strains, XDR strains arise due to the inappropriate use of SLDs in a patient for whom first-line drugs are failing. A March 2006 report by the CDC and the World Health Organization (WHO) documented the presence of XDR-TB in at least seventeen countries. Though not representative, the data showed that in the United States four percent of MDR-TB strains were XDR strains. “The emergence of XDR-TB arises from a collective failure to implement large scale and effective anti-tuberculosis programs in all parts of the world.  We are all responsible for this failure,” says Richard Feachem, DSc, executive director of The Global Fund to Fight AIDS, Tuberculosis, and Malaria.

    Popular belief postulates that TB is not a major threat in the US, but this misconception hampers effective and aggressive action to combat the upsurge of MDR and XDR strains. Scientists face challenges in conveying information about the MDR-TB threat to the general public because “the public is under the impression that the disease is not threatening here [...] it is difficult to make people care about TB unless it directly affects them or their immediate family or friends,” says Lee Riley, MD, professor of Epidemiology and Infectious diseases at the University of California, Berkeley.


    In the US TB incidence decreased steadily at about five percent annually from 1953 until 1985, before reversing and peaking at a prevalence of ten cases per every 100,000 individuals in 1992.  According to the CDC’s Morbidity and Mortality Weekly Report published in March 2006, between 1993 and 2004, a total of 2,689 MDR-TB cases were identified, of which 74 met the criteria for XDR-TB.  Despite an overall decline in MDR-TB incidence in the United States, the proportion of this illness has increased slightly, climbing from 3.9 percent of cases from 1993 to 1996 to 4.5 percent from 2001 to 2004.

    California composes a startlingly high number of cases. In 2003, California reported the most cases in the nation. Over the past decade, the proportion of cases with MDR-TB has remained constant. CDC data indicate that 83 percent of MDR-TB cases in California were foreign-born.  The countries of origins for most immigrants—Mexico, the Philippines, and Vietnam—have demonstrated prevalence of drug resistance that are two to four times that of such strains in the United States.  Despite these alarming figures, most people, such as students, workers, or undocumented immigrants from countries with high TB rates, enter the United States sans any TB screening. As a result, MDR-TB is often unidentified until diagnosis in the United States. Additionally, there can be considerable local variation in TB rates. For example, while TB strain drug resistance declined in the United States as a whole from 1993 to 2002 and the proportion of MDR-TB cases was reduced by about half, the proportions of drug-resistant and MDR-TB in San Diego, California have remained relatively unchanged. If California does not devote more resources to its TB-control programs, we may have an epidemic on our hands.

    “Without urgent and concerted action, in five years time, I would expect the XDR-TB problem to have worsened considerably. The solution lies in two areas. First, existing programs to treat ordinary tuberculosis and MDR-TB have to be both expanded and improved in their quality.  Second, research has to bring us radically better diagnostic tests and drugs applicable to both MDR-TB and XDR-TB. This will take time,” warns Feachem.

    When we list the conquests of modern medicine, our victory over tuberculosis is often high on the list. Unfortunately, the disease is far from being on its last legs. In fact, the exhaustive, perhaps too exhaustive, use of antibiotics has led to the resurrection of “consumption” in an ever more threatening form, that of XDR- TB. This disease has emerged as a worldwide threat, raising concerns of a future epidemic of a virtually untreatable disease.
