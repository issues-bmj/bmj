continued from Accupuncture & Fertility


   With such promising conclusions by experts, more women are turning to acupuncture. But is such faith well-placed? Charlotte Paterson, MD, of the University of Bristol, addressed the placebo effects possible in acupuncture treatment in a study published in the May 2005 issue of the British Medical Journal. In a given treatment session, the acupuncturist will often diagnose the patient before any insertion of the needle. Paterson contends that this examination process creates a therapeutic relationship and is independent from any physical intervention that might take place during the treatment. Her hypothesis is also supported by the statistical research that both real acupuncture and “sham” acupuncture facilitate pregnancy at similar rates.

    In April 2005, Weiping Wang, MD, of the Robert Wood Johnson Medical School at Camden concluded that acupuncture had no direct improvement in pregnancy rates in his studies of embryo transfer. His results showed that there was little or no difference in the pregnancy rates of his subjects. Such conclusions suggest that the effects of acupuncture for infertile women remain ambiguous and require further examination.

    Evan Myers, MD, MPH, of the Department of Obstetrics and Gynecology of Duke Clinical Research Institute in North Carolina, addressed some of the ambiguities in the acupuncture literature in an article published in the October 2005 issue of Fertility and Sterility. He specifically discusses the possible placebo effects from the Westergaard study from Denmark, arguing that the neuroendocrine changes that create a favorable intrauterine environment are not directly associated with the acupuncture treatment but instead relate to their dependence on the timing relevant to the implantation. He also argues that the lack of knowledge of the actual mechanism of acupuncture leads to complexities in evaluating outcomes. Myers suggests future studies use alternative controls such as the relatively new placebo needles – acupuncture needles with altered tips – and calls for a standardization of all acupuncture methods.

    As alternative treatment therapies are gaining a larger-than-ever foothold in the sphere of allopathic medicine, it will become increasingly important to validate such remedies on a scientific basis. The jury is still out on whether acupuncture will withstand the barrage of scientific inquiry that it must face, and this uncertainty is likely to leave millions of infertile women without the answers they need.
