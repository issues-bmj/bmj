Art of War


Mankind's Newest Weapons Against Cancer and Drug-Resistant Bacteria


Nicole Lee

Spring 2013


    We are engaged in an arms race. Failure to properly equip ourselves with the best of the best--the weapons which can infiltrate and then devastate with an almost elegant finesse--will result in no reprimands, no warnings, and only death.  Our strategy will be to annihilate our opponent, striking with a combination of attacks to the point where retaliation is not only impossible, but unthinkable.  This horrific warfare I speak of takes place within our own bodies. Our opponents are formidable: cancer and drug-resistant bacteria. However, researchers at Rice University have developed the newest guns in our arsenal: a potent cocktail of natural toxins, and man-made compounds designed to overcome the bacteria's ability to rapidly evolve drug resistance.  It's time to get ready, aim, and fire.


    Both capable of mounting multifaceted defenses against traditional drug treatments, cancer and drug-resistant bacteria (also dramatically referred to as superbugs) are the latest scourges to mankind.  However, researchers have found the One-Two Punch Strategy to be a viable method for overcoming these diseases.  The strategy itself is quite simple: distract the opponent with an attack, then immediately follow up, surprising and destroying it with a knockout blow.  Applying this at the microbial level, Professor Onuchi of Rice University says that “by combining drugs, particularly those that place stress on different parts of the cell, we expect it will be possible to knock out either cancer cells of bacteria while simultaneously inhibiting their ability to becoming drug-resistant.”


    Onuchic and his colleagues, Professors Jennings and Ben-Jacob reached this insight through multiple studies of antimicrobial peptides (AMPs), which are corkscrew-shaped amino acid chains produced by various animals to kill Gram-negative bacteria.  Since AMPs are naturally produced by the animal, they possess the benefit of leaving the body’s own cells unscathed, while they tear through the double membranes of the Gram-negative bacteria.


    Cancer researchers have already found that AMPs can be adapted for use against cancer cells. By modifying AMPs with special marker molecules, scientists have enabled them to get across the cancer cell membrane.  Once inside the cell, the AMPs selectively target and attack the mitochondria (which has a double membrane similar to that of Gram-negative bacteria, according to Professor Ben-Jacob).  However, there still remains the problem of cancer’s ability to rapidly evolve drug resistance.  This issue partially stems from the fact that AMP’s aren’t exactly rare, and already, there are organisms with the necessary mutations to fend them off.
