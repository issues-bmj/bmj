A Hidden Monster: Antibiotics May Lead to Obesity


Jaclyn Wright

Fall 2011


    Researchers at New York University Langone Medical Center have found another reason for doctors to think twice before prescribing antibiotics, and it has nothing to do with resistant strains of bacteria. Early findings suggest that the overuse of antibiotics is permanently altering our internal bacterial composition. Bacteria are a vital part of human survival, helping with digestion and fighting off other harmful bacteria. Losing these bacteria, therefore, could have detrimental effects on one’s health, according to the chair of the department of medicine at NYU, Martin Blaser, MD.  Dr. Blaser published his research in Nature in August of 2011.


    Removing one particular bacterium, Helicobacter pylori, from the gut may contribute to the increase in obesity and some esophageal diseases. This bacterium resides in the stomach and plays an important role in energy balance by regulating the production of two hormones, ghrelin and leptin. Ghrelin stimulates the appetite, while leptin suppresses it.


    At the beginning of the 20th century, H. pylori was the most prominent bacterium in the human stomach. Now less than 6% of children in the United States, Sweden, and Germany carry it. This drop in the presence of H. pylori, Dr. Blaser argues, could be a result of the over-prescription of antibiotics like amoxicillin for common illnesses such as ear infections. For example, amoxicillin may successfully kill the bacteria causing the ear infection, but according to Dr. Blaser’s research, it also removes all of the body’s H. pylori in 20-50% of cases. Dr. Blaser also notes that as the percentage of H. pylori present in the body has decreased over the past century, the occurrence of gastroesophageal reflux and esophageal cancer has increased.


    Because the bacterium regulates the hormones that control when people eat and when they stop, the loss of H. pylori could be a contributing factor in the rise in obesity over the past century. In addition to weight gain and esophageal diseases associated with eradication of H.pylori, early results from Dr. Blaser’s studies suggest that people without these bacteria are also more likely to develop asthma, hay fever, or skin allergies in childhood. Dr. Blaser notes that the presence of H. pylori is related to the risk of peptic ulcers and stomach cancer, but argues that if the bacteria did not benefit humans in any way then it would not be present in our microbiomes.


    One way to combat problems associated with a decrease in microbiome diversity is to create special probiotics. These microorganisms can be introduced into the body to replace bacteria like H. pylori lost after a course of antibiotics. Another option identified by Dr. Blaser would be to develop more narrow-spectrum antibiotics; in other words, researchers could try to create antibiotics that would only destroy one particular bacterium. While this would prevent good bacteria from being destroyed unnecessarily, it would also place a greater burden on doctors to make diagnoses that are more precise.


    It is important to remember that all of Dr. Blaser’s claims are based on preliminary findings, and need to be researched far more before any conclusions can be made. However, Dr. Blaser warns, “If antibiotics do cause long-term physiological changes, we may not be able to wait.”
