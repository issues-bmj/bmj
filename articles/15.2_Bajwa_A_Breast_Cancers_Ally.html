Breast Cancer's Unlikely Ally


Increased estrogen renders the immune system ineffective

Amrita Bajwa

Spring 2008


    Estrogen could be the primary determinant of enhanced growth and motility of breast cancer cells. Originally reported by David Shapiro, PhD, University of Illinois, and other collaborators in the January 2007 issue of Oncogene, this recent finding has created waves of interest in the scientific community. Furthermore, this study implicates estrogen in blocking the body’s immune response to cancer cells by upregulating the production of an inhibitor that deters immune cells from effectively killing tumor cells.

    The female sex hormone estrogen primarily originates from developing follicles in the ovaries, promoting the development of secondary sexual characteristics and regulating the menstrual cycle. Shapiro ’s study demonstrates that when estrogen binds with its cognate receptor, the complex begins to promote the expression of a gene encoding a particular enzyme inhibitor, proteinase inhibitor 9 (PI-9). PI-9 prevents natural killer cells from releasing cytoplasmic enzymes called granzymes into breast cancer cells, thus allowing the cancer cells to evade elimination by the immune system.

    Shapiro’s research began as an endeavor to understand the interplay between estrogen receptor activation and gene transcription. After identifying PI-9 as an estrogen-regulated gene, Shapiro and his colleagues discovered that estrogen actually blocks immunosurveillance of breast cancer cells. Increasing the production of PI-9 and SerpinB9, a closely related inhibitor, disrupts tumor cell destruction by NK92 natural killer cells. In Shapiro’s study, high levels of estrogen receptors allowed tamoxifen (TAM)-related signaling to have a synergistic effect with PI-9 in preventing the destruction of tumor cells by NK-92 cells. On the other hand, numerous experiments involving the activation of estrogen receptors have shown that an increase of PI-9 is also related to the production of TAM, an estrogen receptor modulator currently used for treating early stages of breast cancer. Thus, different levels of TAM can produce varying effects with elevated levels of estrogen receptor, which ultimately prevents destruction of cancer cells.

    Lillie Shockney, RN, MAS, administrative director of the Breast Cancer Center at Johns Hopkins University, considers the results of Shapiro’s studies “fascinating and actually supportive of some other hypotheses that have been made in the past.” Ultimately, she notes, “The results of this study and others will trigger the need to re-evaluate hormones and their use.”
