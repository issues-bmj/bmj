The Certainty of Memory is Questionable in the Criminal Justice System


Jasmine Elmiari

Spring 2012


    Witness testimony in judicial court has long been considered an integral part of what the ultimate verdict of the court is upon the defendants, but scientists continue to question how much a person can really remember factually. This question has great implications for the criminal justice system, and suggests that adjustments need to be made in order to account for the ambiguity of memory.


    In recent years with the development of DNA identification methods in crime investigations, it had been found that 75% of convictions proven wrong by DNA were due to faulty witness testimony. As recently as November 2011, the United States Supreme Court began the Perry v. New Hampshire case involving a man who was convicted of theft after being accused by a woman who saw him at a distance in the obscurity of night. Even in August of the same year, the New Jersey State Supreme Court formed new guidelines to assuage the faults of witness testimony during the appeal of Larry Henderson, a man who was chosen from a photo lineup and later convicted of weapons possession and manslaughter in 2003.


    Witness accounts are unreliable due to the selectivity of memory in the brain. The human brain has evolved to only remember a rough outline of past events because it does not need to have a detailed memory in order to promote the survival of humankind according to Harvard psychology professor Daniel Schacter. In any given situation, there is often a lot of information for humans to process, so humans are more likely to focus on a certain event at any given time. Consequently, it is very likely that the witness to the crime may not have been paying attention to the event in question, thus skewing their memory of the event, as according to Barbara Tversky, a psychology professor at the Teacher’s College in Columbia University. In the November New York Times article The Certainty of Memory Has Its Day in Court by Laura Beil, Tversky says that the witness would have trouble remembering the details well due to outside factors such as their emotions during the event, the social pressures incurred by the police and even additional information that the brain used to fill in what the person did not remember that might not be true.


    Memory can also be affected by what we were concentrating on during an event. In a study from Harvard, when asked to focus on how many times the white team passed a basketball, subjects were so focused on the task that they did not even notice a woman walking by in a black gorilla suit. More recently, the University of London did a study presented to the Psychonomic Society that formed an audio test similar to the Harvard study. Participants were asked to listen to a 69 second recording of two men and two women talking and preparing for a party while focusing on what the women said. Nearly all of the participants did not hear a third man saying “I’m a gorilla” for 19 seconds in the middle. Visual distraction is not the only factor that can affect memory.


    Most importantly, fear combined with this selective attention can cause people to become more affirmed in their memory; they may have been focusing on the weapon in their fear, not paying attention to much else, but that fear could influence them to have paid much attention to that source of fear. As such, the witness would be confident about what happened that was in the scope of their concentration, but then they may apply that confidence to other aspects of the event on which they were not as focused.


    Researchers are trying to find ways to excise such falsities from witness comments, especially in suspect line-ups, a source of many inconsistencies in witness accounts. Gary Wells, a psychology professor at Iowa State University, released a study which showed that the likelihood of choosing the wrong person from a police lineup decreased from 18% if suspects shown side-by-side to 12% if shown sequentially. It is believed that the sequential method limits the amount of people the suspects can be compared to; in the line-up, there is likely to be someone who looks like the suspect more than the others, but is still not the suspect in side-by-side line-ups. Dr. Wells suggests that police make sure that the witnesses know that the criminal is not necessarily in the group and having the line-ups done by a person who does not know who the suspect is. The authorities may be asking the wrong questions of the witnesses, telling them to choose the criminal or say exactly what happened when their minds may not be able to do exactly as they are told. If measures are made to counter the ambiguity of memory in witnesses, the criminal justice system could be more accurate and improved overall.
