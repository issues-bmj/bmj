continued from Sex, Drugs, and ... Surgery?


   The implications of this study are far-reaching and thought-provoking. Addiction to fentanyl is not something to be taken lightly. Nearly a hundred times more potent than morphine, fentanyl causes euphoria and impaired concentration upon initial use. After several uses, the side-eﬀects can become life-threatening as the cardiovascular and pulmonary systems become compromised. As demonstrated in a 1990 study conducted by a team of anesthesiologists at the Brooke Army Medical Center at Fort Sam in Houston, Texas, opioid abuse can lead to death in almost 18 percent of addicts. Joe Stirt, MD, an anesthesiologist at Richmond Community Hospital, explains, “In at least two of my former residents—one from when I was on the faculty at UCLA, and the other from my days at the University of Virginia—addiction led to their deaths. I know personally of other anesthesiologists whose dead bodies were discovered in call rooms or bathrooms in the hospital with narcotics, needles, and syringes scattered in the vicinity.” Patients may now have to worry about not only the outcome of their procedure, but also the possibility that their surgeon is an opiate addict.

    Across the country, physician-help groups have begun to address this issue of patient safety as well as the concern for physician conﬁdentiality. McAuliﬀe notes that, overall, more than 90 percent of physicians who get help recover from addiction. However, the majority of treatment centers have yet to come across physicians who have become addicted by second-hand exposure. Kara Miller, the Assistant Clinical Director of Meditox Opiate Recovery Program, remarks, “We have seen thousands of cases of addiction to opiates, especially fentanyl, but never have I heard of any medical professionals such as surgeons or anesthesiologist becoming addicted to an opiate through second-hand exposure.”

    The lack of physicians seeking help for second-hand exposure to opiates raises several important issues. Are physicians simply refusing to acknowledge that a serious problem is developing, or is second-hand exposure an anomalous route of addiction? While more studies are sure to investigate this matter further, one thing is clear: opiates and other potentially hazardous substances need to be removed from the air of operating rooms more eﬃciently. After all, for high-risk procedures such as surgery, nothing can be allowed to compromise the operator’s precision, judgment, and concentration.
