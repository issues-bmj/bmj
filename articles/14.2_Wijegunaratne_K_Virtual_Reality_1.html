Virtual Reality Therapy


Fighting against fear and trauma

Kanishka Wijegunaratne

Spring 2007


    Although fear is an adaptive response to danger, serious problems can arise when it becomes so exaggerated and unreasonable that it interferes with one’s daily life. But today, a form of treatment known as Virtual Reality Therapy (VRT) can help a patient combat his phobia through exposure to a virtual environment containing the feared stimulus. By using a platform and headset, a patient is immersed into a computer-generated 3D environment that incorporates body tracking devices and live digital video. Controlled by the therapist, the sensory aspects of the virtual environment—auditory, visual, and tactile—are delivered to the individual through the head-mounted display. By re-enacting the feared situation, the phobia can be confronted and hopefully conquered within the safe confines of virtual reality.

    Dean McKay, PhD, and his research group at Fordham University’s department of psychology have been very successful in using current VRT protocols to treat aviophobia, the fear of flying. In a typical VRT session, an aviophobic patient can gaze out of the window of a videotaped plane during take-off, making VRT a practical and economical procedure. The set environment is replete with realistic sights, sounds, and vibrations. McKay is currently investigating methods to improve the effectiveness of VRT for glossophobia, the fear of public speaking, and astraphobia, the fear of thunder and lightning. The revolutionizing step, however, “will [be] in the realm of realistic video resolution,” McKay believes.

    Researchers are not only striving to improve VRT technology but are also keen on formulating novel applications for it. Judith Cukor, PhD, of the Program for Anxiety and Traumatic Stress Disorders at Weill Cornell Medical College, is currently conducting clinical research studies to determine the effectiveness of VRT in the treatment of post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD). Cukor states that the previous standard treatment for PTSD was imaginal exposure therapy, in which the patient describes his experience repeatedly while attempting to “re-experience” it and feel the emotions from that day. Unfortunately, many patients are reluctant or incapable of self-generating these painful, emotional feelings. Additionally, Cukor notes that “one of the symptoms of PTSD is numbing oneself, so numb patients may be able to recite their experiences without generating any of the emotions that they felt on that day.” VRT resolves this issue by immersing a patient in realistic environments, thereby allowing these emotions to resurface without forcing the patients to purposefully recall traumatic experiences.

    Currently, Cukor’s research group is focusing on assessing the effectiveness of treating patients with PTSD stemming from the September 11th attacks and the ongoing war in Iraq. She emphasizes that previous ventures into this realm have been effective. Most notably, JoAnn Difede, PhD, at the department of psychology of New York-Presbyterian Hospital/Weill Cornell Medical College, in collaboration with Hunter Hoffman, PhD, at the department of psychology of the University of Washington, have already developed a virtual environment called the “World Trade Center” to help treat September 11th-related PTSD. As described in a case report published by Difede in the November 2002 issue of CyberPsychology & Behavior, this virtual environment was used to treat a patient who was present at the World Trade Center attacks and suffered flashbacks, sleep problems, hypervigilance, anger, and irritability. After putting on the head-mount and entering the virtual world of lower Manhattan, the patient saw the twin towers and cried for the first time. The patient began with manageable situations, such as a plane flying past the World Trade Center, and ended with gruesomely realistic ones such as images of people jumping from the burning buildings and the sounds of sirens and screams. Cukor emphasizes the importance of replaying the events in a gradual, therapeutic manner so that the patient does not become re-traumatized. Only after habituating to one scene can a patient move on to a more traumatic one. This particular case report concluded that the patient showed a large and stable reduction in her PTSD symptoms and depression after successfully completing VRT treatment.
