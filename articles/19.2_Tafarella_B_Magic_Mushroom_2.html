continued from Magic Mushroom


   A second study led again by Robin L. Carhart-Harris followed very similar methods to show to where the blood flowed. Like the previous study, fMRI imaging showed that there was decreased blood flow to the mPFC after being injected with psilocybin, but this study also showed there was increased blood flow to areas of the brain that function in memory vividness and visual imagery. These areas of the brain are the bilateral auditory cortex, somatosensory cortex, superior parietal cortex, left visual association regions and the occipital pole. The activation of these memory and visual centers explains why subjects who were administered psilocybin reported a greater increase of feelings of euphoria and increased vividness when asked to recall positive memories compared to subjects who were given a placebo.  The study also showed a positive correlation between the intensity of subjective experience or vividness during their psilocybin trial and the reported well-being of subjects during follow ups. During follow ups, reported well-being was much higher for those who took psilocybin versus those who took the placebo and was higher for those who reported more vivid memory recall during the trial. Researchers believe that these results lend strong evidence for the use of psilocybin in conjunction with positive memory recall tasks for treating depression. In one of these therapy sessions, one may be administered psilocybin and then be asked to recall special and positive memories in order to conjure up tangible, strong, emotional responses that have a lasting effect on their mood.


    Side-effects of psilocybin include dose-dependent headaches due to the changing constriction of blood flow in the brain. Researcher Erich Studerus at University Hospital of Psychiatry, Zurich, Switzerland completed a study in October of 2011 that assessed risk factors affecting patient responses to psilocybin. The study showed that many factors and personal traits including drug experience, emotional excitability, experimental setting, and mental state prior to injection have an effect on the reaction and responses to psilocybin. These factors should be taken into account when determining if psilocybin is a proper method for treating depression in a particular patient. By treating with psilocybin, people living with depression may increase positive emotional perceptions that their connector hubs normally work to suppress.
