Mind Over Matter


Sam Greene

Fall 2013


    Movement, like running or playing sports, is a highly measurable activity. In soccer, we can count exactly how many times any given player touched the ball or scored a goal to derive a rough idea of how productive any given player was in a given match. If a player frequently touched the ball but never scored, we might think he or she was less productive in a game than a player who touched the ball less but scored more. Likewise, in neuroscience, we can count exactly how much brain activity is occurring in any given part of the cortex and correlate this activity with a cognitive test to  get a rough idea of the cortices’ productivity. As you might expect, with practice, repetition, and a bit of determination, a soccer player can become more productive (score more goals), while touching the ball less. How is this possible? Individual soccer players can actively use their teammates in an attack, passing the ball more and dribbling less, for example, to move the ball up the field and ultimately score goals.  Neuroscientists at the Carmena Lab in the Electrical Engineering and Computer Science Department at the University of California, Berkeley  are investigating this concept of neural recruitment in people suffering from tetraplegia, a severe neurological and corticospinal disorder causing a loss of mobility and basic motor control. Leveraging the power of Brain-Machine-Interfaces (BMIs) and Closed-Loop Decoder-Adaptations (CLDAs), researchers are attempting to make the brain more effective at completing given motor control tasks.


    Professor of Electrical Engineering Jose Carmena and MIT Neuroscience PhD Simon Overduin are taking the lead in developing BMIs, or small, implantable computers connected to the brain via an electrode.


    “Traditional BMIs including the ones in our lab are strictly focused on reading out, or decoding information, from the brain. They are not concerned, yet, with putting information into the brain, or replacing information that would come in through the senses.  There are a couple of labs out there that are working in this direction,” Overduin said.


    In an interview with Overduin, when asked if this is the future of neuroprosthetic devices he replied with unwavering resolve; yes.


    “Designing better brain machine interfaces, through understanding what the brain is planning during movement, and efficiently translating those decoded intentions into control signals for a neural prosthetic device or simulation” is the next step, Overduin suggests.


    Some neuroprosthetic device companies like BrainGate have harnessed the power of brain machine interfaces, effectively enabling subjects to regain motor control by interpreting signals sent from their brains while they imagine and think about using their limbs. In a human trial, funded by the National Institute of Health in 2012, BrainGate released a video on YouTube displaying a person with tetraplegia using a robotic arm to execute a complex reach-and-grasp motion to pick up a bottle all with her imagination.


    Among the many research projects currently in the works, developing smarter CLDAs to increase the voluntary control of neural activity in the brains of subjects suffering from tetraplegia is a primary one. The basic idea behind traditional CLDAs and BMIs is that a computer is implanted in the brain and connected to a part of the motor cortex via an electrode. In a given motor task, the brain sends impulses to the computer, which runs a series of algorithms governed by four vectors, representing the kinematic state of the actuators, or limbs. The computer first receives two different electrical impulses from the brain at two different times.  It then derives an algorithm modeling the two electrical signals and relating them by comparing the vectors associated with the signals and produces a prior estimate, or an initial estimate. The prior or initial estimate is then used to determine the values of the vectors associated with the intended movement projected by the brain.  Finally, the computer predicts the next movement based on the initial estimate to form the posterior estimate, the final estimate, effectively updating the signal or impulse to be better and more accurate in respect to the intended movement. After updating the signal or impulse, the computer closes the loop by sending the updated signal or impulse, via precise and calculated intracranial microstimulation (ICMS). This closing step of the loop via ICMS is the mechanism defining the ‘closed’ part of the Closed Loop Decoder Adaptation. Ultimately, Carmena and Overduin are investigating whether subjects will be able to use this closed loop system to generate more accurate motor signals voluntarily effectively using the the power of their minds.


    “What is microstimulation? It’s shocking, if you will, the tissue right around the electrodes.  The stimuli are calibrated to be near the voltage threshold required to evoke a movement. ICMS is intracortical microstimulation.  It’s completely painless, because there are no pain receptors in the brain. If applied in the motor cortex, you can evoke a movement. If you apply it in the visual cortex, you can evoke visual hallucinations,” Overduin explains.
