continued from A Tat to Beat That


   Despite the benefits of tattoo vaccines that Müller has observed in his research, he acknowledges the flaws in this method of vaccination. Pain upon tattoo administration is a critical factor affecting the success of the vaccination, as well as concerns about how the vaccine could possibly alter the human body. “Safety issues regarding the use of DNA that potentially can integrate into the host chromosomes have to be addressed first,” he says.

    When asked about the future of tattoo vaccination, Müller replies, “I don’t believe it will be available to the general population but as DNA vaccinations have great advantages, application by tattooing might be considered for special treatments such as tumor therapy, and it might help us to respond quickly to emerging diseases.” Specialized devices for administering the vaccine must still be created before this vaccine is administered to the human body, so for now, Müller tests this method on mice.

    Although this new medical advancement may make great leaps in public health, vaccination through tattooing may not be readily accepted by society. Müller remarked on the future of this vaccination method stating that “the acceptance rate may not be very great. I see, therefore, DNA vaccination via tattooing mostly for the treatment and prevention of serious diseases where there is no adequate alternative.” For those who look upon tattoos with fear and disdain, even a potential health benefit may not be enough to encourage them to try it. Nevertheless, there are still people who push forward in the hopes of improving medical technology.

    Müller clearly emphasizes the importance of vaccine research, stating “I think it is clear that research on vaccination was a great success. We now take it for granted that we can protect ourselves against polio and that smallpox is no longer a threat. It is not that long ago that people were facing the consequences of both diseases every day by seeing the victims all around.” With his research in tattoo vaccination, Müller hopes to contribute to vaccine history and improve the health conditions of today.
