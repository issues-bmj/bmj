The Spectrum of Autism


A survey of the latest studies

Elena Radicati

Spring 2008


    Think of some of the most basic movements, emotions, or interactions in your life – everything from telling your mother you love her to making eye contact with someone during a conversation. Now imagine only being able to perform these simple gestures with extreme difficulty and practice, if at all. Unfortunately, for millions of those with autism, this mysterious condition profoundly shapes their social development, or lack thereof.

    Three recent research studies show that autism is linked to both genetic and environmental factors. Although the authors have concluded that more research is necessary to fully support their results, the initial findings are still influential in showing that autism is most likely not just a disorder with a single cause, but rather, a group of disorders of varying and even multiple origins.

    Autism is one of a group of five disorders, known as the autism spectrum disorders (ASD). It primarily impairs communication and social skills and affects approximately one in 150 children in the United States, with four times as many males affected as females, according to Autism Speaks, the world’s largest autism advocacy group. Autism is mainly diagnosed in children through observation of impaired social interactions and communication, repetitive behaviors such as body rocking or self-injury, atypical eating patterns, and “splinter skills,” which are uniquely specialized abilities such as quick mental calculations or artistic talent.

    Since autism is a spectrum disorder, no single behavior or symptom can be attributed to it. Although there is currently no cure and no clear cause, autism research currently receives less than five percent of the funding that less prevalent childhood diseases receive, according to Autism Speaks. Now, however, with increasing incidence and awareness of autism, more research is emerging in the hopes of answering questions about the disorder.

    One attempt to understand autism is the Autism Genome Project (AGP), a study initiated by the National Alliance for Autism Research and Autism Speaks. It is the largest study of its kind to determine the genes associated with autism by pooling genetic samples from more than 50 institutions in 19 countries. Beginning in 2004 by scanning the genomes of approximately 1,200 families with more than one autistic child, the first phase was completed in February 2007.

    Andy Shih, PhD, of Autism Speaks, explains why autism research needs this kind of sample size. “About a decade ago, there were various geneticists working on autism research, but the problem is that autism turned out to be a lot more complex than we imagined,” Shih says. “There were numerous reports with small sample sizes, very little consistency, and analysis was difficult. This was around the time of the Human Genome Project, so the consensus of leading geneticists was that they needed a very large, standardized sample set.”

    The AGP scanned the sample genomes looking for variations that may be linked to autism, especially the copy number variants (CNVs), or the number of copies of a particular gene in one’s genome. Every person’s genome contains thousands of CNVs, most of which are benign and contribute to individual differences such as height and weight. However, the AGP scan revealed that many of the genetic abnormalities of those with autism occurred only in particular genes. According to Shih, many of the genes found were affiliated with synaptic junctions, specifically those regulating excitatory or inhibitory responses. This is consistent with many common behavioral traits characteristic of autism such as repetitive speech or extreme sensitivity to noise or touch.
