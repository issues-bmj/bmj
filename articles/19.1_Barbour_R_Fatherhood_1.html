Fatherhood and Testosterone


Rachel Barbour

Fall 2011


    A recent study conducted by researchers affiliated with Northwestern University and the University of San Carlos has revealed a surprising twist in the biology of humans: fatherhood leads to a dramatic lowering of men’s testosterone levels. Where society seems to have designated testosterone as the essence of masculinity, this finding may be alarming to the average man -- does fatherhood also mark the loss masculinity?

    The scientific community has long established a connection between fatherhood and testosterone levels, but the exact nature of the relationship has remained unclear. Previously conducted studies have shown that fathers’ testosterone levels measure lower than those of men without children, leading to the belief that fatherhood curbs testosterone levels so as to reorient a father’s concern away from securing a mate and toward the caretaking of their pre-existing family. However, these studies only measured men’s testosterone levels at a single point in time. The data could thus neither prove this hypothesis nor rule out the possibility that it is simply that men with lower testosterone levels are those who are more likely to become fathers in the first place. 

    The recent longitudinal study published in September 2011 in Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences is the first to show how men’s testosterone levels in relation to fatherhood change over a prolonged period of time. Testosterone levels were measured through saliva samples in 624 single 21-year-olds in 2005 and again in 2009, and findings revealed testosterone levels to fluctuate as these men entered committed relationships and had children. Contrary to a previous hypothesis, it was in fact the men with initially higher levels of testosterone who were more likely to become fathers. Upon entering fatherhood, testosterone levels experienced an average drop of 26% in morning measurements and a drop of 34% in the evening. The study also found that men who spent at least three hours a day actively caring for their children had even lower testosterone levels than those who interacted with their children less often.

    Multiple implications stem from these results. Firstly, understanding the fluctuation of testosterone levels gives insight into the evolutionary role of the male sex. The changes indicate that a man’s hormone levels correlate to the different roles he performs at different points in his life: testosterone levels are elevated while a man is in search of a mate, and drop dramatically when his responsibility becomes the care and protection of his family. The study left open the possibility that a man’s testosterone levels are not permanently altered and that they may rise again as his children grow up and become less dependent upon paternal care.
