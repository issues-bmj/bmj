Ozone


Friend or foe?

Sweta Bodepudi

Spring 2008


    Air is a vital commodity that we take for granted. But would you breathe in a little less if you knew that the air around you could potentially disarm your immune system?

    Research by John Hollingsworth, MD, Duke University, published in the October 2007 issue of Journal of Immunology, reveals that the air we breathe could shut down the primary immune response in our lungs and body. Whereas atmospheric ozone prevents damaging ultraviolet light from reaching the Earth’s surface, ground-level ozone may harm the respiratory and immune systems of animals.

    Ozone is composed of three oxygen atoms, making it much less stable than the diatomic form of oxygen that is crucial to our survival. This characteristic instability is a result of ozone’s strong oxidizing tendencies. The highest levels of ozone are in the stratosphere, in the region known as the ozone layer, which protects living species by filtering out shorter wavelengths of ultraviolet light from the sun. On the other hand, ground-level ozone, the layer from which we breathe, was classified as a pollutant by the World Health Organization in January 2003. Unlike most pollutants, it is not emitted directly from industrial operations or car exhaust but is created by the reaction between sunlight and air containing hydrocarbons produced from smog.

    Ozone has been proven to exact numerous damaging effects on the body according to Alexander Leaf, MD, Columbia University. It breaks down diatomic oxygen to yield free radicals, which are highly unstable and capable of reacting with and damaging many organic molecules, setting the foundations for cancer development. Ozone also converts cholesterol in the bloodstream to plaque, which causes hardening and narrowing of the arteries. Consequently, recent scientific interest has stemmed from the human immune response to ozone.

    As policymakers debate over safety levels of ozone in the air, studies in mice reveal that the inhaled pollutant impairs the body’s first line of defense, the innate immune response, rendering it more susceptible to subsequent foreign invaders such as bacteria. Hollingsworth’s team found that ozone exposure appears to augment apoptosis, or programmed cell death, in critical immune system cells, interfering with their ability to engulf foreign invaders.

    One of the key cells in the nonspecific defense system is the macrophage, which engulfs and digests substances the body flags as non-self. Hollingsworth’s study demonstrated that ozone exposure in a mouse model reduced the number of macrophages present in the lungs after being introduced to an inhaled bacterial toxin. Ozone exposure had stimulated these cells to undergo premature apoptosis. Without ozone interference, the macrophages would normally function as scavenger and secretory cells and would attempt to eliminate the inhaled toxin.

    “Small amounts of inhaled foreign material can be relatively harmless, since they stimulate an appropriate innate immune response that protects the lungs,” says Hollingsworth. “However, it appears that ozone causes the innate immune system to overreact, killing key immune system cells.”

    Scientists are continuing to conduct further studies on the mechanisms behind ozone’s ability to induce apoptosis in immune cells. In the meantime, take into consideration that the fresh air we breathe everyday might not be as fresh as we think.
