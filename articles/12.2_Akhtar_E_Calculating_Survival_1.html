Calculating the Survival Rate


A new machine may determine who gets treatment first in the ICU

Ehsaan Akhtar

Spring 2005

   
    In order to more effectively use Intensive Care Unit (ICU) resources, hospitals all around the country are slowly beginning to introduce a newly developed machine that uses a series of calculations to provide a scoring system that predicts patients’ chances of survival. Critics—patient, doctor, or otherwise— argue that, when the stakes are as high as human life, making the right call in terms of deciding treatment should be about anything but keeping score.

    Acute Physiological and Chronic Health Evaluation III (APACHE III), the most widespread of these systems, takes into consideration a number of factors: the patient’s diagnosis and abnormalities upon being admitted to the ICU, age, and pre-existing medical conditions. By making use of a number of complicated algorithms that place different weights on these various factors, the computer assigns a score to each patient, indicating his predicted mortality rate.

    William A. Knaus, M.D., the designer of APACHE III, has been working on these systems since 1978. When he created APACHE, “it was very clear [that] the amount of information that came into the ICU on a daily basis was far more than any individual could organize and interpret correctly.” In response to this issue, physicians created the first APACHE system, which had a doctor assign certain symptoms of the patient on a mathematical scale. APACHE III, the most recent of this series of systems, is the first to become fully digitalized, measuring the factors entered as inputs and releasing its own output.

    The algorithms used to create this output are fine-tuned by being tested against an information database that constitutes the heart of the machine’s ability to make predictions. Millions of past patients’ diagnoses and symptoms coupled with their survival rates before and after ICU discharge are stored in the memory of APACHE III. The computer compares the conditions of current patients against this database in order to make its predictions.

    While numerous studies have been released praising the accuracy of the machine’s work, several have also been released that express that the tool must be used with caution. A study done by the Mayo Clinic, a non-profit organization based in Rochester, Minnesota, found that the computer machine was five times more pessimistic regarding outcomes for a group of patients than the observed outcomes. Furthermore, the study found that the physician’s personal consideration of the patient’s health was significantly more accurate than any outcome predicted by APACHE III.

    Patient attitudes reflect a similar distrust for these machines. Patients do not want to put their lives in the hands of a machine and prefer the compassion and humanity that goes with a doctor’s diagnosis. Zain Yahya, a college student who has visited the emergency room twice, expresses a similar sentiment: “They take in other factors that can’t be put in [a machine] statistically, for example, the will of the patient. They can get a sense of the attitude of the patient and other immeasurable qualities… part of the stuff that makes every patient’s situation truly unique, whereas a machine could never do that.”

    This hesitance towards accepting the machine is due largely to the nature of APACHE’s purpose: it predicts mortality rates and can be used to determine how ICU resources are allocated. Both these aspects involve the higher levels of medical care that focus on the most critical patients admitted to hospitals. Thus, APACHE occupies one of the most critical jobs of patient care, one where the public expects the margin of error to be as small as possible.
