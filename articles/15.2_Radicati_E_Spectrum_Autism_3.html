continued from The Spectrum of Autism


   The third aspect of autism research focuses solely on environmental effects. One study published in Environmental Health Perspectives on July 2007 found a link between pregnant women who lived in close proximity to pesticide-sprayed fields in the California Central Valley and autism spectrum disorders in their children. E.M. Roberts, MD, California Department of Health Services, found an association between autism and the two most commonly used pesticides, dicofol and endosulfan, during his research from 1996 to 1998.

    “Many pesticides in current use are neurological toxins that may affect development via other mechanisms, and many, including both scientists and the lay community, have voiced concern that substances in the environment could contribute to autism,” Roberts says of his interest in researching this topic.

    Roberts’ research examined the pesticide-autism connection through every combination of a few key factors: the types of pesticides used, the mother’s physical distance from the site of spraying, and her gestation period at the time of possible exposure. The main pregnancy time periods examined were those linked to embryonic central nervous system development and neural tube development – from one to eight weeks. Through these preliminary examinations, organochlorines like dicofol and endosulfan emerged as potential linking factors between pesticide use and ASD.

    Since this was the first project of its kind and its results are preliminary, Roberts’ findings are still debatable. The importance of this research is not that it shows a definite causality between pesticides and autism, but that it was one of the first studies to use a variety of data sources, from social service to chemical use records, to answer complex questions.

    Still, Roberts is quick to point out the variety of the disease’s symptoms and possible causes. “Although our work provides a possible clue, it in no way demonstrates that exposure to pesticides during pregnancy causes autism,” Roberts says. “The study of environmental factors in autism is just beginning.”

    Roberts already has ideas for the future direction of autism research. “We would like to look closely at whether the association between proximity to the organochlorine pesticides and autism holds up over time,” he says. “Given the clue that specific compounds such as endosulfan and dicofol could be related to autism, we also hope that scientists in other fields could build on this finding to understand more about how autism occurs and how it might be treated and/or prevented.”

    By understanding what causes autism, many scientists and organizations believe they are closer to finding a solution. The wide variety of recent research on genetic and environmental causes of autism has provided the scientific community with interesting new theories and directions for upcoming research, but most of these, like the disorder itself, require more information.

    “It’s like the analogy of the blind men and the elephant: autism is the elephant, with scientists looking at different parts of it,” MacFabe says. Without being able to see the figurative elephant that is autism as a whole, each “blind” scientist studying individual factors have their own interpretation of what defines an elephant. “Everybody’s right but everybody’s wrong. We need to bring together experts in different aspects – that is going to be the new way of researching autism.”
