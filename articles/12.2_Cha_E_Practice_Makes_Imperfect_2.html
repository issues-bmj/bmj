continued from Practice Makes Imperfect


   In their article, “Evidence-based medicine: an approach to clinical problem-solving,” William Rosenberg, D.Phil., a clinical tutor in medicine, and Anna Donald, M.D., M.P.P., Senior House Officer of Nuffield Department of Clinical Medicine, claim that the many computer skills that are now universal among medical students may seem daunting to older doctors. Younger doctors are simply more efficient in searching through online databases. They also write that older doctors who base their decisions largely on their years of experience may see evidence-based medicine as a threat. This concern is even more relevant to authoritarian clinicians in the setting of a large hospital or a group practice, as the routine use of evidence over experience may erode hierarchical distinctions based on seniority.

    Possible solutions include developing more effective ways to keep doctors up to date and reevaluating current standards in testing for doctors’ competence. Currently, most boards waive requirements for renewal of specialty certification for older doctors, while some boards do not have recertification requirements for doctors of any age. However, there is a growing trend for stricter requirements. In emergency medicine, physicians are now required to take an annual exam that tests their knowledge in order to keep their certification.

    “Leaders in the healthcare industry, and mainly, the doctors themselves, should be spearheading the effort to reform the current system,” says Khiem Nguyen, M.D., a cardiologist in Westminster, California. The consensus among many healthcare professionals is that neither trusting the doctor’s own initiative to keep himself educated nor the distribution of printed information is effective enough. He agrees with his colleagues that a training and testing center where physicians can learn the newest theories and practice with the latest equipment would be very helpful. “But there are only so many hours in the day,” he says. “With the current system of healthcare, doctors have to see more patients and spend even more time with the piling paperwork. The inability of doctors to keep up is just a symptom of a larger problem.”

    However, just how many of us actually believe that younger doctors provide better care? Christine Le, a student at the University of California, Berkeley, and her family immigrated to America from Vietnam a few years ago to seek better medical care for her father. When asked about the difference between young and old doctors, she replies, “I would imagine that younger doctors are more enthusiastic and energized to make sure that you get the best treatment possible. The idealized view of the profession they picked up in medical school would still be fresh in their minds. I think that as the years go on, doctors end up being more concerned with taking care of their own interests and stop making the effort to learn new techniques and theories.” But when asked the age of the cardiologist she entrusts with the care of her father, Christine sheepishly shrugs her shoulders. “He’s pretty old. I know what I just said, but my family [and I] still feel more comfortable with a really experienced doctor.”
