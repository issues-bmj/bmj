Making A Splash


Underwater birthing

Meiji Tran

Spring 2005


    In the October 2003 issue of Pediatrics, Jerold F. Lucey, M.D., writes: “I’ve always considered underwater birth a bad joke, useless, and a fad that was so idiotic that it would go away. It hasn’t!” Although underwater births have increased in popularity over the past decade, the advantages and risks of this modern-day form of delivery remain controversial.

    There are several benefits to giving birth underwater. The buoyancy and warm temperature of the water alleviate pain for mothers; warmth diffuses the pain throughout the entire body and provides an important relaxing effect, allowing for easier and swifter labors. As a result, fewer injuries occur inside the birth canal when mothers assume the sitting position. Furthermore, the need for pain relief or other medical intervention is reduced due to the lower pressure gradient of the water. Another benefit is a shorter recuperation period for the mother. Additionally, many underwater deliveries, reports Pediatrics, have demonstrated that there is no increase in the risk of infection for either the mother or the baby. The controversy remains, however, due to the lack of scientific data on this matter.

    Adversaries of underwater births claim that the risks associated with the procedure should be considered by all expectant mothers. The primary concern is drowning, since doctors fear that babies may aspirate water as they emerge. Other possible complications include a deficiency of blood sodium, excessive bleeding, a dramatic loss of oxygen in the blood, and death.

    Underwater births pose various threats that are uncommon among land-based births because the procedure has not been studied enough for it to be deemed safe. While women may benefit from sitting in pools during their initial stages of labor, doctors urge that actual deliveries should still take place out of water.
