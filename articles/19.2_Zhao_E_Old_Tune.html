The Old Tune of Renewed Life


Eric Zhao

Spring 2012


    Some welcome change with open arms, but for senior citizens going through their golden years, change can prove to be a fearsome and traumatizing opponent. As their body slowly deteriorates, they are being thrown into an environment never seen before. As these conservative seniors grow older, they look to family, close friends, and doctors to try to regain the vitality of their youth. In January, neuroscientist Alexandra Parbery-Clark and her colleagues at Northwestern University published an article in the journal Neurobiology of Aging that claims to have discovered biological evidence that musical experience can help a senior citizen fight the deterioration of neural timing.

    Previous research by neuroscientist Nina Kruas published in the journal Nature Reviews Neuroscience shows that just as physical exercise improves fitness of the body, musical training improves the brain’s auditory fitness. This auditory fitness is essential for speech perception and social interaction in daily life. In Parbery-Clark’s study, the Northwestern neuroscientists monitored a pool of young (mean age of 23 years old) and old (mean age of 56 years old) musicians and non-musicians with standard hearing abilities, and examined their subcortical responses to different frequencies and syllables. The study tracked neural timing by identifying peaks in subcortical response generated by synchronous neural firing. Results indicated that the older non-musician test subjects were less responsive to noise bursts compared to younger non-musicians, but this distinction was absent in the musician sample pool. With the data, the Northwestern study concluded that the deterioration of inhibitory receptors in the auditory system due to aging is one of the potential candidates for this response timing since inhibitory processes are known to be responsible for the neural response patterns to speech.

    These findings are significant to the general population because the vigor of youth is cherished as an important aspect of a senior citizen going through aging. With exposure to music, seniors may be encouraged to delay the effects of aging by spending time interacting with music. As musical abilities clearly protect against the aging of neural timing, further research may elucidate other aspects of aging that can be gently delayed through the environment. Parbery-Clark and her colleagues encourage further research into how music may interact with other parts of the brain in order to promote “neural resilience across the lifespan” and give the general population the ability to enjoy life to the fullest.


About the Author

Eric Zhao is a third year Integrative Biology major and intends to pursue a career in medicine. With a profound curiosity in neuroscience, he is currently a student researcher at the Ernest Gallo Clinic and Research Center, studying alcoholism and addiction.
