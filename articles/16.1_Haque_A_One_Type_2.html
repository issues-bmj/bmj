continued from One Type to Rule Them All


    While ZymeQuest® first developed prototypes of its machine in the 1990s, it was not until recently that it found the enzymes that make it fully functional. According to Samira Johnson, the vice president of marketing and business development at ZymeQuest®, “what we have been able to pull from [certain] bacteria is an enzyme that can take those terminal sugars off.” Although many other bacterial enzymes have been found to strip these sugars off of red blood cells, all previous options have damaged the cell in some way until now. The enzymes that provided the breakthrough for the invention were first discovered in 2007 by a research team led by Henrik Clausen, PhD, at the University of Copenhagen in Denmark. After examining more than 2,500 fungi and bacteria, the team observed that only enzymes from Elizabethkingia meningosepticum and Bacterioides fragilis can safely strip the sugars covering red blood cells.

    Despite the complication involved in its creation, ZymeQuest®’s machine functions in a very straightforward manner. Blood that is going to be converted is first transported into a sealed container to prevent contamination. The previously mentioned enzymes are then added to convert the blood cells into O-type by latching onto and stripping off the antigenic sugars on non-O-type blood. Finally, the machine washes the blood cells, cleaning away the removed sugars and enzymes.

    The machine, however, is unable to remove the Rhesus factor, a protein that determines whether a blood cell has the RhD antigen or not (and is thus labeled positive or negative, respectively), so the machine does not make the blood cells completely universal. Because the Rhesus factor’s proteins are located inside the blood cells, there is no simple way to remove them without damaging the cell in the process. Nevertheless, converting all blood into O-type does greatly simplify the problem of blood types, as blood transfusions would only need to be divided into positive and negative O-types.

    Although the universal blood machine has been proven to convert human blood types into O-type, ZymeQuest® is still in the process of refining the procedure. However, Johnson says that the company has “done physical trials in humans and [has not encountered] safety issues and no physical conditions.” The company must also obtain Food and Drug Administration (FDA) approval, which involves several tests to ensure that blood from the machines is identical to unprocessed O-type blood.

    After these goals are achieved, ZymeQuest® will be permitted to sell the machine to hospitals and blood banks. Nonetheless, Garratty still remains skeptical. Although the machine promises great changes, it may “take many years to convince the FDA that [these new approaches] are efficient,” Garratty points out, “It will be a few years down the road before we will actually be able to do it.”
