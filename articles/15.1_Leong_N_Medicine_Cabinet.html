Medicine Cabinet of Addiction


Common cough medicine frequently abused

Narita Leong

Fall 2007


    Remember our parents coaxing us into taking cough medicine when we got sick? Recently, however, cough medication has become the source of an epidemic of teenage drug abuse and overdose. Although prolonged publicity about overdosing on over-the-counter drugs has made the issue almost mundane, many teenagers now feel that cough medicine is a moderate alternative to illegal drugs. Its high availability and low cost translate into increased legal circumvention and facilitation of substance abuse.

    Essentially, cough medicine abuse entails ingesting a large dosage of a dextromethorphan (DXM)-containing substance in a very short span of time to achieve a “high.” In 1958, DXM was approved by the Food and Drug Administration (FDA) as a safer, less addictive alternative to codeine, its narcotic predecessor, and is currently the active ingredient in more than 125 over-the-counter cough suppressants found in the United States, usually labeled “DX” or “maximum strength.”

    According to a study by Jodi K. Bryner, PharmD, University of California, San Francisco, and colleagues from other institutions published in the December 11, 2006 issue of the Archives of Pediatrics & Adolescent Medicine, DXM abuse has increased ten-fold from January 1, 1999 to December 31, 2004. Specifically, there has been a fifteen-fold increase in the 9-17-year- old age bracket that makes up approximately 75 percent of total reported abuse cases. This six-year study analyzed the increasing trend in DXM abuse as reported to the California Poison Control System and found that California’s patterns of abuse paralleled the nationwide trends documented by the American Association of Poison Control Centers and the Drug Abuse Network.

    While the effects of DXM vary with dosage, blurring the fine line between side effects and symptoms of an overdose, abusers claim to experience distinct levels of the high that range from mild stimulation or excitation with visual distortions to a sense of complete dissociation from one’s body. Side effects include nausea, abdominal pain, vomiting, irregular heartbeat, hypertension, headache, and numbness. Overdose can cause paralysis of the limbs, inability to speak, slowed or arrested breathing, permanent brain damage, cerebral hemorrhages, and stroke.

    DXM is explicitly exempt from regulation under the Controlled Substances Act of 1970. However, the Drug Enforcement Agency has recognized that DXM is recreationally abused as a dissociative anesthetic similar to phencyclidine (better known as PCP) and is therefore listed as a chemical of concern that will be considered for possible re-evaluation. The FDA considers DXM to be a “misbranded drug,” which means it can legally be sold without proper directions or warnings in a variety of forms. For the time being, abusers can only be prosecuted for disorderly conduct or disturbing the peace while under the influence. Aside from the aforementioned detriments of DXM abuse, the average abuser should be aware that DXM abuse and overdose can be fatal. Despite appearing to be deceptively benign, DXM is a neurotransmitter inhibitor that can significantly retard and even shut down neural pathways. While this ensuing disassociation of the brain is typically the desired effect of recreational use, it can also be a harbinger of lethal overdose. Focus on adults should primarily be concerned with their roles as parents; they should be aware of rampant DXM and cough syrup abuse. The common misconception of cough syrup being solely medicinal, as well as (continued on page 10) the fact that it is an affordable over-the-counter consumer product, often allows recreational abuse to go undetected until it is too late.
