continued from A Healing Vision


   Since the lens cells rely so heavily on glycolysis for energy, high blood glucose levels causes the lens cells to uptake more glucose. Once in the lens cells, the glucose is converted into an intermediate sugar known as sorbitol.  From there, sorbitol is converted into another sugar called fructose by the polyol dehydrogenase enzyme. Problems arise when polyol dehydrogenase becomes saturated at relatively low sorbitol levels. This means that the polyol dehydrogenase converts sorbitol into fructose at a much slower rate than the sorbitol is produced, creating an abundance of sorbitol in the lens.  The excess sorbitol increases osmotic pressure, causing the uptake of water into the lens cells. The lens fiber cells then begin to swell, causing light to scatter and cataracts to form.


    Currently there is no effective means of reducing opacity in the lens, and surgical extraction and replacement of the lens is the sole method available to physicians for treating cataracts. The procedure involves first making an incision in the sac that contains the lens, known as the lens capsule. Then the surgeon performs a phacoemulsification, where he or she uses an ultrasonic hand piece to liquefy the lens and vacuum it out of the eye with a miniature vacuum. The doctor finishes by inserting a plastic intraocular lens back into the lens capsule.


    Despite the prevalence of lens removal surgeries, there are some disadvantages. The main disadvantage of lens removal surgery lies in the cost. According to the American Academy of Ophthalmology, the average cataract surgery in the United States costs $3,300 per eye. This is difficult for families in lower income groups to afford, especially those with no healthcare.


    The future treatment of diabetic cataracts must be approached via a two pronged method. First is the issue of diabetes management and prevention. This includes maintaining good blood sugar levels, keeping a healthy blood pressure, and not smoking. Diets high in carbohydrates and linolenic acid, which is found in almost all cooking oils, have also been shown to increase the chances of getting diabetes.
The next step in the two pronged approach directly deals with the diabetic cataract. Currently the only way to treat cataracts requires surgery. While some studies have suggested that high doses of vitamin E can help to reverse cataracts these studies have yet to be proven and much more research and inquiry must be done.


    Researchers have also looked into drugs that may help to prevent or cure cataracts, but their efforts are complicated by the fact that the mechanisms of cataract formation are still not entirely understood. Before any potential drugs can be tested, the problem of how to administer the drugs must first be solved. Currently the most common method of administering eye medication is through eye drops. However, eye drops evaporate relatively quickly, making them ineffective for treating cataracts due to the fact that the medication must remain on the eye for a long period of time in order for it to penetrate the cornea. Also, patients are often not diligent with their medication and will contaminate it, causing further damage to their eyes. By bypassing the cornea and directly injecting the medication into the thick watery substance between cornea and lens, higher and more sustained levels of the drug can be achieved. The hope is that the direct injection of medication into the eye in conjunction with future drugs will reverse the development of cataracts, providing the patient an alternative to expensive and risky surgical procedures.


    As the United States struggles to deal with the obesity epidemic, obesity-related illnesses such as diabetes are expected to continue their upward climb. According to the American Diabetics Association, a staggering 79 million Americans have prediabetes, the stage in which individuals are highly at risk for developing diabetes.  Ultimately, the best advice for people to prevent diabetic cataracts is to eat a healthy balanced diet and to exercise regularly. This will cut medical costs, reduce stress due to surgeries and vision loss, and help us continue to see clearly even as we enter our golden years.
