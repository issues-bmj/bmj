continued from The Silent Thief


   A study done recently in Taiwan by Dr. Shiu-Dong Chung of National Taiwan University sought to see if there was a correlation between erectile dysfunction and prior diagnosis of open-angle glaucoma. Glaucoma was introduced around 400 BC, during the time of Hippocrates, and originally described a swollen cornea or a developing cataract due to increasing intraocular pressure. According to Dr. Jerald Bell, an ophthalmologist, glaucoma is more commonly understood to be damaging to the optic nerve caused by such elevated intraocular pressure over an extended period of time; as a result, glaucoma is a slow and progressive disorder that ultimately leads to blindness, but only half of the people who may have glaucoma are truly aware that they have the disease. Open-angle glaucoma is what the majority of glaucoma patients suffer from, as aqueous fluid is chronically unable to drain from the eye, whereas closed-angle glaucoma is when the iris completely blocks fluid and leads to a build-up of pressure. According to Dr. Nathan Congdon’s study published in the Journal of the American Medical Association, glaucoma is the second leading cause of blindness worldwide, and open-angle glaucoma is among the most common cases. Possible cases of open-angle glaucoma are correlated with older age, hypertension, and diabetes. The prevalence of erectile dysfunction in men with such risk factors shows that erectile dysfunction may be related to open-angle glaucoma, and the Taiwanese study was interested in researching this link.

    Using a population-based study, Dr. Chung and his colleagues examined the association of erectile dysfunction with prior open-angle glaucoma by sampling people in Taiwan over 40 years of age who were recently diagnosed with erectile dysfunction. Patients with prior open-angle glaucoma were compared to matched controls. Results were further adjusted for a number of environmental variables, such as economic class, geographic region and urbanization of the region, as well as other health disorders such as hyperlipidemia, diabetes, obesity, hypertension, and coronary heart disease. Results indicated that people with prior open-angle glaucoma were 2.85 times more likely to have erectile dysfunction, suggesting that there may be a related mechanism of disease for the two disorders that has not yet been found.

    With this new research by the Taiwanese study, Dr. Chung looks forward to implementing clinical guidelines for evaluating patients with  previously diagnosed erectile dysfunction as well as furthering researching on the correlation between open-angle glaucoma and erectile dysfunction in order to better prepare physicians on advising patients with both conditions. With research, doctors can recommend patients with the disorder to have their intraocular pressures checked routinely, allowing for better and more efficient diagnosis in patients with erectile dysfunction and open-angle glaucoma. Doctors are also empowered to utilize such knowledge to educate patients in fighting effectively against such an invasive obstacle. With such exciting new therapies and treatments, doctors are closer to bringing the fundamental concept of simple peace of mind and freedom of life to patients that need it most.
