continued from In Oxytocin We Trust


   In recent years, Dr. Heinrichs has investigated oxytocin’s specific relationship to trusting behavior. His research suggests that oxytocin does not specifically stimulate trust, but instead helps an individual “overcome his or her social avoidance behavior.” His findings have revealed that oxytocin “attenuates behavioral and neuroendocrine responses to social stress” and increases the “stress-protective” effects of social interaction. Most importantly, Dr. Heinrichs emphasizes that the presence of social interaction is “crucial” for the operation of the behavioral, cognitive, and emotional effects of oxytocin. The results suggest that an oxytocin-derived drug could eventually be used to treat various social phobias, anxiety disorders, and even autism.

    Dr. Heinrichs and Dr. Kosfeld have led the way in human-oriented research for oxytocin. Heinrichs’ team is currently conducting a series of studies on the clinical effects of oxytocin on disorders such as social phobia, and their research combines oxytocin administration with cognitive-behavioral therapy.

    Dr. Keltner, on the other hand, prefers therapy-based interventionist treatments of extreme developmental disorders like autism, but he indicates that the use of oxytocin or similar drugs in extreme circumstances would be permissible. “Autistic children have trouble making emotional connections, do not respond to touch, and have trouble understanding emotion. If you can make these kids more connected through oxytocin, I’d be for that.”

    The prospect of such a potentially powerful drug also comes with negative implications, such as abuse or misuse, prompting the need for federal oversight. Walter Freeman, PhD, of UC Berkeley, believes oxytocin has “real potential” for the therapy of chronic mental disorders, though he adds that its benefits may be outweighed by its potential for misuse.

    Dr. Freeman acknowledges that the likelihood of a panic scene is low. He adds, however, that the “individual dosage to recruits given by cults, gangs, military and paramilitary cadres, evangelist churches could easily lead to abuse, if the route of administration is tolerable. It can just as easily be forced.”
 
    He notes that if “the drug is as effective as ecstasy was found to be, and if it is widely adopted by organizations preying on adolescents, poor people, the homeless, prisoners in jails, pimps recruiting prostitutes, and corporations instilling corporate ethos, then I expect the Federal government will make it a Class One controlled substance.”

    According to Dr. Heinrichs, oxytocin intranasal spray is available in all pharmacies in Europe, but prescriptions are dispensed only to aid lactation in breast-feeding women. Because oxytocin cannot be administered orally and has no psychotropic effects like many other drugs, Dr. Heinrichs dispelled notions that this hormone could be manipulated.

    “Thank God there is no technical solution to indirectly manipulate people with oxytocin,” Dr. Heinrichs said. “So, it’s not likely that political operators will generously spray the crowd with oxytocin at rallies of their candidates.”

    Dr. Heinrichs and Dr. Kosfeld concluded their report in Nature by suggesting that their findings may yield “positive clinical implications for patients with mental disorders that are associated with social dysfunctions.” They believe their current clinical studies of oxytocin may lead to “fertile research on the role of oxytocin in several mental health disorders with major public health significance.” Dr. Heinrichs’ team expects final results in the beginning of 2006.
