continued from Look Ma: No (Externally-Powered, Hook-Outfitted, Prosthetic) Hands!


   Sullivan travels to Chicago frequently to participate in further research. When at the RIC, Sullivan uses a cutting-edge six-motor    myoelectric prosthesis that combines a shoulder from Scotland and a wrist developed in Germany with a hand manufactured in China. This high-tech bionic arm is still a prototype, and when at home, Sullivan relies on a three-motor prosthesis. Although the arm is driven by only three motors, the fact that it is operated by the redirected nerves in his chest muscles, as opposed to a pair of remaining muscles, allows him to manipulate his arm, wrist, and hand at practically the same time. Actions such as weed-whacking or climbing a ladder that would have been significantly more difficult, if not virtually impossible, with a conventional prosthesis have now become commonplace for Sullivan. Eric Westover, editor of UpperEx, a magazine devoted solely to upper extremity amputees, and goalkeeper for the United States National Amputee Soccer Team remarks that “there is definitely practicality in the bionic prosthesis.” He notes that harnesses are not needed to operate the device, unlike with a body-powered unit. Further, moving one’s arms behind the back and above the head, which Westover contends can become “very difficult, if not impossible, to achieve” with a body-powered unit, is relatively easy with the new myoelectric arm. Schultz adds, “Because the control of each degree of freedom is more natural and because the reinnervation surgery has provided more myoelectric control sites […] the subject can control multiple degrees of freedom at once, a task impossible with a conventional myoelectric prosthesis. We have found that our patients are capable of controlling multiple degrees of freedom with little additional effort.”

    Sullivan’s success has recently been confirmed in another patient, Claudia Mitchell, who lost her arm in a motorcycle accident. Encouraged by these remarkable procedures, the Pentagon’s Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency (DARPA) has consequently begun research into replacement limbs for veterans. Although the commercial market for such high-tech myoelectric arms is limited, the recent increase in amputee soldiers has led the military to investigate these incredible prosthetics. With the cooperation of 30 science and industry leaders, DARPA has jump-started research into an even more advanced bionic prosthesis, which incorporates sensory input mechanisms, better myoelectric control and lower-limb applications that thousands of patients could eventually use. Westover notes that “with all the media attention on the returning veterans from the conflicts around the world and the money that the government is investing into this area, it has definitely sparked a technological race among scientists, researchers, engineers, and all those involved with the industry of prosthetics and service providers of upper extremity amputees.” Grace C.Y. Peng, PhD, program director at the National Institute of Biomedical Imaging and Bioengineering’s Division of Discovery Science and says, “One of the next steps is to move beyond movement control and integrate other sensory systems into these devices. Users depend on these devices to relieve the burden of daily activities. Information of touch, pain, [or] temperature...will further facilitate daily function.” Research into these areas, coupled with the success of Sullivan and Mitchell, seems to suggest on the whole a brighter future for amputees and a more realistic one for their prosthetic accompaniments.
