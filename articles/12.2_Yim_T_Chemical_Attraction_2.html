continued from Chemical Attraction


   Forty-four post-menopausal women participated in a six-week double-blind study. Half the group added Athena Pheromone 10:13™ to their perfume and the other half added a placebo compound. In an article published in The Journal of Sex Research, principal investigator Dr. Rako reported that “among postmenopausal women, a significantly greater proportion of those using the pheromone than those using placebo showed an increase over their own baseline in intimate sexual behaviors.” The study reported that 41 percent of the pheromone users experienced more petting, kissing, and affection with partners compared with 14 percent of the placebo users. Furthermore, 68 percent of the pheromone group reported increases in “intimate socio-sexual behaviors” such as formal dates and sex. Only 41 percent of the placebo group experienced an increase in these behaviors. The results were analyzed across age, height, weight, body mass index, education, and numerous other factors to ensure that the pheromone had indeed been the cause of increased frequency of sexual behavior.

    What is the special ingredient in Athena 10:13™? Although the exact chemical makeup will remain under wraps until a patent has been granted, Dr. Cutler has disclosed that Athena 10:13™ does contain a synthetic blend that resembles women’s axillary secretions. Axillary secretions are typically steroids produced by the testes, ovaries, and adrenal glands. Though humans typically have far fewer scent glands than do other animals, the human axillary scent glands (located primarily in the hollow of the upper arm and shoulder) are actually quite large and contain a high density of apocrine glands. These apocrine glands produce a number of steroids, three of which are hypothesized to be human pheromones—androstenone, androstenol, and androstadienone. Only a marginal amount of steroids are released into axillary sweat, producing virtually no detectable odor. Instead, axillary coryneform and coccal bacteria convert these steroids into more aromatic molecules.

    Other pheromonal evidence includes the well-documented menstrual synchrony due to communal exposure to axillary odors. Such synchrony exists in mammalian species such as rats, hamsters, and some primates. Numerous studies report synchronization of menstrual cycles among roommates, close friends, co-workers, lesbian couples, family members, and women’s sports teams. In a similar vein, studies show that the presence of men accelerates the menstrual cycles of women, supposedly due to their axillary pheromones. Women who see men socially at least three to four nights a week have significantly shorter menstrual cycles than those who do not.

    Evidence of the sexual dimorphism of the axillary scent glands and of menstrual synchrony supports the theory of axillary steroids as human pheromones. Studies in which women were exposed to the airborne axillary steroid androstadienone, the epithelial cells of the female VNO showed an electrochemical response. Exposure to airborne estratetraenol—a steroid found in pregnant women—produced similar effects in men. When exposed to the airborne estratetraenol, men (but not women) showed electrochemical responses in the epithelial cells of the VNO. MRI analysis of the men showed increased activity in certain areas of the brain. Furthermore, exposure to androstadienone and estratetraenol was also found to alter skin temperature and conductance in both sexes.

    If human pheromones do exist, it is necessary to consider the ramifications of social customs that encourage the removal of natural odors. Do such institutionalized customs handicap nature’s intended mode of sexual communication? A study in 2001 reported that while humans do replace their natural odors with artificial ones, their personal selection of these replacement odors is determined by their major histocompatibility complex (MHC) genetic patterns. Both men and women seem to prefer axillary odors of people who have MHC patterns dissimilar to their own. Could this mean that opposites really do attract? While this remains a distinct possibility, it is important to remember that whatever effect human pheromones may have taken place within the context of other complex physiological, psychological, and sociological factors.
