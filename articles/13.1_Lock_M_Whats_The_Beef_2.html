continued from What's the Beef?


   Indeed, research asserts that the levels of HCAs in cooked meats drastically increase the chances of developing different types of cancer. Many global epidemiological studies have tested whether the amounts of HCAs in cooked meats are significant enough to induce carcinogenesis in humans. “Seventy-seven percent of these studies show a correlation with eating overdone meats and cancer of one type or another. Breast, prostate, colon, [and] stomach stand out as the most suspect,” proclaims James Felton, PhD, who is head of the Molecular Toxicology Group of the Biology and Biotechnology Program at Lawrence Livermore Laboratories.

    Dr. Malfatti agrees, but urges caution. “Since we can’t do carcinogenicity studies in humans, all we have to go by is animal data. All the HCAs that have been tested in animals have been tumorigenic,” he notes. The HCAs have seemed to produce liver, colon, mammary, and prostate tumors in rats as well as lymphomas in mice. “So, there may be some cancer risk from consuming well-cooked meats over a lifetime exposure, but there are no definite conclusions that can be made. Personally, I think that there is enough scientific evidence to indicate that the HCAs do pose a cancer risk and that you should limit your exposure.”

    Fortunately, a lot can be done. “The best way to limit your exposure is to not cook your meat to well done status. By cooking [it] medium [well,] you can reduce the time it takes to cook your meat, thereby reducing the amount of HCAs formed significantly,” advises Dr. Malfatti. “Also if you marinate your meat before cooking you can reduce the amount of HCAs formed by ninety percent.” Marinating meats before cooking provides a barrier between the cooking surface and the meat, which prevents    the meat from reaching excessively high temperatures. Additionally, “marinating would increase the amount of liquid in the meat, and the more liquid there is, the longer it takes the meat to dry out [in order] to get to the temperature that is producing those chemicals,” articulates Dr. Bjeldanes.

    HCAs are not commonly formed in foods like vegetables and grains due to their low creatine content. Therefore, cooking garden or tofu burgers might be a safer alternative to burgers made with meat. Eating more vegetables and less meat would also reduce HCA ingestion. If using vegetable alternatives is not an option, cutting the meat into smaller pieces and cooking at low temperatures reduces the concentration of HCAs.

    Questions remain regarding the interaction of HCAs with other foods. “Prevention foods and their role in dietary exposures need to be understood. [We need to know] how other foods prevent, metab[olize] or detoxify HCAs,” says Dr. Felton. Researchers hope that in a few years they will be able to identify certain chemicals in food like fruits, vegetables and grains that can protect us from these chemical carcinogens. The ultimate goal is to produce drugs that effectively treat or prevent cancer. Maybe after some time we will be able to cook our meat thoroughly and eat it too. But until then, be cautious the next time barbeque Sunday rolls around!
