Chemical Attraction


Human pheromones in perfume

Timothy Yim

Spring 2005

   
    Have you ever wished for that irresistible chemical attraction between you and a member of the opposite sex? Today, science has delivered the answer—a potion in the form of human pheromones. Historically, there has been much debate over the existence and function of human pheromones. The difficulty in identifying such pheromones is summed up by Harvard University’s Catherine Dulac, Ph.D., Professor of Molecular and Cellular Biology and Investigator at the Howard Hughes Medical Institute. According to Dr. Dulac, “It has been difficult to identify human pheromones that elicit innate behavioral arrays since behavior in humans is far more likely to be tempered by learning and experience.”

    Pheromones are chemical substances released by one individual and received by a second individual of the same species, triggering sexual attraction when detected by members of the opposite sex. In order to participate in pheromone-induced behavior, humans must have a sensory organ in addition to the olfactory epithelium to aid in the detection of airborne chemicals. A great deal of evidence points to the vomeronasal organ as this pheromone sensor.

    Vomeronasal organs (VNOs) are found in many mammalian species, including humans. Located in the vomer bone between the nose and the mouth, the VNO is used concurrently with the olfactory epithelium as an airborne chemical detector and pheromone receptor. The VNOs of hamsters are surrounded by muscular tissues, which pump odors onto the receptor cells. Horses, dogs, cats, and other species have been known to draw odors into their mouths and through the nasal palate to facilitate placement of pheromones on the vomeronasal organ upon encountering a member of the opposite sex in a process known as flehmen.

    Historically, occurrences of VNO degeneration in late fetal development have prompted scientists to postulate that the human VNO was a vestigial organ—a degenerate, usually nonfunctioning, structure that was fully developed or functioning in a preceding generation or at an earlier stage of development. However, recent s t u d i e s have shown that at least 90 percent of adults have a visible VNO on their nasal septum. These human VNOs are a pair of tiny tubes that are only millimeters long. Closer inspection of the human VNO reveals an epithelium consistent with the hypothesis of the VNO as a chemical sensory organ. Further analysis also reveals cells similar to sensory neurons. However, there is as of yet no conclusive proof that neural connections exist between the VNO sensory nerves and the brain. Nonetheless, there is a growing body of evidence that suggests that the VNO does not degenerate and remains functional throughout a person’s life.

    The debate concerning the purpose of human pheromones may finally come to a conclusive end with the recent discovery and successful synthesis of a female human pheromone. The Athena Institute for Women’s Wellness Research has isolated and synthesized Athena Pheromone 10:13™, purified from the scent released by young women as a subconscious sex attractant. According to Winnifred Cutler, Ph.D., founder of the Athena Institute, the pheromone’s discoverer, “Athena Pheromone 10:13™ can enhance the ‘sexual attractiveness’ of women” and has passed “science’s ‘gold standard’ test of whether a product works—a double-blind, placebo-controlled study.” Studies in 2002 reported that heterosexual women wearing Athena 10:13™ engaged in increased sexual activity with men. In a more recent study published in 2004, Joan Friebely, Ed.D., of Harvard University, and psychiatrist Susan Rako, M.D., showed that the synthesized human pheromone discovered by the Athena Institute increased the frequency of intimate sexual behavior with partners in post-menopausal women who no longer produced the pheromone naturally.
