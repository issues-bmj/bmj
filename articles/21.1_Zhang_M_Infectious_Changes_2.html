continued from Infectious Changes


    This experiment shows that Toxo infections cause loss of aversion to feline predators in mice. This behavior change would make infected mice more likely to be prey, but why would a parasite want its host to become eaten? Toxo can only complete its lifecycle in the gut of a cat, making the mouse a crucial vector for reproduction. By manipulating the mice to lose their innate fear of cats, Toxo can reproduce. In the cat’s gut, Toxo forms oocysts, a thick walled structure which protects developing Toxo parasites, which serves to transfer them to new hosts. These oocysts can survive harsh environments and will release the parasites after it is ingested by its new host. Once inside the host, the parasite can ensconce itself within neurons, create dormant cysts in the brain, and potentially stay in a host for its entire life. But even if it is eliminated from the host, Ingram’s experiment shows that behavioral changes can persist.


    The permanence of behavior changes is not only significant to understanding Toxo, but also when considering the possibility that other pathogens also have the potential to cause permanent changes. Currently, the field of medicine assumes that pathogens cause symptoms such as fever, pains, etc, and that the elimination of the pathogen from the body will also cure the symptoms. However, there are some medical cases where there are symptoms but no detectable culprit. Could it be that these symptoms are caused by Toxo like infections which also can cause permanent changes after infection? Though more research is needed, Ingram believes this study could change the current paradigm of treating symptoms and diseases and help health professionals better understand how to treat symptoms. Perhaps medical history is an important piece of the puzzle when treating patients. Ingram states that a powerful new direction of medicine is to use anti-bodies to see all the pathogens a patient has interacted with. The body produces antibodies every time it mounts an immune response to a pathogen, and these antibodies are left in a patient's bloodstream. By studying antibodies, medical professionals can study past and present infections to find the culprit for symptoms and provide a more effective method of treatment. Toxo is making waves in the medical community, and further research may provide key insights into battling currently untreatable symptoms.


    Toxo is by no means understood yet, and Ingram plans to continue studying the exact mode of infection of Toxo. Further research into the mode of infection will not only help health professionals better understand “permanent” symptoms of infections, but hopefully also provide strategies for treating such symptoms.
