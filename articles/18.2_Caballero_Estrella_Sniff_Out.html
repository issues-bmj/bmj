Sniff Out Your Sweetheart


Estrella Caballero

Spring 2011


    Is it possible for a woman to sniff out her soul-mate? Well, maybe not exactly her soul-mate but a potential father. Biologically speaking, it is not always about the striking good looks, or the tall-dark-and-handsome type that will determine who will be a female’s preferred mate. Using Major Histocompatibility Complexes (MHC), females mechanistically look for what is “on the inside” all the way down to their genes. MHCs help women in the evolutionary quest to find more genetic variability for their progeny.


    MHCs are a group of molecules that help the immune system distinguish between harmful and safe foreign substances in the body. An MHC consists of a large cluster of genes, human leukocyte antigens (HLA), located on the short arm of chromosome 6. MHCs also contain genes that determine the structure of HLA molecules which ultimately influence human body odor and odor preference. In this case specifically, HLAs control a man’s body odor and a female’s body odor preference for a potential mate.


    While MHCs and pheromones work in conjunction with each other, they are different. Pheromones interact with the vomeronasal organ that lies between the nose and the mouth, which processes scent signals. Pheromones act as emotional stimuli and carry markers that help identify MHCs. The MHC influences both body odors and body odor preference in human females. This is thought to ensure the production of genetically diverse offspring and prevent inbreeding.


    Research on female selectivity might prove how females specifically affect natural selection through their mate preference. They do this by choosing certain scents that are associated with a specific genetic make-up that is identified in a person’s MHC. In an individual, there are multiple MHC molecules which present a wide variety of short proteins. For example, in an epidemic MHC diverse individuals will be more likely to possess the adequate proteins to initiate an immune response, and survive the infection. In this way, MHC diversity provides long-term protection of a population or species. Through this biological underpinning, women seek diverse MHCs to ensure that their offspring are genetically fit.


    Researchers at the University of Bern in Switzerland conducted a study concerning this phenomenon, discovering that females actually choose their genetically preferred mate based upon their scent. In this study, subjects were categorized by their HLAs, –AB and –DR, which are antigens found in human cells that make up MHCs that help the immune system identify its own cells from foreign cells.


    In the study, researchers collected MHC types among both males and females. They accounted for females who were on the contraceptive pill as the steroid imitating pregnancy could be a confounding factor. They gave each male an untreated cot ton t-shirt and asked them to wear it from Sunday to Monday avoiding deodorant, perfume, a specific list of odor producing foods, smelly rooms, and sexual activity. They also required them to sleep alone and were given a special unscented soap bar and laundry detergent. On Monday, the females in the study were asked to smell and rate six of the worn t-shirts.


    The women were given three t-shirts that were similar to them according to their MHC and three t-shirts that were dissimilar to them in their MHC. The women did not know which was which. The study discovered that women who were not taking the contraceptive pill found the scent that was most dissimilar to their own MHC most attractive. Odors of the MHC-dissimilar men reminded the females of their own mate or ex-mate twice as often as those with similar MHC. Unexpectedly, women who were on the contraceptive pill found the scent that was most similar to their own MHC most attractive. 


    The results of this study found that a woman’s preference changes in accordance with her hormonal status. The study suggested that MHC influences mate choice according to the body odor emitted by the male. The females on the contraceptive pill were more attracted to the t-shirts that presented a similar MHC to their own since the contraceptive pill simulates pregnancy hormones; therefore the female prefers to be near people to whom she is genetically related. This is why she finds the similar MHC scents attractive; she identifies their MHCs as being relatives.


    These studies have also been conducted in mice, birds, and insects and have come to similar conclusions. Animals are more attracted to the opposite sex when they smell a different MHC combination than their own through the pheromones that are being emitted. MHCs have also been thought of as aiding animals to avoid inbreeding which leads to more genetic diversity. More genetic diversity in turn gives offspring a better chance of surviving certain illnesses.


    Even though this study provides evidence supporting the argument that MHCs influence mate choice, there is still much to be researched in this area. In addition, there are factors such as culture and religion that influence mate choice. It is fascinating to know that humans are subconsciously looking for more than what is perceived on the outside. Biological nature drives these mechanisms to ensure that our genes are carried on. The complexity of nature unraveled is absolutely remarkable, especially when it comes to understanding how we can potentially choose our prospective mates through the scent they emit.
