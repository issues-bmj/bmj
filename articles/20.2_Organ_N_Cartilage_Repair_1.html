Stem Cell Research Targets Cartilage Repair


Nancy Organ

Spring 2013


    The American population is no stranger to the symptoms of damaged cartilage. According to the Center for Disease Control and Prevention, up to 27 million Americans suffer from Osteoarthritis, a chronic condition that causes painful inflammation of the joints and joint damage.  In addition to that, the Hospital for Special Surgery in New York estimates that each year 400,000 Americans require surgery for cartilage damage. Fortunately, the stem cell world is exploding with promising research into tissue repair and regeneration.  In particular, cartilage repair has attracted substantial attention in the last year as a target for stem cell research.


    Labs around the world are engaged in exciting projects to develop and perfect stem cell therapies that target one of the most commonly debilitating injuries. Unlike muscle or bone, cartilage is notoriously difficult to repair because the tissue does not have direct blood or nerve supply. Current treatments for cartilage damage and degradation include pain management measures and surgery to transplant cartilage cells from another location in the patient’s body. One of the most renowned techniques is called autologous chondrocyte implantation, or ACI. The surgery is a multi-step intervention that uses the patient’s own cultured cartilage cells and transplanted membranes to trap new cartilage cells in defective parts of a joint.


    While ACI is helpful for many, it has its share of drawbacks. Firstly, ACI cannot be performed on those with osteoarthritis (OA). OA is an autoimmune disorder, which means that the body is just as likely to attack the transplanted cartilage as it is the original cartilage; transplant surgery is therefore not helpful. Additionally, ACI transplants can take months to heal and more than a year to reach full functionality. To narrow the pool of potential ACI recipients even further, candidates must have otherwise healthy knees and sound biomechanics to reduce the risk of complications after the transplant. Clearly, options are limited for many people suffering with cartilage-related knee and joint problems.


    Near the end of 2012, however, Dr. Liang Xin Tay et al. from the University of Malaya, Malaysia, published work in The American Journal of Sports Medicine comparing ACI with a newer method involving stem cell implants.  Tay’s study implemented allogenic mesenchymal stem cells, or alloMSCs. MSCs are undifferentiated stem cells that can be extracted from umbilical cord blood, bone marrow, and adipose tissue. The fact that they are allogenic means that the cells are harvested from a different organism than the one receiving the treatment.


    The alloMSC cells are of special interest because they have an excellent ability to differentiate into different types of tissues. Here, it is of primary importance that MSCs are able to differentiate into bone cells and cartilage cells. Additionally, alloMSC are less vulnerable to attack by the patient’s body.  “Mesenchymal stem cells can evade host immune system and modulate and suppress rejection from the recipient after injection (...),” explained Tay in an interview. “In our study,” he confirmed, “rabbits treated with alloMSCs were not observed going through any attack or immune response.”


    Dr. Tay’s trial was conducted on 30 white male rabbits of similar age and weight. All of the rabbits underwent operations to intentionally damage the cartilage in both knees. The left knees served as controls while the right knees were randomly assigned to one of the two treatments, either ACI or alloMSC therapy. Stem cells were harvested from the bone marrow of 3 additional rabbits. The autoC and alloMSC cells were encased in alginate, an algae-derived polymer that is used to provide a structure into which the new cells can grow.  Six weeks after the cartilage injuries were performed, the rabbits received implants of either autoC or alloMSC beads.  To secure the beads, a small flap of healthy tissue from the uninjured side of the injured knee was sewn on top of the injury.
