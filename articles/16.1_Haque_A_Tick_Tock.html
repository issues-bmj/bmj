Tick-tock, Where's the Clock?


Our perception of Time

Adnan Haque

Fall 2008


    For most people, the phrase “time flies when you’re having fun” is merely a figure of speech to account for the feeling experienced during enjoyable situations when time seems to rapidly elapse. However, this notion of fleeting time is in fact a biological process known as interval timing, a previously mysterious phenomenon that one recent study may now shed light upon. Duke University neurobiological research team, led by Warren Meck, PhD, and Catalin Buhusi, PhD, narrowed the source of interval timing to the brain’s basal ganglia. This discovery, published in the October 2005 issue of Nature Reviews Neuroscience, may one day allow humans to control the way they interpret time.

    For the past few years, Meck and Buhusi have been examining time perception in patients suffering from mental disorders, particularly in patients with Huntington’s and Parkinson’s disease, both of which are characterized by abnormal function within the basal ganglia, an aggregate of nuclei in the brain. This region has long been implicated in central timekeeping functions since its nerve cells primarily contain the neurotransmitter dopamine. For example, Huntington’s disease results from the genetically-programmed degeneration of neurons, specifically those in a structure of the basal ganglia known as the striatum, which plays an integral role in the timekeeping process. Furthermore, dopamine levels within the basal ganglia are so abnormally low in Parkinson’s disease patients that they experience difficulties in time perception. These levels improve to some extent when patients are administered drugs that restore optimal levels of dopamine in the brain.

    By studying the relationship between timekeeping and medication, Meck and Buhusi have discovered the link between abnormal brain activity and time perception. They concluded that interval timing is in fact a cognitive process known as the striatal beat frequency model. In this model, every organ in the body oscillates to a certain beat. These pulsations eventually reach the brain, where they are sensed by the basal ganglia. The striatum then interprets the oscillations to form a mental clock, allowing the brain to calculate the duration of an action by the number of vibrations that occur during the event. This allows us to keep track and interpret incidences that last longer than a few seconds.

    Currently, most neurobiologists split time perception into three categories: circadian rhythm, millisecond timing, and interval timing. The circadian rhythm is a 24-hour cycle that functions as a biological clock for nearly all higher-order organisms. At the other extreme, millisecond timing is responsible for our perception of events during periods shorter than a second. For years, the basis for the third type of timekeeping, interval timing, was unknown.

    What was known about interval timing was that it serves as the middle ground, allowing humans to understand actions that occur within a time frame ranging from a few seconds to several hours. It coordinates a wide range of actions, such as communicating, eating, and working. While the basis for both circadian rhythm and millisecond timing has been fairly well studied, investigation into interval timing has been stagnant until now.

    Meck and Buhusi are currently refining the understanding of this process. For example, they have determined that one of the main components of interval timing is the neurotransmitter dopamine. By altering dopamine levels, Meck and Buhusi hope to understand the direct relationship between the neurotransmitter and the striatum. They have also mentioned that by controlling dopamine levels, humans could one day distort their perception of time, an effect already achieved by certain narcotics such as cocaine and cannabis.

    If scientists are able to find chemicals which have specific effects on time perception, then they may be able to create drugs specially designed to alter human awareness. By taking a certain chemical, students could get through a boring lecture in only a few minutes or make a fun game seem to last for hours. Since interval timing is also responsible for getting people into the zone, – a feeling of deep concentration that usually results in an increase of productivity – neurobiologists may one day discover a drug that could compel people to focus intensely on a task, increasing their productivity and quality of work. However, Meck and Buhusi admit that since further research into the specifics of interval time perception is needed, there is still a long way to go before medicine reaches this point.
