The Silent Thief of Vision and Sensation: Open-angle Glaucoma and Erectile Dysfunction


Eric Zhao

Spring 2012

   
    Ask a male about his thoughts on erectile dysfunction. Most men will respond to such a topic with feelings of embarrassment, disappointment, and despair as the inability to sustain the foundation of a content relationship implies weakness and ultimately evolves into shame in today’s cultural foundations. According to José Pomerol of Fundació Puigvert in Barcelona, Spain, in a study published in The Journal of Sexual Medicine, sexual health is a basic human right for individuals to take pleasure in sexual behavior. Pomerol claims that such a disability harms a healthy sexual relationship and leads to psychological consequences and an unfavorable quality of life. He also says that erectile dysfunction is a highly common health problem, citing “The Epidemiological Study on Male Erectile Dysfunction,” done in his home country of Spain, which claims that as much as 12% of men have some extent of erectile dysfunction.

    According to a study on the molecular basis of erectile dysfunction led by Dr. Jesse Mills of the University of Colorado School of Medicine, smooth muscle deterioration, lower androgen levels (a steroid hormone), and less stimulation of the corpus cavernosa (the sponge-like regions of the penis that contains erectile tissue engorged with blood) as a result of high levels of penile nitric oxide contribute to erectile dysfunction. Erectile dysfunction is also notably associated with old age as well as the common disorders that come with old age. According to JT Korpelainen of the Department of Neurology of the University of Oulu, stroke patients experience a significant decline in their sexual lifestyle in aspects of frequency and satisfaction. Moreover, erectile dysfunction is also an indicator for cardiovascular disorders. According to Dr. Juliana Chung-Ngor Chan of The Chinese University of Hong Kong, erectile dysfunction is a powerful indicator in men with Type 2 diabetes for cardiovascular complications. Additionally, according to a study led by Dr. Judith Finn published in The Journal of Sexual Medicine, erectile dysfunction can also be used to predict health problems, especially at younger ages, related to atherosclerotic cardiovascular complications, which involves the thickening of an artery by fat, which is common to diets with higher cholesterol.  With this knowledge, researchers have clearly asserted a correlation between erectile dysfunction and metabolic and cardiovascular disorders. And recently, researchers in Taiwan have decided to take a step further in bringing quality care and comfort to afflicted patients fighting against erectile dysfunction.
