Video Games: A Treatment for Dyslexia


Garren Langford

Spring 2013


    Everyone has played them before. Most would associate them with their childhood. Urban Dictionary defines it as commonly used as a “scapegoat for politicians and crappy parents.” It is probable that you and your siblings would spend endless hours fighting enemies and solving puzzles in order to have the fulfillment of reaching a new level and finishing a quest. Video games are very prominent in our lives especially with all the new forms of multimedia that comes with improved technology. But do video games affect our brains and who we become as people in the future? How much power can this form of entertainment hold?


    A new study shows that video games may be a possible treatment for dyslexia because they are able to sharpen the focus skills of children. In this study, 10 dyslexic children played an action video game for 12 hours over the course of many days. Meanwhile another group played another non-action video game. Researchers discovered that children who had reading disabilities scored better on tests after they had played hours of the action video game. This data is thought to be correlated to the idea that their focus and concentration had greatly enhanced from playing over the duration of an extended period of time. Assistant professor of Cognitive & Neuroscience at the University of Padua in Italy claims that they are not suggesting a “do it yourself” training for kids, but rather a tool for reading teachers who teach kids with dyslexia. The children in the study who had played the action game had improved their reading speed by much more than they normally would in a concentrated reading program.
