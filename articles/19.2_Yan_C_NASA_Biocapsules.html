NASA Biocapsules


Charley Yan

Spring 2012


    For many diabetics, the idea of constantly administering shots is the largest hassle of their treatment. The daily injection of insulin is a necessary part of their lives, but the annoyances that come with having the disease, such as remembering to bring insulin supplements and constantly monitoring blood sugar levels are an extreme inconvenience to the patient.  Furthermore, the cost associated with treating diabetic patients is relatively high, costing around $174 billion in 2007 according to the American Diabetics Association. The high costs are caused by the manner in which treatment is given to patients. Blood sugar monitors, pills, and shots all have to be constantly given to a patient, which results in an effective, but costly treatment plan.

    However, with a new discovery from NASA, there is a unique and fascinating solution to this problem. Currently being developed by Dr. David Loftus, the NASA biocapsule is an indestructible carbon nanotube that stores and administers treatment based on the varying concentrations of cells in the body, maintaining homeostasis in the body. The nanotube was created to aid astronauts who are unable to receive treatment for radiation sickness at the International Space Station. If the invention is approved for use by NASA, the nanotube will house a hormone called G-CSF (Granulocyte colony-stimulating factor) to treat the effects of radiation in an astronaut's body. The G-CSF hormone is already used on earth to treat cancer patients that are suffering from radiation sickness.

    The biocapsules are composed of a carbon polymer that is nontoxic and “indestructible” even in the harsh environments of space. The release of a protein or drug is controlled by a “protein cap” that unfolds or folds based on the amount of protein that attaches to it, allowing the drug to be administered. For example, in diabetes, the cap would unfold or contract based on the amount of insulin or glucose in the bloodstream. The cap would contain a receptor to which the the insulin or glucose would bind. The cap would possibly contain insulin supplements that would be released when glucose in the body has been bound to it. The capsule's receptor could be tailored to the disease it is trying to treat. For example, in diabetes, it would be fitted with a receptor structure to which insulin could bind. In addition, the fact that these caps are not permeable means that they can be removed from the human body without serious harm if the capsule needs to be altered.

    Dr. Loftus's invention has already been patented and is currently being tested on lab mice for any malfunctions or problems with the device. According to Dr. Loftus, a few safety issues still need to be resolved that deal specifically with the capsule's inability to be denatured by enzymes found in the body. Since the nanotube is indestructible, the cells it releases can have reign over a patient’s bloodstream, causing problems with the immune system if the “protein cap” release system is not properly controlled. The protein cap can be denatured through high temperatures or attack by chemical compounds. This can be a serious problem as a denatured protein loses its original function. In the case of the protein cap no longer acting as control mechanism for the nanotube, dangerous amounts of chemicals can be released. 

    At its core level, this is an alternative delivery system for antibiotics. While this might not seem any different from a regular pill, the biocapsule allows for healthy cells to be administered in a patient's body that can eventually cure the disease. This could revolutionize the ability for the body to heal and change the manner in which drugs are administered. Now instead of taking shots and pills constantly, this one pill can supply a constant stream of antibiotics based on the state of the body.  The advantages are obvious. These biocapsules are a form of constant drug administration that removes the inconveniences and dangers of over the counter drugs.
