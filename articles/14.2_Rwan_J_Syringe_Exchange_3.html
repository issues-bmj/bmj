continued from Syringe Exchange Programs


   Joseph Guydish, PhD, MPH, professor at the University of California, San Francisco and associate director of the California-Arizona node of the National Institute of Drug Abuse Clinical Trials Network explains, “One reason for lack of federal support is our national approach to drug abuse and addiction, which I believe is more driven by punitive, legalistic, and criminal approaches than in other countries. In the context of a federal war on drugs, I believe there is a troublesome symbolism to federal distribution or exchange of clean syringes. So the matter is not so simple as federal funding for syringe exchange, but may require retooling decades of drug policy to frame a national drug policy approach in which funding of SEPs can exist in the policy environment.”

    Furthermore, within each state, some community members oppose SEP on the same moral basis from which they oppose drug use as an illegal and immoral activity. Popular opinion data indicates that at least half of American adults are in support of SEP, according to Robert MacCoun, PhD, professor of Law and Public Policy at the University of California, Berkeley and author of the 2001 book Drug War Heresies: Learning from Other Vices, Times, and Places. MacCoun warns, however, that “those who are opposed are far more passionate about their opposition. Supporters are more likely to display what Mark Kleiman [PhD, Director of the Drug Policy Analysis Program at the University of California, Los Angeles] calls ‘grudging tolerance.’ They aren’t enthusiastic about needled exchange; they just recognize it might be necessary.”

    Additionally, new evidence suggests that SEP may actually encourage, rather than prevent, drug injection behavior in unsafe locations. In a 2006 Substance Use & Misuse article, Kate Ksobiech, PhD, of Marquette University, notes that clean needles alone do not appear to be sufficient motivation to change high-risk behaviors. In fact, Ksobiech finds that SEP participants are more likely to engage in risky behaviors such as injecting drugs at shooting galleries than non-participants. Although the slight increase in risky injection behavior was the only negative outcome in an analysis of five categories of effectiveness, this finding can be taken as support for the federal stance of refusing to fund SEP.

    The lack of federal funding has dampened the reach and effectiveness of SEP in injection drug user communities. Even with state and local support, many programs cannot meet the demand for supplies, affecting their reliability in the community as supplies run short due to lack of funding. Matthew LeComte, a needle exchange volunteer in San Francisco, worries about the detrimental public health consequences if the shortage of supplies continues. “If you flood the community with free, clean needles, then injection drug users will use them unsparingly. However, if the supply of syringes is low due to lack of funding, then users will reuse and share needles, effectively returning to the pre-SEP days of high-risk behaviors.”

    From lowered transmission rates of HIV and hepatitis C virus to cessation of injecting drugs, most research indicates that SEPs are an effective means to reducing harmful behaviors in injection drug users. Nonetheless, these programs cannot mollify the unquantifiable fears of crime and moral degeneration that are invoked by the idea of injection drug use. This fear, combined with lack of federal funding, has proven to be a significant barrier to widespread adoption of syringe exchanges. The future of these programs lies in the ability of the proponents and detractors alike to balance the public health benefits with the broader moral implications of continued illegal drug use.
