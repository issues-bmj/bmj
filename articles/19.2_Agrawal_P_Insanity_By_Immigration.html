Insanity by Immigration


Puja Agrawal

Spring 2012


    The United States of America is known to be a “melting pot.” Approximately 12% of the population is foreign born, thus making recent findings of a correlation between the onset of psychosis and age of immigration strikingly important. And in the face of growing international economies, these findings are also significant for those considering emigrating.

    Psychotic disorders are defined as illnesses such as schizophrenia, where the distinction between real and unreal becomes blurred and emotional and social situations become difficult, bipolar disorder, where one varies between mania and depression, or delusional disorder, where one experiences delusions that are not completely impossible and does not suffer from any emotional disorder or have hallucinations. In December 2011, Dr. Wim Veling, Dr. Hans Hoek, Dr. Jean-Paul Selton, and Dr. Ezra Susser of the Parnassia Psychiatric Institute in the Netherlands published their findings of the relationship between immigrant age and psychosis in The Hague, the capital of South Holland, Netherlands. A previous study of the UK and the Netherlands showed that Africa Caribbean immigrants in the UK have four times the risk of developing a psychotic disorder and that there is an elevated risk for Moroccan, Surinamese, and Antillean migrants in the Netherlands. This study aimed to relate the age of migration with the risk of developing a psychotic disorder.

    Their study spanned seven years and focused on migrants between the ages of 15 to 54 who had made contact with a physician for the first time regarding psychosis. Those who showed symptoms before migration were not considered, and the patients were natives of four countries: Surinam, Netherland Antilles, Turkey, and Morocco.  After developing a detailed medical history of each patient illustrating the illness’s progression, the researches obtained migration age data from municipal authorities and used a statistical analysis program and a chi-square test. They found that a younger age at time of migration is associated with a higher risk of psychosis with those who immigrated at ages 0 to 4 being the most at risk. Additionally, risk was highest in those who did not move by their own choice.

    The effect of age on the probability of developing psychosis may be due to the length of the risk period immigrants have. An individual’s risk period is from birth until the late teens, during which particular events and experiences can increase the individual’s susceptibility to psychotic disorders. In opposition to previous theories of being genetically predisposed for psychosis, Veling et al. found that parents of Moroccan patients did not have a higher risk than those of Dutch patients, supporting the findings found previously with African Caribbean parents and patients in the UK. Another theory is that perhaps these individuals migrate because children are showing symptoms of psychosis. However, Veling et al. argue that it is unlikely for a parent of a child five years old or younger will see these symptoms. Overall, their findings support the idea that psychosis develops from a young age. It is possible that there is childhood trauma, from parental separation during the move, to simply the process of moving. There can also be problems with language and assimilating to a new culture, leading to social exclusion that can amplify the risk of developing psychosis. Social exclusion within the community and even within the family can potentially influence a child’s dopamine levels, a neurotransmitter directly associated with schizophrenia. Additionally, viral infections and Vitamin D deficiency have been seen to be additional factors influencing the probability of developing psychosis, and Veling et al. mention recent findings that show that high and low vitamin D levels are associated with schizophrenia.

    While it would be best to study the effects of immigration and psychosis in each individual county, it is difficult because census data does not take into account an individual’s age of migration in most places. However, it has been found that in the United States, there is a relationship between age of migration and mood and anxiety disorders. Although these are not directly related to psychosis, they are at least related to psychiatric disorders. The United States is a country of different ethnicities, and these findings shed an interesting and important light on immigration and mental health.
