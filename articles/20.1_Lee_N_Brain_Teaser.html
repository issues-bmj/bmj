Not Your Average Brain Teaser

Brain Parasites: A Growing Health Concern in California


Nicole Lee

Fall 2012


    Suffer from headaches? Perhaps it's not the chronic stress and tension from a busy lifestyle.  Perhaps it's not from the annoying screeching of loud neighbors.  And perhaps it's not from sleep deprivation or dehydration, or any of those commonly prescribed causes for headaches.  Maybe, there are worms in your brain.


    Sara Alverez from Sunnyvale, California was experiencing precisely that problem.  While she was in the middle of a stroll in the park, numbness suddenly seized her right leg, and then the rest of her body.  The numbness was soon followed by an incapacitating weakness. Her husband drove her to the Redwood City  Hospital that night.  There, doctors madly scrambled to determine whether she had cancer or tumors, only to discover her problem was far more unorthodox.


    It was a CT scan that revealed what was wrong with her.  Alverez was diagnosed with neurocysticercosis- a condition in which calcified tapeworms had become nested in her brain.  Although neurocysticercosis is pretty common in third world countries, it is not exactly seen as a major health issue in the United States.  Because of this, there is a lack of proper education regarding this malady.  When diagnosed in its early stages, it can easily and inexpensively treated with steroids.  Putting treatment off, however, can be deadly to both your brain cells and your wallet.  The Center for Disease Control and Prevention states that hospitalizations due to neurocysticercosis cost an average of $37,600.


    According to Mollie Bloudoff-Indelicato, journalist for the Scientific American Journal,  Alverez most likely became infected by eating pork contaminated with tapeworm larvae.  The tapeworm larvae use their incisor-like teeth to tear into the soft, fleshy intestinal walls.  Hooking themselves into their host, they then commence in draining nutrients from the body.  As horrifying as this sounds, tapeworms at this stage are still relatively harmless.  It’s when they begin to reproduce that complications start to arise.


    The adult tapeworm body structure is composed of hundreds of segments called proglottids.  Older proglottids contain thousands of eggs, that will regularly be excreted from the host’s anus.  When other people are infected with tapeworm eggs, the eggs hatch into larvae, and remain in that developmental stage.  Instead of staying in the intestines, tapeworm larvae float along the bloodstream like tiny demented terrorists in a lazy river, using their hooks to rip flesh apart in their search for richer nutrients.  Eventually, some manage to gain access to the brain.  Researchers still aren’t sure exactly how the tapeworm larvae are able to traverse the blood-brain barrier.  Some believe that the larvae are able to secrete a type of enzyme that dissolves a part of the barrier.  In any case, while this is all going on, their human host is usually blissfully ignorant of the proceedings.


    The reason for this is that while they are alive, tapeworm larvae secrete a thin, mucus-like fluid that tricks the immune system into thinking everything is fine and dandy.  Once the larvae die, the immune system suddenly realizes that there are intruders in the brain, and attacks the dead larvae, calcifying them.  This is when the host starts to experience headaches, seizures, and paralysis.  By this stage, surgery is often necessary.


    Unfortunately, according to the Scientific American Journal, many physicians are unaware of this disease, causing treatments to be largely postponed.  Latinos, the group most majorly affected by this disease, do not receive education about this dangerous organism either, and as a result, often do not know what is going on when they start experiencing the symptoms.  Due to lack of funding, states have not been able to fully address neurocysticercosis as a medical issue, which is the main factor in why there is so little awareness about this insidious threat to our health.


    So, if you are a sufferer of chronic migraines, not to cause alarm (though if the thought of brain-eating parasites isn’t a legitimate cause for alarm, I’m not sure what would be), but perhaps a visit to the doctor wouldn’t hurt.
