continued from Cheers to Resveratrol


    These findings were equally promising when Patel examined resveratrol in human cells.  Brown incubated two cultures of human colorectal cells: one with the resveratrol metabolite mixture and one with just resveratrol. Patel found that the metabolized forms of resveratrol successfully regenerated the resveratrol compound within the cells. The human cells were seen to transform the metabolites into the parent form in an intracellular reservoir. This finding suggests that resveratrol could be regenerated with specific molecular targets. This radical discovery illuminates huge possibilities for the application of resveratrol as a form of medical treatment.


    This thorough examination of resveratrol suggests that the utilization of the metabolite forms of resveratrol might be more beneficial in medical applications than the parent form itself. The ingestion of the resveratrol mixture bypasses potential toxicity concerns due to the metabolites chemical stability, while the metabolites’ ability to target specific cells within which to regenerate the parent resveratrol compound increases the vast potential of the compound to be used in a medical context.


    Patel and others are continuing to pursue research examining resveratrol Ketan Patel Ph.D, the primary researcher, notes that more research on the “safety and tolerability and further assessment of the long-term toxicity of resveratrol is needed” will have to be completed prior to releasing resveratrol as an official medical treatment; however, it soon could be.


    This study, as well as others, has illuminated the vast potential of resveratrol. Although, resveratrol is found in red wine, don’t fall for the illusion that drinking red wine will provide the benefits of resveratrol, as studies have suggested that alcohol consumption increases one’s cancer risk. Perhaps red wine does not live up to its historical hype. However, resveratrol has proven its vast potential.
