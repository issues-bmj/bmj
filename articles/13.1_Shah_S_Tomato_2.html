continued from I Say Tomato, You Say Cancer Prevention


   Dr. Blum also examined the differences in the apoptotic indices between the two groups. Apoptosis refers to cellular suicide (i.e. the neat death of damaged, cancerous or unnecessary cells) in response to chemical signals within the cell itself or from neighboring cells; the apoptotic index is a measure of the degree of apoptosis a particular tissue has undergone. Dr. Blum found that the apoptotic index was significantly higher in patients who consumed the lycopene-rich diet, further strengthening the claim that lycopene plays an important role in cancer prevention.

    Though much remains to be discovered about lycopene’s exact cancer-preventing mechanism, research suggests that it affects cell-to-cell gap junction communications (GJC). Gap junctions are channels between neighboring cells which allow for the transport of low molecular weight compounds (e.g. chemical signals and nutrients). Whereas normal cells have normal GJC, tumorous cells lack a fully functional GJC and thus are more prone to undergo apoptosis. A study conducted in 2003 by Olivier Aust, PhD, of the Heinrich-Heine-University in Dusseldorf, Germany sheds light on the exact role played by lycopene in regulating GJC. Once absorbed and degraded, lycopene’s components were found to either activate healthy GJC or inactivate dysfunctional GJC, thereby signaling tumor cells to undergo apoptosis.

    Cancer prevention is not the only benefit tomatoes can provide. Another study conducted by Dr. Blum shows that lycopene helps decrease the amount of low density lipoprotein cholesterol in the body by enhancing the rate of cholesterol oxidation. This ultimately leads to an increased rate and efficiency of cholesterol degradation. The Antioxidant Supplementation in the Atherosclerosis Preventions study, which followed 520 asymptomatic men and women, found that low plasma levels of lycopene correlated with an 18 percent increase, on average, in the thickness of on aspect of the carotid artery wall in men. Without lycopene in a typical diet, one can expect higher levels of cholesterol and ultimately clogged arteries.

    Dr. Pedersen often jokingly tells his organic chemistry students that “a slice of pizza a day helps keep free radicals at bay.” We now know that this and much more is true because of the lycopene in processed tomato pizza sauce. While sauces, juices and condiments like ketchup are simple and easily obtainable sources of lycopene, Dr. Sapuntzakis emphasizes that “the safest way to increase lycopene intake is by eating a wide variety of fruits and vegetables, including mixed dishes with tomato products, which are likely to provide an optimal mix of carotenoids and other phytochemicals.” So do not go running off to the nearest pizzeria just yet–there are healthier ways to increase lycopene intake.
