When Patient Health Information Leaks


Lauren Mock

Fall 2012


    Other than the necessity to complete administrative forms, the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA) has generally been invisible to consumers of health services, although healthcare workers may suffer from the bureaucracy and policies needed to comply with its provisions.


    HIPAA was passed by Congress and signed into law by President Bill Clinton in 1996. Among its provisions, the act comprehensively and uniformly safeguards patient privacy and upholds security of patient information. Eight years later, the first conviction for a HIPAA privacy violation occurred in an identity theft case, when in 2004, a Seattle Cancer Care Alliance employee used patient information to fraudulently obtain credit cards. Still, HIPAA would remain an esoteric acronym until the exposure of private information of several well-known celebrities.


    The public and media awakened to HIPAA when in 2005, employees at Santa Monica-UCLA Medical Center and Orthopedic Hospital were "caught prying into records” of pop music star Britney Spears after her maternity stay. Another hospitalization in 2008 again tempted employees to access Spears' non-psychiatric medical records. Other revelations included violations of patient data concerning television star Farrah Fawcett, the former California first lady Maria Shriver, and dozens of other patients.


    Though some violations of HIPAA are maliciously willful, much leakage of private information is accidental. The wide range of communications available to parties throughout various healthcare entities exposes significant risks. Familiar sources of information leakage may include exposed documents on desks, uncollected output from fax machines and printers, improperly disposed documents, or a lost laptop or USB memory card or stick. With the popularity of social networking, the potential for HIPAA violations is magnified through personal blogs of healthcare workers or information posted in Facebook or other social networking sites. Even an activity as mundane as discussing in public areas information regarding a patient may represent a significant source of information leakage.


    While the original HIPAA civil penalties were relatively small, the more recent Health Information Technology for Economic and Clinical Health (HITECH) Act, enacted as part of the American Recovery and Reinvestment Act of 2009 upped civil penalties to $50,000 per violation, with an annual maximum of $1.5 million.  Further, the U.S. Department of Justice (DOJ) also clarified in 2005 that violators can be held criminally liable, in extreme cases with imprisonment for up to ten years.


    Following two separate complaints filed by two celebrity patients against the UCLA Health System (UCLAHS), the Department of Health and Human Services (HHS) Office for Civil Rights (OCR), finally entered into a resolution agreement with UCLAHS, fining the organization $865,500 and securing their agreement for corrective action to fix "gaps in its compliance" with HIPAA's privacy and security rules.


    Under the Obama administration, the HHS and OCR have become more active in its pursuit of HIPAA violation complaints. This past February, the OCR fined Cignet Health $4.3 million in civil penalties for numerous violations including its inability to provide patient records within 60 days to patients who requested them, and its failure to cooperate with the subsequent investigation. The Obama administration announced this as the largest fine for a violation under HIPAA.


    Escalating penalties have not yet stemmed the tide of information breaches. Just this September, TRICARE, the Defense Department’s healthcare program recently reported possibly the largest health information breach in HIPAA history with 4.9 million San Antonio area military hospital and clinic patients impacted by the loss of data on backup tapes.


    Although more violations will be exposed through audits and elevated scrutiny, healthcare organizations have been training employees on proper information handling and investing in procedures and technologies to secure information and communications. Through growing public and industry awareness, security of patient information and safeguarding of patient privacy continues to improve in compliance with HIPAA.
