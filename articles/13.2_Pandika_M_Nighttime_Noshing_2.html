continued from Nighttime Noshing OK


   Cameron’s ﬁndings agree with earlier studies that reported a low correlation between nighttime eating and subsequent weight gain. A ten-year study of over 7,000 people by Queens College of the City University of New York published in May 1997 found that the extent of evening eating was not a signiﬁcant predictor of long- term weight gain. Another study by Queens College in 1995 on
1,802 women between the ages of 19 and 50 found that body mass index (BMI) was not associated with percent energy of evening food intake. Moreover, a 1990 study by the Faculté de Medecine X in Paris, France on the relationship between circadian distribution of energy intake and obesity found that obese subjects did not consume higher proportions of their daily caloric intake during dinner than non-obese subjects. Instead, obese subjects had a higher total daily caloric intake than their non-obese counterparts.

    Despite these ﬁndings, there is still room for skepticism. Several studies have shown, for instance, that obese individuals consume a greater proportion of calories at night than their leaner counterparts. A 1988 study conducted at the University of Paris reported that obese and overweight children ate less at breakfast and more at dinner than their leaner peers. Furthermore, a 2000 study by the New York Obesity Research Center found that night shift workers ate later and gained more weight on average than those who worked during the day.

    More recently, research has focused on sleep deprivation and nighttime levels of ghrelin, a hormone produced by the stomach that stimulates appetite in response to hunger. Ghrelin concentrations in the blood follow a daily cycle, reaching high concentrations at night, possibly explaining the association between sleep deprivation and weight gain observed in large populations. In the December 2004
Wisconsin Sleep Cohort Study, Stanford University researchers examined 1,000 individuals, measuring each person’s sleep habits as well as ghrelin levels. The study found that decreased sleep was inversely proportional to BMI levels and that ghrelin levels were 14.9 percent higher in individuals who slept ﬁve hours or less per night compared to those who slept eight hours per night.

    Nonetheless, Cameron’s ﬁndings are substantial and have profound implications for a nation in which obesity has reached epidemic proportions and more individuals are obsessed with losing weight than ever. This study is particularly useful because it addresses the major problem with obesity research today: ambiguity in reporting just how often human participants actually eat and exercise. In primate studies, however, subjects are constantly under the watchful eyes of researchers and their food may be pre-measured, allowing for greater experimental control. Cameron plans to implement her primate model in future research on factors leading to individual diﬀerences in weight gain among people who consume high-calorie diets, such as diﬀerences in metabolic rate, physical activity, and absorption of nutrients from the gastrointestinal tract.
