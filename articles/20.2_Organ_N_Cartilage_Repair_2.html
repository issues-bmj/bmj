   Of course, the recent study dealt only with fish. It doesn’t show what the effects of these trace amounts of drugs are on humans – if they have any effects at all. Professional opinions vary on this. Dr. Karl Fent of University of Applied Sciences Northwestern Switzerland states that “drinking water contamination by pharmaceuticals seems not to be critical.” Dr. Christian Daughton of the Environmental Protection Agency, on the other hand, sees matters differently. He provided direction to an article of his, published in 2010 in the ACS Symposium Series, in which he states: “That [active pharmaceutical ingredients] can occur in drinking water certainly poses complex questions regarding the significance of long-term human exposure. Although the minute concentrations when compared with therapeutic doses appear to be far below those that might pose any health concerns, the possibility of delayed-onset health effects cannot yet be ruled out.”


    Perhaps the real threat comes not from drinking the tap water, but in eating fish who have lived in contaminated water. If the medications become concentrated in the bodies of fish, you may be getting a higher dose than you realize when you eat these fish. The Swedish researchers led by T. Brodin found that “The concentration of oxazepam in muscle tissue of European perch (Perca fluviatilis) from River Fyris was more than six times that in the water.”


    Another unknown is what effect various other drugs have on fish. Researchers in this study tested only anti-anxiety drugs, but quite a few others have been found in our tap water – such as the sex hormones in San Francisco. These other medications found in tap water may have even more profound effects on fish – or they may have no effects at all. At this point, we simply do not know. We also do not yet know whether trace amounts of these other drugs have any impact on us humans at all. Dr. Fent even states that “the interpretation of the behavioral changes [of the fish] might be somehow exaggerated.”


    At this point, unfortunately, there is not a clear solution to this situation of pharmaceutical-contaminated tap water. You could avoid tap water entirely in favor of bottled water. However, some bottled water is simply glorified, filtered tap water (and there is no reason to think the filtering process would remove the medications). For that matter, if you are one of the many people who try to avoid eating or drinking from plastic containers, this option may be even less appealing than drinking contaminated tap water. Perhaps, continuing UC Berkeley’s long history of protests and driving change, the best solution is to pressure water treatment plants to develop more sophisticated technology that can handle the task of removing these medications from the water.


    With all the pollution in our world – from the day-to-day like smog to the occasional orders to stay indoors after an accident at the Chevron refinery in Richmond – a bit of medication in your tap water may be the least of your worries. And hey, maybe the anti-anxiety drugs will even help your mood… at least, if you’re lucky enough to have those particular drugs in your water.
