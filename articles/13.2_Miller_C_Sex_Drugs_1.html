Sex, Drugs, and ... Surgery?


Second-hand exposure to opioids in the operating room

Courtney Miller

Spring 2006


    Everyone knows that being a surgeon entails late nights, long hours, and high amounts of stress – but few would expect opiate addiction to be included in the job description. Nonetheless, a pioneering study spearheaded by Priscilla McAuliﬀe, MD, PhD, of the University of Florida’s School of Medicine, suggests just that. As reported in the January 2006 edition of Medical Hypotheses, McAuliﬀe and colleagues examined the relationship be- tween drug exposure in the operating room and the development of substance abuse in physicians who were exposed to drugs. They found that surgeons and anesthesiologists are at a much higher risk for opiate addiction after second-hand exposure to the medications fentanyl, which removes all sensation, and propofol, which eases pain. The investigators found trace amounts of the drugs in the air of the operating rooms, patient’s lungs, and the spaces above the boxes holding the drug-containing syringes. Is this the missing link between surgeons and opiate addiction, or is the addiction simply a product of high stress and easy drug accessibility?

    According to the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders, 4th edition, well over 100,000 individuals across the United States are dealing with opiate addiction at this time. Physicians and those in the medical ﬁeld are especially challenged with addiction problems. McAuliﬀe reports that approximately 7.6 percent of all physicians in Florida have undergone treatment for substance abuse since 1980. This is an incredibly high proportion, especially considering that it does not even include those who remain untreated.

    Past studies have linked several factors with the high prevalence of addiction among physicians: high occupational stress, easy access to medications, and inappropriate self-medication. While it was previously thought that exhaled drugs were non-detectable and did not cause addiction, McAuliﬀe’s new study suggests that experts may need to consider an alternative view: second-hand exposure and inadvertent sensitization to opiates in operating rooms may cause addiction.

    First, McAuliﬀe and her colleagues compiled data from addiction clinics in the state of Florida, focusing on drugs more prone to being abused and recording the medical specialties of enrolled physicians. They found that in 2002, 150 Florida-based physicians began treatment for addiction. Though they account for only ﬁve percent of the state’s licensed physicians, twelve percent of those physicians undergoing substance abuse therapy were anesthesiologists or surgeons. The researchers also discovered that 25 percent of the substances abused by these physicians were opiates. When considering surgeons alone, the proportion of opiate abuse skyrockets to an astonishing 40 percent. These ﬁndings satisfy one important criteria of the second-hand exposure theory: because surgeons and anesthesiologists spend more time in the operating room than other specialists, it is not surprising that they have a higher risk of developing opiate addiction.

    In fact, of those physicians who reported being addicted to fentanyl, 94.4 percent were surgeons and anesthesiologists. McAuliﬀe and colleagues suggest that the bodies of physicians were likely primed to the drug through inadvertent second-hand exposure to opiate medications. As David Presti, PhD, professor of Neurobiology at the University of California, Berkeley, explains, “Withdrawal could have more devastating eﬀects than the actual drug if someone has become dependent on it. For me personally, I wouldn’t want my surgeon operating on me while on opiates, but I wouldn’t want him going through withdrawal either.”
