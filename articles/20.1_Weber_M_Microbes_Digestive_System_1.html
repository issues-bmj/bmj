Microbes in the Digestive System


Merrill Weber

Fall 2012

   
    "I get by with a little help from my friends," sang the sensation band, The Beatles. More so than ever, this statement rings true as scientists have found that people have millions of "friends", or microbes, living inside them which help their bodies do things such as absorb nutrients and balance the immune system. Due to the incredible abundance of microbial life in the human body, scientists are hard at work figuring out exactly how these microbes interact with the body. However, at the University Of North Carolina School Of Medicine, a recent study shed light on a particular microbe that lives within the digestive system and increases the absorption of calories. While an increased absorption of calories can be beneficial in some cases, in many it is not. The discovery of a microbe that contributes to this condition has the potential to alter the way obesity is treated and prevented in modern-day medicine.

     Obesity, its causes, and what can be done to prevent it have come to the forefront of society since the number of people suffering from this condition has skyrocketed in the past twenty years. Doctors and scientists have performed countless studies to diagnose the causes of weight gain and obesity. They have made connections between weight gain and diet, sleep patterns, and exercise habits, but there are assuredly more to be found. For example, few have investigated microbes as a factor in obesity. However, the digestive system is the perfect place to start when looking into a disease that revolves around food and how the body uses it. When people eat, their food goes through a complex tour of the human body. From the esophagus in the throat, to the stomach and through both the small intestine and the large intestine, there are many places for microbes to live and collect calories from the passing food.  This means more opportunities to glean the maximum nutrients from food, which are stored in the body as fat. The presence of microbes that specialize in absorbing the maximum amount of calories from a minimal amount of food could be another factor of obesity. Perhaps, as long as this microbe is in the body, exponentially increasing an obese person’s calorie intake, the person would have much greater difficulty losing weight and would be more likely to remain either obese or overweight.

    At the University Of North Carolina School Of Medicine, a team of scientists have been performing experiments to investigate microbial function within the digestive system. It was already known that microbes assisted with the digestion of complex carbohydrates, which make up sixty percent of an average person’s daily carbohydrate consumption. The way they helped to metabolize the fat harvested from these complex carbohydrates (which are strings of sugar molecules) has remained unknown until now. In a study led by Ivana Semova, a team of researchers fed fatty acids to baby zebrafish. These fatty acids were tagged with fluorescent dye, which could be tracked as they traveled through the zebrafish, which are transparent at a young age. While some fish were fed these fatty acids daily, others were denied food as a comparison. The fish that were fed fatty acids developed a higher concentration of microbes in their digestive system; hence, the researchers identified more fluorescent fatty acids absorbed into digestive cells. The fish that were denied food had significantly less fatty acid in their digestive cells than did the others, which showed the researchers that this microbe’s presence in the body is based on diet. The researchers were able to identify this microbe as a part of the phylum Firmicutes, species Exiguobacterium. Because the specific organism responsible for the increase in fat absorption is known, scientists have been able to do more research in different species to see just how prominent Firmicutes bacteria are. Similar tests have been executed with mice, rather than fish, as the subject. Like the zebrafish, the mice fed fattier diets had a more abundant Firmicutes population in their digestive system than those mice fed low-fat diets. They also gained more weight in fat, which reinforces the correlation between the presence of Firmicutes and weight gain. In humans, those suffering from obesity have also been documented to have more Firmicutes bacteria than individuals of good health.
