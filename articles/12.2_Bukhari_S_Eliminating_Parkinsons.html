Eliminating Parkinson's Disease


The cure may be a cup of coffee away

Syed Bukhari

Spring 2005


    As children, we were warned that coffee would stunt our growth, and as young adults, we are still told that coffee is deleterious to our health. When compared to tea, coffee’s reputation is anything short of healthy, but prospects may soon change for this dubious drink.

    According to a new study conducted by the American Medical Association, coffee may offer protection against Parkinson’s disease. This disease, caused by the degeneration of neurons in the substantia nigra region of the brain, afflicts approximately one million Americans. Neuronal degeneration causes the levels of the neurotransmitter dopamine to fall, and muscle control is greatly hindered.

    Researchers in the Honolulu Heart Program, who studied the effects of coffee on patients, conducted experiments with coffee consumption and the occurrence of Parkinson’s disease. The test subjects consisted of 8,004 Japanese-American men who were studied over the span of 30 years. Of those studied, 102 eventually developed Parkinson’s. However, the incidence of the disease was substantially lower in those individuals who drank 28 ounces of coffee regularly. Men who drank the most coffee had the smallest chances of getting Parkinson’s disease, and those who had not consumed any coffee were five times more likely to show symptoms of the disease.

    How does coffee specifically reduce the risk of Parkinson’s disease? Coffee consists of caffeine, a type of xanthine neurotransmitter. Adenosine is another xanthine neurotransmitter used in the brain. As soon as adenosine receptors get blocked, as is the case with Parkinson’s disease, the level of dopamine neurotransmitters in the brain fluctuates. Caffeine may protect against Parkinson’s by out-competing dopamine for blocking adenosine receptors and increasing levels of free dopamine within the brain. However, there is a trade-off: coffee consumption causes caffeine to bind to the adenosine receptor. Reducing caffeine intake makes the body oversensitive to adenosine. As a result, blood vessels in the brain dilate, causing an excessive amount of blood to flow to the head and blood pressure to quickly drop, leading to the infamous “caffeine withdrawal” headache.

    As ideal as it may sound, to state that caffeine will eliminate Parkinson’s disease would be a stretch. Many outside factors have the possibility of biasing the data. For instance, certain individuals who enjoy the taste of coffee could be genetically predisposed to resist the neuronal degeneration that causes Parkinson’s. Furthermore, in the research conducted by the Honolulu Heart Program, only Japanese men were studied. The effects of coffee on women or other ethnic groups have yet to be understood. Although concrete evidence in the near future might support the claim that caffeine eliminates Parkinson’s, this does not mean that individuals should consume large amounts of coffee in hopes of avoiding the disease.
