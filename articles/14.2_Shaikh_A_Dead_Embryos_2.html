continued from Dead Embryos Give Life


   Assuming that living embryonic stem cells can be harvested from arrested embryos, the ethical debate may soon be over – for some. Nevertheless, many assert that rather than ameliorating the ethical debate, the recent conclusions of Stojkovic and associates have raised challenging philosophical questions. First of all, how do we know that the embryo is really “dead?” Ben A. Rich, JD, PhD, an associate professor of bioethics at the University of California, Davis, compares this issue to the concept of brain death. He says that, “Although we have a set of critical criteria to determine if all brain function has ceased and we call that individual dead, we want absolute certainty, which is not available.” Even though the death of an embryo is defined as its inability to undergo cell division after a period of 24 to 48 hours, there is no way to conclusively prove that the embryos Stojkovic used would stop growing if they were implanted into a woman’s uterus. After all, there is always the possibility that artificial laboratory conditions may have stumped embryological development.

    An interesting implication that stems from this argument questions the morality of intentionally discarding embryos that could potentially be of great benefit to human health. According to Erich H. Loewy, MD, professor and founding chair of bioethics at the University of California, Davis, although an embryo may have the ability to proliferate its cells and thus “be alive,” it may not “have a life.” He comments, “Being alive is a biological statement that applies to tissue cultures, spinach, amoebas, dogs, and college students, while having a life means possessing self-awareness, an idea that there was a past, there is now a present, and there may be a tomorrow.” Being alive is the necessary condition for having a life, but as yet it is merely a potential. Further, since stem cell therapy has shown a host of uses in dealing with a number of illnesses, it may be argued that failure to experiment with stem cells from an embryo destined to be discarded is unethical. Hence, some may contend that it is immoral to refuse to carry out experiments that could revolutionize medicine.

    It is also possible that the new findings may have little or no impact on those that regard research on living human embryos as an unethical practice to begin with. As Roberta S. Loewy, PhD, associate clinical professor of bioethics at the University of California, Davis says, “Those who take the most polarized anti- human embryo destruction stance will not be swayed by the claim that the embryos are now dead or arrested.” Instead, it is likely that the “pro-life absolutist” position will continue to argue that collecting excess embryos even for the purpose of in vitro fertilization is in and of itself unethical.

    No longer just an idea, the capacity of “dead” embryos to yield living stem cells has been discovered, bringing us one step closer to creating artificial tissue for transplantation. Still, further inquiry and examination must be made before new treatments for widespread diseases like diabetes and Parkinson’s are developed. Unfortunately, with such power comes immense responsibility, leaving the debate over the ethics of stem cell research alive and well.
