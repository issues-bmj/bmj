continued from Fatherhood and Testosterone


    A study on Vietnam War Air Force veterans supports the notion of a fluctuation of testosterone levels rather than a single and permanent decline. Conducted by Allan Mazur of Syracuse University and Joel Michalek of the Brooks Air Force Base Armstrong Laboratory, this study found that men’s testosterone levels dropped during marriage, but rose again in the event of a divorce.

    Additionally, the Proceedings study likened the human testosterone cycle to that of birds and select other mammals. Furthermore, in 1994, penguin experts John Cockrem of Massey University and Philip Seddon of the University of Otago conducted a study in New Zealand showing that penguins experience an annual testosterone cycle. For male penguins, testosterone levels are high while the penguins are mating but are drastically reduced when the male begins his parenting roles. This too is evidence that testosterone levels fluctuate depending on the role of the male.

    Authors of the study hope their findings will help redefine masculinity to include parenthood. Co-author Lee Gettler told the New York Times, “This should be viewed as, ‘Oh it’s great, women aren’t the only ones biologically adapted to be parents.’” He went on to explain, “Humans give birth to incredibly dependent infants. Historically, the idea that men were out clubbing babies has been largely discredited. The only way mothers could have highly needy offspring every couple of years is if they were getting help.”

    This study has raised many questions as well. For instance, are older men who use testosterone gel or patches more likely to find new partners? Are they less likely to maintain involved roles as fathers? But the potential applications of the study do not stop at the social and behavioral. Conditions such as prostate cancer and heart disease have been linked to long-term elevated testosterone levels in the same way that breast cancer is associated with higher estrogen levels in women. While fatherhood could not be directly used as a means of treatment or prevention, the connection between paternity and testosterone deepens scientists’ and doctors’ understanding of the hormone and its behavior.
