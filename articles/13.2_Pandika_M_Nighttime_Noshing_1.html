Nighttime Noshing OK


Midnight snacks might not be so bad afterall

Melissa Pandika

Spring 2006

   
    Rest assured, late-night eaters: recent primate studies reveal that midnight snacking does not promote weight gain.

    It is a widely-accepted belief that consuming food late at night leads to an increase in weight. Incidentally, many popular diet regimens recommend limiting nighttime caloric intake because the metabolic rate of humans as well as other animals decreases during periods of sleep.

    According to researchers at the Oregon National Primate Research Center in Beaverton, this seemingly logical age-old belief is largely ﬁction. In December 2005, a research team headed by Judy L. Cameron, PhD, evaluated the percentage of daily calories consumed at night by sixteen female rhesus monkeys placed on a weight gain regimen for one year. Since increased caloric consumption and weight gain have been associated with ovariectomy—surgical excision of one or both ovaries—and adherence to a high-fat diet in numerous species, Cameron and her colleagues ﬁrst ovariectomized the monkeys and then placed them on a high-fat diet to generate weight gain. Because some of the monkeys ate approximately 65 percent of their total daily calories at night, Cameron and her research team were able to observe the eﬀects of nighttime meals and compare the results with human eating patterns.

    The study found no correlation between nighttime caloric consumption and weight gain following the ovariectomy procedure and implementation of the high-fat diet. Monkeys consuming a large proportion of the calories at night did not exhibit an increased tendency to gain weight and were neither substantially heavier nor fatter than monkeys eating the majority of their calories in the daytime. In other words, weight gain depended more on the total caloric intake than on the time at which those calories were consumed. Moreover, the study surprisingly found no correlation between the change in the monkeys’ caloric intake and the change in body weight in response to the high-fat diet: some monkeys gained weight, some remained lean, and a few even lost weight.

    The lack of correlation between calorie consumption and body weight is due to various factors, but Cameron points primarily to activity level. Highly active monkeys did not gain weight, whereas extremely sedentary monkeys showed signiﬁcant weight gain. The results of the study suggest, then, that individuals seeking to lose weight should not rely solely on reducing evening caloric intake but should also focus on reducing overall caloric intake and increasing activity level.

    But just how applicable are the results of Cameron’s monkey study to humans? Cameron maintains that the rhesus monkey is an excellent model for humans in the genetic, physiological, and metabolic sense. According to the National Institutes of Health’s February 2006 press release on the deposit of a draft of the rhesus macaque genome sequence into free public databases, rhesus monkeys and humans share as much as 95 percent of their genes. The two species also respond similarly to high-fat and high-calorie diets, making the rhesus monkey a popular animal model for adult-onset diabetes and atherosclerosis. The monkeys used in Cameron’s research consumed 6 to 64 percent of their total calories at night, similar to the 24 to 65 percent range of nighttime caloric intake reported in humans.
