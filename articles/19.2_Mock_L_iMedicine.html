iMedicine


Lauren Mock

Spring 2012


    Shortly after the introduction of the iPad, Bernstein Research reported that about 4.5 million iPads are being sold every three months worldwide, a rate faster than the one million units the iPhone sold in its first quarter, and the 350,000 DVD players sold in the first year. At this rate, the iPad will surpass gaming hardware and the cellular phone to become the fourth largest consumer electronics category. Rapidly rising to ubiquity, it is inevitable that tablet computing will make its way into medical practices as well, though its adoption in medicine is still in its infancy, and not without barriers.

    Spyglass Consulting Group reported that 70% of doctors own Apple iPad tablets for personal use, but very few use them within their practice. Other than being an electronic version of a notepad, the major weakness of the iPad tablet is the lack of compelling clinical software that can transform patient care delivery today. Today, few electronic health record vendors offer iPad-compatible systems.

    The use of computers has been well established in the medical setting. However, the technology often goes unused, or intrudes into the doctor-patient relationship as doctors step away to click at a mouse and tap away on a complicated, non-user-friendly interface of a clunky PC while examining a patient. Data intensive health information management systems require doctors to spend much time entering detailed patient information at a computer.

    Slowing down the adoption of tablets for medical applications is a mixture of technology risk and federal regulation. In general, hospitals are reluctant to embrace any new unproven technology especially “consumer-grade” devices. Issues remain with the device and software, such as the security of information. Also, being a general-computing device, the iPad is not regulated by the U.S. Food and Drug Administration, while certain medical applications are regulated. Mobile MIM, a medical imaging app, was the first of a handful of apps to be cleared by the agency in a process that took nearly two and a half years.

    Despite the current state, much research and piloting of applications is being done to explore the use of tablet computing in the healthcare setting, often in medical schools. For example, a recent study published in the Archives of Internal Medicine reported that doctors-in-training at the University of Chicago use iPad tablets to help them become more efficient at ordering tests and procedures for their patients.

    Other technology trials have shown that tablets like the iPad tablet can replace bulky files and free physicians from workstations to look up patient information no matter where they are — at home, while traveling, or doing hospital rounds. With the tablet computer, doctors can order tests, prescribe drugs, and check medical records using a computer rather than pen and paper.  The U.S. Veterans Administration has 1,500 iPads in limited trails, most of them in its Washington, D.C. hospital. Within a year, the administration may deploy as many as 100,000 tablets across its network of 152 hospitals.

    New applications are in the pipeline. In the recent Symposium of the American Medical Informatics Association, researchers at the Indiana University School of Medicine introduced a tablet-based application that allows physicians to take a patient’s medical history. A visual interface where patients can touch an image of the human body to indicate sources and severity of pain, and future voice recognition enhancements on the user interface can further improve the human-computer-interface so that doctors do not have to type anything.

    Though the Spyglass Consulting Group survey reported current skepticism by practicing doctors, 80% of physicians nevertheless believe that the Apple iPad has a promising future in healthcare. For tablet computing, this is just a start.
