Magic Mushroom's Alteration of Blood Flow for the Treatment of Depression


Brigitte Tafarella

Spring 2012

   
    A sense of euphoria, hallucinations, the perception of geometric patterns, and an overall dream-like state are all subjective effects created by psilocybin, the naturally existing psychedelic compound found in magic mushrooms. These perceptions caused by psilocybin culminate in a spiritual, or out-of-body, experience that are said to have contributed to indigenous healing rituals. In more modern times they have been explored for their use as treatment for depression, anxiety, and alcoholism. Despite the long history of the use of magic mushrooms, little is still known about how their subjective effects are generated in the brain.


    However, recent studies have made progress in the investigation of the physiological effects of psilocybin. In November of 2011, Robin L. Carhart-Harris and researchers at Imperial College London, University of Bristol, Cardiff University, University of Oxford, and University of Copenhagen collaborated on a study that used fMRI imaging, a technology that monitors changes in blood flow to different areas of the brain while a mental process is performed. An increase in blood flow to an area correlates to increased activation in that area of the brain. The researchers monitored the changes in blood flow to different areas of the brain before, during, and after the administration of psilocybin to human subjects. A total of fifteen healthy and psychedelic-experienced subjects went through a series of trials in which they entered the fMRI machine and were either injected with a placebo solution or a moderate dosage of 2mg of psilocybin. The subjects were all monitored with a fMRI to image the switch in blood flow from normal consciousness to the psychedelic state. The subjects were also asked to rate the intensity of their subjective effects by pressing certain buttons that correlated with relative intensities throughout the duration of the scan. These rating alongside the acquired fMRI images allowed the researchers to map blood flow in the brain against the intensity of the subjective effects.


    The results from this study show that with increasing intensity of psilocybin's subjective effects, there is increased deactivation of important connector hubs in the brain, represented by up to 20% decrease in blood flow. Connector hubs are important brain networks that regulate and constrict information flow between different areas of the brain. Deactivation of connector hubs by psilocybin thus allowed for an unrestricted style of cognition.  This means that the connector hubs were prevented from weeding out and downgrading the significance of certain perceptions and emotions that the body experienced.


    The two main connector hubs that showed drastic deactivation after the administration of psilocybin were the posterior cingulate cortex (PCC), and the medial prefrontal cortex (mPFC). Both of these connector hubs have a strong function in self-referencing and the creation of our ego.  The ego satisfies our needs of identity which include the juxtaposition of self versus other, the reduction of importance of emotional stimuli and the relation of self to societal standards. Patients with depression have abnormally high activity in the mPFC, which explains why the psychopathology of depression follows intense self-focus and comparison with inner standards. Psilocybin, like the class of antidepressants called selective serotonin re-uptake inhibitors (SSRIs), lowers blood flow to the mPFC. Psilocybin and SSRIs both decrease blood flow by blocking the receptors of serotonin, a neurotransmitter that regulates the constriction of capillaries. It usually goes that if there is a decrease in blood flow to one area of the brain, there is heightened blood flow to a different area of the brain.
