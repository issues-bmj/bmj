Gene Doping: The Next Step to Cheating


Christina Lin

Fall 2012


    The advancement of technology brings not only benefits to society, but also opportunities to misuse it. One issue that has come to the public’s attention, specifically the sporting industry, is gene therapy. While the 2012 Summer Olympics in London was one of the cleanest in terms of banned substances, there is concern that any use of doping was simply undetected. On the international level, athletic competitions conduct regular, strict drug tests through either urine or blood samples. However, these methods would not detect direct changes in genes. Therefore, if athletes used gene therapy to alter their DNA to enhance athletic performances, then anti-doping experts would not detect any use of banned substances since no drug was technically used. The potential of gene therapy, or more appropriately, gene doping, has alarmed experts of the World Anti-Doping Agency (WADA).

    Potentially using gene therapy as a new form of doping was brought about by the research publications of Lee Sweeney at the University of Pennsylvania. He showed that gene manipulation was possible to genetically modify mice to increase muscles and produce insulin-like growth factor, a natural stimulant, in muscles to improve performance. The results were astounding. The mice’s muscle strength increased by at least thirty percent and allowed them to run not only faster, but also longer on treadmills, in comparison to unmodified mice. However, the effects on the human body have still not been tested or fully understood, so whether this technology can be considered as doping is an entirely different case. Dr. Sweeny even stated that the availability of this technology to humans is not for quite some time. However, research in genetically modifying mice to enhance physical abilities has continued to show promise. Two years after Dr. Sweeney’s publication, in 2006, researchers at Dartmouth College modified a liver gene to produce more AMPK, a substance boosting the concentrations of glycogen, the natural store of chemical energy in mammals, to allow genetically modified mice to not only run three times as long as that of unmodified mice, but also suffer minimal exhaustion. Two years later, in 2008, another group of researchers at University of Pennsylvania were able to once again improve GM mice performance, having them run six times further, by altering the interleukin-15R alpha gene.

    While there is much to learn about gene manipulation before it can be fully controlled, WADA is on the alert. There is no evidence that gene doping is currently being practiced, according to Professor Steve Harridge, a muscle physiology expert at King’s College London. However, as the technique becomes more refined, its illegal use in competition will become more likely. As Andy Miah, sports ethicist and director of Creative Futures Research Centre at the University of the West of Scotland, describes, “There is no guarantee that gene doping would work, but theoretically, is feasible, which piques the interests of athletes and WADA, alike.”

    The spirit of sports has always been to see who is the best in a certain field through direct, fair competition; athletes relying solely on their own skills to determine who is better. However, using illegal and unfair methods to give oneself an edge tarnishes the fairness that is supposedly present in sports. Furthermore, gene therapy is meant for medicinal purposes, to treat patients whose wellbeing is at stake. This technology is expensive and should be prioritized for patients suffering from a genetic disorder, or any disease. Gene therapy is a powerful tool that can cure numerous currently incurable diseases, but non-medical related investors have other plans for such a potent treatment.
