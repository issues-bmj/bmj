Blue Collar Medicine


Lauren Mock

Spring 2012

   
    A medical epidemic has been accelerating throughout the United States. Healthcare Finance News reported in December 2011 that all the large cardiology firms in the Columbus, Ohio area have been purchased by hospitals. NPR.org reported in October 2010 that in the Raleigh, North Carolina area, only 67 doctor practices remain that are not affiliated with a hospital, and that within the next five years, local hospital systems will have acquired the majority of them. Data from the Medical Group Management Association (MGMA) Annual Physician Compensation and Production Survey reported that as recently as 2005, more than two-thirds of medical practices were physician-owned. In only three short years, that share had fallen to below a half. Large hospital systems are rapidly absorbing private medical practices, and this phenomenon leaves an uncertain impact for both doctors and patients.

    Physicians are migrating to institutional employment for a number of reasons. Many established doctors tire of the time-consuming administrative frustrations in managing the complicated billing and reimbursement system. Many clinicians are also wary of the expensive new capital investments required for health information management systems and other healthcare technologies. These investments improve patient safety and quality of care, and have become de facto requirements for running a viable and competitive practice.    

    Attitudes are also changing – the idea of working nine to five in a hospital has become more acceptable to the new generation of doctors. Long-practicing doctors lament that younger colleagues are less willing to be on call or expend the long hours as they did when they began practicing medicine. Instead, younger physicians tend to value lifestyle balance and favor predictable hours. For many new doctors who have graduated medical school with large financial debt, protecting and stabilizing their income with steady, salaried employment is a priority. In its 2011 Survey of Final-Year Medical Residents, Merritt Hawkins & Associates reported that in 2001, only 3 percent of medical residents preferred hospital employment. This last year, 32 percent made institutional work their first choice.

    The recession is also taking its toll on the private practice. Fewer patients come in for care and more who do could not pay their bills.  An April 2011 MGMA letter to the U.S. House of Representatives reported that the total operating cost per full time equivalent physician has increased by 51 percent since 2001, while Medicare physician payments have remained stagnant during that same period. Insufficient reimbursement from insurance companies and reductions in federal Medicare and Medicaid payments threaten the revenue stream of a private practice and place severe pressure on doctors to reduce operating costs to keep their businesses viable. With a sharp decrease in federal reimbursements forecasted for 2012 and beyond, the situation becomes dire for the independent physician.

    Poor economics encouraged by national policy, changes in attitudes in the new generation of doctors, and mounting administrative burdens are motivating doctors to surrender the autonomy and control of their private practices to seek employment at larger organizations. This transition creates a positive feedback loop as remaining doctors in private practice rush to sell their practices before their peers sell out. Else, they risk prices for their own practices falling, limiting their own exit options.

    On the cost side, national health policies continue to reward efficient, integrated models of care that can better coordinate services and elevate the quality of care while keeping costs down through economies of scale. For example, hospitals can wield market power by acquiring large quantities of supplies at the best prices from vendors, optimize the use of expensive medical equipment among many doctors, compete for the best doctors in the labor markets, distribute the costs of acquisition and maintenance of expensive health information management systems, or prevent repeated medical tests. Institutions can theoretically offer patients the best possible care at the lowest prices.

    On the revenue side, competitive, hospital systems readily hire larger numbers of doctors. According to the MGMA’s 2010 Physician Placement Starting Salary Survey, half of doctors newly out of residency or fellowships were hired by hospitals. Robert Kocher, M.D. and Nikhil Sahni, in their May 2011 article, “Hospitals’ Race to Employ Physicians” in the New England Journal of Medicine, reported that hospitals lose $150,000 to $250,000 per year over the first 3 years of employing a physician. However, after new physicians establish themselves and build a network of referrals, hospitals expect to turn a profit. In its 2010 Physician Inpatient/Outpatient Revenue Survey, Merritt Hawkins & Associates found that the average salary or guarantee offered to a pediatrician is $171,000, while the average annual inpatient/outpatient revenue generated by a pediatrician for their affiliated hospitals is $856,154.  A neurosurgeon will earn $571,000 while bringing in $2,815,650. A large staff of doctors and specialists who use hospital facilities guarantees over the long term a steady flow of patient referrals, especially those with private insurance, which typically pays more than federal programs.
