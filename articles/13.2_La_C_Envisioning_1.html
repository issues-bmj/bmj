Envisioning the 21st Century


Improving vision with phakic intraocular lenses

Connie La

Spring 2006


    What is the most irksome part about wearing contact lenses? Scouring the bathroom ﬂoor on your hands and knees, searching for an indiscernible lens? Waking up from a nap to ﬁnd a contact lens stuck to the inside of your eyelid? How about the monotonous hygienic routine? When we think of improving vision without the hassle of glasses or contact lenses, one procedure often comes to mind: Laser-assisted In-situ Keratomileusis, or LASIK. However, for many people—especially those with extremely high-power prescriptions, dry eyes, or thin corneas—surgically implanted phakic intraocular lenses (IOLs) may be a better alternative.

    Currently, it is estimated that several million people nationwide are ineligible for LASIK; nonetheless, they may be potential candidates for IOL implants. Although both procedures aﬀect the structures of the eye that focus incident light, each does so in its own fashion. LASIK uses a laser to reshape the surface of the cornea, whereas IOLs function as adjuncts to the natural lens, which modiﬁes focusing power.

    The phakic IOLs are analogous to the intraocular lenses that have been used in cataract surgeries for over half a century. Initially, the IOLs that cataract patients received were ﬁxed focal lenses that were designed to replace the natural crystalline lens of the eye and could only compensate for deﬁcits in distance vision; consequently, the patients still had to wear eyeglasses to correct problems in intermediate and close-up vision. Phakic IOLs, however, allow the lens to move with the muscles of the eye and focus on objects at all distances, and, moreover, can be placed inside the eye without removing the natural lens. This technology is now no longer limited to cataract patients alone, as more surgeons are exploring the use of IOLs for people who need correction for their myopia (nearsightedness), hyperopia (farsightedness), or presbyopia (farsightedness with advancing age).

    One type of phakic IOL, patented as the Verisyse™ lens, is marketed in North America by Advanced Medical Optics and elsewhere under the trade-name ARTISAN® by Ophtec Holding BV. The Verisyse™ lens is an option for those seeking correction of common vision errors such as moderate to severe myopia, characterized by prescriptions ranging from -5.0 to -20.0 diopters. This is well beyond the range of LASIK, which is only approved for the treatment of patients with prescriptions of -14.0 diopters or less. In truth, many surgeons do not proceed with LASIK under myopic conditions above -8.00 diopters.

    Composed of a plastic known as polymethylmethacrylate (PMMA), the Verisyse™ phakic IOL is inserted behind the cornea and on top of the iris. According to Gary Kawesch, OD, of the Laser Eye Center of Silicon Valley, “the implantation of a phakic IOL is done as an outpatient procedure in an ambulatory surgery center, under local or topical anesthesia. Most patients experience little to no discomfort during the actual procedure, which takes approximately thirty to sixty minutes.”

    How do ophthalmologists and optometrists quantify visual acuity? Focusing ability is measured against 20/20 vision; for example, an individual with 20/40 needs to be 20 feet away from a given object in order to discern it clearly, whereas an individual with 20/20 vision could accomplish this from a distance of 40 feet. Having 20/20 vision is considered normal, while having 20/40 vision is the cutoﬀ for driving a car without wearing corrective lenses. In a clinical study conducted by Ophtec USA, Inc., a subsidiary of Ophtec Holding BV, about 92 percent of 662 myopic patients who received a Verisyse™ phakic intraocular lens implant achieved 20/40 vision or better, while 44 percent achieved 20/20 vision or better. According to the October 2005 issue of Ophthalmology Times, another clinical study by Ophtec USA, Inc. involving a one-year follow-up of 102 patients that underwent the same procedure revealed that 47 percent of the patients gained 20/20 vision or better, while 93.6 percent gained 20/40 vision or better.
