continued from Do Good, Feel Good


   Moreover, altruism has been proven time and time again to effectively promote good health whilst selfishness confers detrimental consequences. Negative neural signals, resulting from selfish behavior, cause psychological stress, “which is associated with lower telomerase activity, and shorter telomere length, all of which are known determinants of cell senescence and longevity, ” according to Elissa Epel, PhD, University of California, San Francisco. The length of telomeres, repeating segments flanking the ends of DNA, decreases with each DNA replication and thus leads to aging. Simply put, psychological stress contributes to biological aging, which can lead to the premature onset of many diseases. Altruistic behavior, on the other hand, prevents negative stress, “which may contribute to good health and longevity by preventing the acceleration of aging at the cellular level,” says Stephen Post, PhD, Case Western Reserve University. Likewise, Doug Oman, PhD, University of California, Berkeley, reaffirms this point in one of his studies, in which high volunteerism actually decreased the mortality rate of the senior population.

    In his 2005 International Journal of Behavioral Medicine paper, Post states, “The consequences of negative emotional responses are increased susceptibility to disease and worse health outcomes. It has been shown that unforgiving thoughts can prompt more aversive emotion and significantly higher heart rate and blood pressure changes from the baseline.” The opposite effect is supported by findings in a 1999 experiment conducted by Carolyn Schwartz, ScD, Harvard Medical School, and Rabbi Meir Sendor, PhD, Brandeis University, in which voluntary peer supporters trained in client-centered psychotherapy were given the task of contacting and giving support to multiple sclerosis patients. The supporter’s job was to record and listen compassionately, but never to give advice. Over a period of two years, these volunteer supporters described “a sense of inner peace that allowed them to listen to others without judgment or interference...and a greater benefit in quality of life outcomes than those they helped... and consequently developed more confidence and gratification from the role,” Schwartz said.

    If this is still not enough motivation to do good deeds and think of others compassionately, studies on undergraduates show the positive effects of altruism. In 2006, a team of researchers led by Kristin Neff, PhD, University of Texas at Austin conducted an experiment with 177 undergraduate students. “The study found that compassion had a significant positive association with self-reported measures of happiness, optimism, personal initiative, extroversion, and conscientiousness.” A similar 1995 study by Sara Little, PhD, University of Rhode Island, tested 470 undergraduates from four major New England universities, where altruistic volunteers reported “higher levels of empathetic concern, personal responsibility, and practical moral reasoning than did no volunteers.” These are all indicators of a healthy lifestyle in college students, and these good deeds no doubt trigger positive feelings that reinforce Epel and Post’s earlier statements.

    Evidently, it pays to do good. After years of evolution, it is clear that altruistic behavior benefits not only the recipient but also the benefactor. Performing altruistic acts has resulted in elevated levels of happiness, positive feelings of self-worth, increased cellular longevity – all important indicators of good health. So next time, do take the time to help your friend with that assignment, or get out and volunteer more. Put your skills to work for the benefit of others because, ultimately, doing good is good for you.
