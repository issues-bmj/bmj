continued from Evolutionary Medicine


    Neuron production begins by the sixth week of gestation, and the OSVZ arises during the eleventh week. For the next 6 weeks, the OSVZ undergoes significant cellular expansion, and correspondingly there is an increased growth of the neocortex. The oRG cells also function to increase the surface area of the brain by expanding the radial fiber scaffold necessary for neuronal migration from the OSVZ to the neocortex. In general, the stem cells of the OSVZ generate exceptionally more neurons, and construct a greater surface area of the neocortex. These effects ultimately equate to a larger brain.


    As Kriegstein describes, “the human brain is highly folded, or gyrencephalic, and (...) this is because the cortical surface is so big that in order for it to fit into a reasonably sized skull, it involves folding on itself. So,” Kriegstein continues,  “it is folded, like an accordion in a way, in order to fit into a small space. What the folding really means, is that the brain has gotten hugely expanded, and this expansion is due to a lot more neurons.”


    In order to understand differences in neural maturation, size, and shape, research involved in the brain development of various other animals should also include a further exploration for OSVZ stem cells. For example, the human neocortex is quite different from mice, which could offer limitations for scientists modeling human brain diseases using mice specimens. “The overall goal of this research, in part, is to teach us just how good a model the mouse is for diseases that involve the human cortex,” Kriegstein explains. “Now we are going back and looking again at rodents, and discovering that they contain certain features that have not been appreciated before.”


    The findings in Kriegstein’s lab has shown that the complexity of the cerebral cortex is still far from being understood, but through our advances in research, we are continuing to grow closer to deciphering the elaborate puzzle of the human brain. The stem cells identified dictate the biological processes underlying the expansion of the neocortex. Between mammals, there are differences between the properties of these cells, and this directly affects the number of neurons they produce. These cells are further being used, and their properties compared, to examine mammalian diversity in brain size.


    Through this newfound understanding about the brain’s evolution, researchers have an alternative direction to examine modern health illnesses. Dr. Arnold Kriegstein emanates thrill and exhilaration as he explains, “What could be more fascinating than the human brain? It is the source of everything that makes us uniquely human; our intelligence, our reasoning ability, our cognitive functions, [and] our personality. All of that has to do with how the brain works…an extremely complicated machine.”


    Humans have always asked fundamental questions about the brain. It is what gives us our similarities, our differences), our small idiosyncrasies, and our immense beliefs. It is what makes us, well … human. These cells provide us with a peek into the labyrinth of the brain. Further discoveries will reveal answers about the complexities of the brain, catapulting us towards ingenious therapeutic advances in the future.
