continued from Memoirs of an Electron


   Recently, Gary Schwartz, PhD, professor of psychology, psychiatry, neurology, surgery, and medicine at University of Arizona has proposed the systemic memory theory, which argues that memory is not stored at the protein level, but rather at the subatomic level. This theory suggests that memory can be stored in organs via electrons and photons. According to Dr. Schwartz, in each energy interaction, the two particles involved—specifically, photons and electrons—inevitably share information and energy in such a way that any future relationships between the two particles will evolve from that instance. To illustrate the basic principle of feedback, Dr. Schwartz explains that when a video camera is pointed at a TV that is broadcasting the image of the video camera, the TV will show multiple images of itself, one inside the other. Dr. Schwartz explains that just as the video camera has the ability to record and update images of light, so are electrons constantly storing and updating information about the history of their interactions.

    The systemic memory theory thus predicts that atoms, which contain electrons, are constantly “remembering” and “updating” information about their surroundings and that organs contain a more complex memory that is retained by these electrons. From this idea, it might be said that a person’s soul or memories from his life continue to live on even after the body dies because the electrons will always have the “memory” of their interactions.

    But can the lifeless, physical memories of individual electrons combine to form the full-scale, animated memory of a human being? As Dr. Stent puts it, “Personality is a complex shmeligan of workings in the brain; although it is possible that personalities could be passed from person to person, it is highly unlikely, unless a brain is transplanted.”

    Dr. Budinger offers a few other possibilities that indicate that hormonal action could be the reason for this alteration of behavior. Hormone disruption can occur both naturally, as in the case of the body’s interaction with the new organ, and unnaturally, as in the case of the injection of hormones into the body. Naturally, cells of transplanted organs always interact with cells of the recipient’s body by traveling to other parts of it, while cells in the recipient’s body may also go into the transplanted organ. This exchange of cells produces chimeras, or organs consisting of tissues with different genetic composition. Likewise, it is not unusual for a female heart transplanted into a male body to eventually contain cells with both X and Y chromosomes. Therefore, it is understandable that any hormonal imbalances or chemical processes characteristic of the cells of a donor may be passed on to a recipient at the organ level.

    Hormonal imbalances introduced to the recipient’s body are not caused solely by the donated organ. Since donated organs contain foreign cells, the recipient’s immune system will react to it by producing hormones that trigger defense mechanisms. To prevent a detrimental immune reaction to the transplanted organ, doctors often prescribe immunosuppressive drugs after the operation. Immunotherapy may alter a patient’s behavior or personality, because immunosuppressive drugs affect the production and recycling of hormones throughout the body. “Changes in behavior may also be attributed to endogenous biochemical abnormalities resulting from problems with the metabolism of the liver,” says Dr. Budinger. One of the liver’s main functions is to break down hormones; when the liver’s metabolism is abnormal or when there are too many hormones for the liver to break down, the hormones go back into the bloodstream, ultimately leading them back to the brain. In the case of dopamine buildup, disorders such as schizophrenia may occur.

    Despite all we have learned through scientific research, humans still do not know completely where behaviors, passions, and memories are stored or even what they really are. Perhaps memories are simply stored in the brain as extremely stable proteins, or perhaps they are a complex amalgamation of the individual “memories” of electrons and photons. As Dr. Schwartz puts it, “We’ve been looking in the wrong place this whole time.”
