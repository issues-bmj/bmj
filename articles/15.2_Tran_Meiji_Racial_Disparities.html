Racial Disparities in Breast Cancer


Trends in incidence

Meiji Tran

Spring 2008


    Breast cancer is among the most prevalent types of cancer diagnosed in women around the world. With more than 130,000 cases in the United States per year, it occurs in 1 out of 3 cases of cancer in American women. A plethora of risk factors determine the probability of developing breast cancer, including socioeconomic status, timing of detection, and availability of treatment. An investigation of the trends in breast cancer incidence and survival rates in the US reveals discernible ethnic disparities between African American and Caucasian women. What, then, are the various breast cancer risk factors that account for these ethnic disparities?

    In general, socioeconomic factors and lifestyles among women bear strong relationships to breast cancer incidence. Women with low incomes enjoy fewer advantages of health care and tend to live in neighborhoods with poor environmental conditions such as air pollution, unclean water, and toxic waste. Lack of wealth also leads to unhealthy dietary consumption of inexpensive fast foods, typically high in fat and low in nutrients, which contribute to obesity, which poses a risk for breast cancer. A higher number of African American women belong to low income groups in comparison to Caucasian women, and hence are at greater risk of developing the disease.

    Age is also a significant risk factor for developing tumors in general as well as specifically in the breast. In recent years, the incidence rates for the entire population have been substantially higher in women ages 50 and older. However, since women younger than 50 years comprise 73 percent of the female population, approximately 23 percent of breast cancers are diagnosed in this age group. Breast cancer is often detected at an earlier age for African-American women than for Caucasian women, and this accounts for the disparity in incidence. Age-specific incidence rates prove to be slightly higher among African Americans aged 35 and younger compared to Whites aged 35 and younger. This trend eventually reverses as Caucasians have substantially higher incidence at later ages.

    Among the various prognostic factors, the presence of estrogen receptors (ER) in breast tumors predicts positive responses to treatment. According to G. Shyamala, PhD, researcher at the Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory, ER-positive tumors “are considered to be hormone-dependent. These tumors will respond to anti-estrogen therapy such as tamoxifen.” Availability of treatment explains why ER-positive tumors are less detrimental for women with breast cancer. However, when the onset of the breast cancer arrives at an earlier age, a more aggressive disease occurs with tumors that are largely ER-negative. African American women tend to have high-grade ER-negative tumors. However, even after adjustment for age, studies demonstrated that African- American women continue to have higher chances of developing high-grade tumors than Caucasian women. Overall rates of survival have increased within the last two decades, but since the 1970s, the survival rates for African-American women with breast cancer have remained lower than those of Caucasian women. In short, lifestyle, socioeconomic factors, and biological differences may account for the ethnic disparity in breast cancer incidence and survival rates.

    Finally, the information presented in this report underscores the need to inform all women, regardless of ethnicity and socioeconomic status, about the risk factors of breast cancer in order to prevent the development of more aggressive stages of disease. Greater efforts should be exerted toward providing equal access to prevention, detection, and treatment of breast cancer. In order to eliminate the inequality that exists in access to detection and treatment amongst women, clinicians should identify patients at high risk for breast cancer and offer them appropriate referrals, screenings, and treatments.

    Although very little is known about the genetic and non-genetic factors triggering the racial disparities in breast cancer between African-American and Caucasian women, future research for determining the risk factors will result in a better understanding of the cancer itself. As technology advances, a great deal of progress must also be made to ensure that medical professionals and informants completely and equitably apply this knowledge to women of all ages, races, and class statuses. Not only can scientists and clinicians expand the knowledge base concerning breast cancer, but women can also contribute to the prevention of the disease by actively educating themselves and participating in routine mammogram screenings.
