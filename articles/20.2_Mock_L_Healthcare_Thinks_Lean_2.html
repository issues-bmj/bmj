continued from Healthcare Thinks Lean


   Despite the benefits, lean approaches often spark fears of Taylorism, the work design theories promoted by Frederick Winslow Taylor in his ground-breaking work, The Principles of Scientific Management (1919) that has often been criticized for its emphasis of the operational aspects of work over human factors. Thus, lean implementations in healthcare often encounter resistance. It seems patently obvious that human patients in need of medical services are not spark plugs or steering wheel columns, to be mass manufactured off an automotive assembly line. Highly educated medical care professionals who self-actualize in their career through greater job responsibility, planning, and expert problem-solving skills loath working in a mechanized environment. The operational approach of lean thinking can neglect human, cultural factors that are essential to the experience of quality by both patient and healthcare professionals.

    Yet fears of the ignorance of human factors had been largely unfounded. In practice, lean organizations implement operational aspects of lean thinking in parallel with programs that serve the healthcare professional, such as extensive training, internal consultancies, internal promotion, or pay for performance incentives. These programs imbue a culture of quality and motivate healthcare managers and professionals to take leadership in driving continuous improvement.

    Further, the application of lean thinking may itself provide the catalyst for job improvement. Standardizing a task does reduce complexity and promotes repetition to reduce errors, but standardized jobs may no longer challenge highly trained physicians. However, standardization enables less trained workers to take over the job and frees the physician to tackle complex patient cases that challenge the physician’s expertise. Thus, lean does not prescribe nor interfere with treatment, but frees up the physician resource to become more productive.

    Certainly, since the introduction of lean thinking at the Toyota assembly lines, the understanding of “lean” has and continues to evolve. Today, lean thinking encompasses both operational and human behavioral aspects in an integrated approach. Barbara B. Flynn and E. James Flynn, in their article, “An exploratory study of the nature of cumulative capabilities,” (2004) submit that an integration of both operational and cultural initiatives can amplify process improvements beyond what operational or cultural changes can achieve alone. This combination of operations and cultural changes provide a more holistic approach that considers the general systemic effects of change and better gauges results from the time and effort invested.

    Numerous success stories promoted by the lucrative cottage industry of lean management consulting continue to lure hospitals toward lean implementations. Research continues to explore factors that mediate the success or failure of lean implementations. While certainly not a panacea for all the troubles with the American health care system, lean thinking, by improving healthcare processes, promises to deliver healthcare quality while reducing healthcare costs. This improved allocation of healthcare resources is one important piece of the puzzle in helping hospitals address the healthcare crisis.


About the Author

Lauren Mock, class of 2015, is a pre-Public Health major. Her research interests include healthcare management and operations, and healthcare information technologies.
