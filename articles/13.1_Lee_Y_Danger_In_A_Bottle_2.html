continued from Danger in a Bottle


   Bottled water’s plastic container also lowers its water quality. Plastic is composed of various chemicals that, if leaked into the water, can be toxic to the drinker. Such leaking can occur if the bottles are exposed to heat or kept on the shelf for an extended period of time. One of the hazardous chemicals that can be transferred from the inner lining of the plastic to water is Bisphenol-A (BPA), a key component of clear rigid plastic as well as a known hormone disruptor. Professor Frederick von Saal, PhD, at the University of Missouri has recently found that even small doses of BPA could have adverse effects on humans. Other studies have shown that BPA exposure damages reproductive organs in test animals and induces aneuploidy, an error in cell division that causes miscarriages and birth defects such as Down Syndrome. In addition to BPA, a group of chemical compounds known as phthalates are added to plastic bottles to make them less brittle. Phthalates are environmental contaminants that can exhibit hormone-like behaviors and, like BPA, act as hormone disruptors. Recently, phthalates were banned by the European Parliament from being used in children’s toys.

    When examining the dangers of bottled water, one aspect which certainly can- not be overlooked is the detrimental effect that the bottles have on the environment. Bottled water companies are drying out springs and aquifers and destroying habitats. Corporations like Nestlé and Coca-Cola have been accused of questionable environmental practices and of causing water shortages both in the U.S. and other countries, such as India.

    Regrettably, plastic has become the fastest growing sector of the waste stream, making up 25 percent of the volume of materials sent to landfills each year. Furthermore, an increased use of water bottles is one of the key factors contributing to the overall drop in the recycling rate. “It is a big problem because the recycling rate [of water bottles] is extremely low,” says Jenny Gitlitz, director of research at the California Recycling Institute. She adds that only around 11 percent of water bottles are being recycled. It does not help that currently only three states in the U.S. carry a deposit for water bottles which encourages recycling by giving back a few cents for each bottled recycled.

    Gitlitz emphasizes the seriousness of the problem. “People think because they’ve had curbside recycling for ten, fifteen years, that the current program is working. In fact, the recycling rate for plastic (PET) bottles has dropped. The issue should command more attention than it has been getting.” In 2004, approximately six to eight million barrels of crude oil were used to make 41 billion PET bottles, and at least one million tons of greenhouse gases were emitted to replace the PET bottles that were wasted that year alone.

    Though the problem could be alleviated by increasing the container redemption value of the bottles, Gitlitz adds that [check quote] “it is the beverage industries—like Coca-Cola, Pepsi Cola, and Anheuser Busch—rather than container industries who lobby against bottle bills because they do not want to pay for the collection of bottles.”

    Fortunately, there are solutions to these health and environmental problems posed by bottled water. The simplest way to start making a difference is to choose tap water over the bottled variety. If local tap water is unappealing, using filters may be a good alternative. However, people should do their best to limit the effect bottles have on the environment by remembering to toss their empty bottle into the recycling bin or by choosing bottled water brands such as Biota that utilize biodegradable bottles. If consumers keep in mind that bottled water can pose a danger to themselves as well as to the environment, buying water from a bottle may no longer be seen as such a necessity.
