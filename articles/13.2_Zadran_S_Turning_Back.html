Turning Back the Clock


Mitochondrial metabolites and aging

Sohila Zadran

Spring 2006


    Aging is a universal phenomenon characterized by a general decline in the physiological competence of cells, tissues, and organs. Because of its combined popular and academic appeal, much research has been done to understand the aging process with the hope of ameliorating the loss of function with age. Unfortunately, most attempts to develop relevant interventions have failed. Recently, however, a team of investigators under the leadership of Bruce Ames, PhD, professor of biochemistry at the University of California, Berkeley, has concocted a supplement via rigorous scientiﬁc studies that appears not only to help repair the physiological damage that accumulates with age, but also to reverse certain parts of the aging process itself.

    Surprisingly, this supplement consists of two endogenous metabolites, acetyl-L-carnitine (ALCAR) and R-α-lipoic acid (LA). That a supplement containing ALCAR and LA can be used to combat the deleterious eﬀects of aging is not surprising in light of the free radical theory of aging. As its name implies, this theory postulates that the harmful eﬀects of aging are caused by the accumulation of free radical-induced oxidative damage within cells.

    Mitochondria convert chemical potential energy in organic compounds into adenosine triphosphate (ATP) – the “energy currency” of cells – but they also contribute to the accumulation of free radicals and oxidative damage in cells. During ATP production, electrons from various donors travel through an electron transport chain within the inner mitochondrial membrane, ultimately reducing molecular oxygen to water. In this process, approximately two percent of the electrons leak out of the chain and reduce molecular oxygen to the superoxide radical and hydrogen peroxide. The leakage of oxidants from the electron transport chain appears to be unavoidable. These oxidants, in turn, can partake in harmful biochemical reactions throughout the cell.

    Because of their close proximity to these oxidants and their limited antioxidant capacity, the mitochondria are extremely vulnerable to oxidative damage. Obviously, the accumulation of these oxidants is time-dependent, explaining the strong correlation between age and mitochondrial decay. Furthermore, an age-related decrease in cardiolipins—the phospholipids in the mitochondrial membrane that serve as cofactors for a number of critical mitochondrial transport proteins—exacerbates the mitochondrial decay. As the mitochondria become more damaged, cellular function generally becomes less eﬃcient. This global decrease in cellular eﬃciency may underlie the decrease in physiological competence seen with aging.

    So how do ALCAR and LA combat mitochondrial decay? ALCAR is derived from L-carnitine, a common nutrient that is required for the transport of long fatty acid chains into the mitochondria for β-oxidation, ATP production, and the removal of excess short and medium chain fatty acids. ALCAR is used in lieu of L-carnitine in the supplement because it enters cells and crosses the blood-brain barrier more eﬃciently. LA, also found in the supplement, is a coenzyme that is involved in carbohydrate utilization necessary for the production of ATP in the mitochondria. Here, LA is usually reduced to dihydrolipoic acid, a powerful antioxidant.

    According to Ames who has examined the role of these metabolites in mitochondrial decay and aging extensively, “both [metabolites] complement each other well and together oﬀer the optimal defense against mitochondrial decay.” Ames’ recent work examines the exact function and beneﬁts of the use of these mitochondrial metabolites, especially the delaying of mitochondrial decay in the brain.

    In the February 2002 issue of the Proceedings of the National Academy of Science, Ames published several studies that examined the eﬀects of supplementation with metabolites on the age-associated mitochondrial decay in both the brains of aged rats and cultures of human neurons. In these studies, the ALCAR/ LA supplement improved the age-associated decline of ambulatory activity and memory, partially restored mitochondrial structure and function, inhibited the age- associated increase in oxidative damage to lipids, proteins, and nucleic acids, and elevated the levels of antioxidants. These mitochondrial metabolites have also been found to protect neuronal cells from neurotoxin- and oxidant- induced toxicity and to inhibit oxidant-induced acceleration of senescence.

    Interestingly, these supplements have not only been observed to decrease the rate of mitochondrial decay but also to reduce and reverse the degenerative processes of aging, an achievement unlike any other. Ames has extended this study to humans, noting that “the supplements have displayed positive results amongst the studies done with people as well.”

    For decades, science has attempted to understand, treat, and ultimately prevent the degenerative eﬀects caused by aging, but what is our ultimate goal? Does man seek to prevent the debilitating events of aging, improve quality of life, or introduce a means for immortality? Momin Khan, a sixty-year-old taking supplements composed of the two metabolites ALCAR and LA, describes that “as we age, we see so many changes within ourselves. We become weaker, everything takes longer to do, and we forget many things. We begin to imagine the end—buried six feet under the soil, leaving everyone behind—and we tremble. This fear of death is what drives us for longer life. We not only seek a long life, but also a healthy life. If science has come to a place where a single pill can give me a chance for a few more years, a few more happy years, then sign me up for a lifetime membership.” These new supplements seem to provide hope that a weapon against aging may exist. However, only time can tell what role these new supplements will have in man’s larger battle against the ever-ticking clock.
